The Paradigm Shift: Remittance Basis vs. Residence Basis

A comparative structural analysis of the pre and post-6 April 2025 UK tax landscape for internationally mobile individuals.

Executive Summary

Effective 6 April 2025, Finance Act 2025, Chapter 1 fundamentally restructured how the UK taxes non-domiciled individuals. The historical system linked taxation to a person's domicile (permanent home). The modern system severed this link entirely, migrating to a strict residence-based framework known as the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime.

The Structural Shift at a Glance

The core differences between the historical mechanism and the new statutory framework:

Tax Mechanism Historical System (Pre-6 April 2025) New FIG Regime (Post-6 April 2025)
Key Test Domicile Status: Dependent on origin, intent, and long-term home. Tax Residence: Determined strictly via the statutory residence test (SRT).
Taxation Method Remittance Basis: Foreign income/gains were only taxed if physically or constructively brought into the UK (ITA 2007, s809B). Arising Basis: Worldwide income/gains are automatically taxed as they arise, unless an explicit FIG relief claim is made.
Relief Availability Available for up to 15 out of 20 years of UK residence before "deemed dom" rules triggered. Limited strictly to the first 4 consecutive tax years of UK residence.
Remittance Freedom Bringing untaxed offshore funds into the UK triggered heavy, immediate tax charges (HMRC RDRM31190). Funds relieved under the 4-year FIG window can be remitted to the UK 100% tax-free at any time.
Compliance Track Could opt to leave unremitted foreign income entirely off the UK tax return if under the £2,000 threshold (ITA 2007, s809D). "Report then Relieve": All worldwide figures must be declared on SA106 before relief is claimed on SA109 (HMRC Help Sheet HS266).

The Nature of the 4-Year Limit: Permanent Policy vs. Rolling Windows

A frequent technical point of confusion during transitional planning relates to the significance of the 5 April 2029 milestone. It is critical to recognize that the 4-year FIG regime does not expire or sunset after 5 April 2029.

The 4-year limit is a rolling personal eligibility window assigned to individual newcomers, not a lifespan cap on the legislation itself.

Statutory Planning Insight: The date of 5 April 2029 simply represents the hard termination point for the very first cohort of arrivals who claimed the relief when the program launched in the 2025/26 tax year.

The underlying regime remains a permanent, ongoing fixture of UK tax architecture. Individuals relocating to the United Kingdom in 2030, 2035, or any subsequent tax year will remain fully entitled to claim the 4-year tax holiday, provided they satisfy the statutory definition of a Qualifying New Resident (QNR)—specifically, executing a clean 10-tax-year block of consecutive non-UK residency prior to their arrival.

Special transitional rules apply if a resident arrived in the UK up to three years before the framework changed on 6 April 2025. These individuals do not receive a fresh 4-year window. Instead, their 4-year timeline is calculated retroactively from their original arrival year, allowing them to claim FIG relief only for their remaining qualifying years (you will see the details as you progress).

Mechanical Differences for Study

1. Domicile vs. Residence

Previously, if an individual was born abroad to non-UK parents, they could reside in the UK for over a decade while maintaining a non-domiciled status. Under the new regime, domicile is irrelevant for income and capital gains tax. If you satisfy the Statutory Residence Test, your worldwide profile enters the UK tax net instantly.

2. The Cost of Clean Capital Tracking

Under the old remittance basis rules, taxpayers spent thousands on professional fees segregating accounts into "clean capital", "income", and "capital gains" to avoid triggering mixed fund rules upon remittance. The new regime replaces this tracking nightmare during the 4-year holiday with complete operational freedom—income can be generated in New York, deposited in Switzerland, and spent in London with zero tax friction.

3. Transparency Evolution

The compliance model moved from an "exclusionary" method to a "disclosive" method. Previously, hiding money legally meant keeping it offshore and un-remitted. Now, HMRC demands absolute transparency up front via the Self-Assessment platform. For formal guidance, refer to the new HMRC Residence and FIG Regime Manual:

  • Step 1: Report worldwide figures on Form SA106.
  • Step 2: Neutralize the UK tax charge by checking the relief boxes on Form SA109 (specifically Box 28 for income and Box 29 for gains).
