Offshore Attribution, Matching and FIG Relief

How income and gains of non-UK companies and settlements can be charged on UK-resident individuals.

Look Beyond the Immediate Recipient

Scope: This page is a study map of the principal UK Income Tax and CGT attribution rules for 2025/26 onwards and reflects enacted law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

Holding an asset or receiving income through a non-UK company or trust does not by itself determine who is taxed. Separate provisions can attribute income or gains to a settlor, transferor, participator or beneficiary. A valid FIG claim may relieve qualifying foreign amounts, but it does not switch off the underlying attribution or pooling rules.

Settlements Legislation

Sections 624 and 629 ITTOIA 2005 can treat settlement income as the settlor's income where the statutory conditions are met. Section 643A can charge benefits or onward gifts matched with protected foreign-source income or transitional trust income. Foreign amounts arising under specified settlement provisions may qualify for FIG relief for a qualifying new resident, while UK-source amounts and disqualified income remain taxable.

Transfer of Assets Abroad

The Transfer of Assets Abroad rules can charge a UK-resident transferor who has power to enjoy income of a person abroad or is entitled to a connected capital sum. A separate benefits charge can apply to a UK-resident non-transferor whose benefit is matched with relevant income.

Foreign deemed income under sections 720, 727 or 731 ITA 2007 can be qualifying foreign income. Relief claimed by one individual can nevertheless leave the underlying income available for matching in specified later calculations involving another individual. The transaction, people, benefits and income pools therefore need to be followed across tax years.

Attributed and Matched Capital Gains

  • Section 3 TCGA 1992 can attribute gains of a non-UK close company to UK-resident participators.
  • Section 86 can attribute gains of a non-resident settlor-interested settlement to its UK-resident settlor.
  • Sections 87 and 89 and Schedule 4C can match trust gains to capital payments received by beneficiaries.

The FIG rules contain specific claim routes for qualifying attributed gains and identified capital payments. Claiming can change what is disregarded or remains in the relevant settlement pool, so the consequences extend beyond the claimant's current-year tax bill.

Illustration

A qualifying new resident settles a non-UK trust holding an overseas investment company. The structure earns foreign and UK income, and another UK-resident beneficiary later receives a benefit.

  • Test the settlements and Transfer of Assets Abroad income charges separately.
  • Split UK-source and foreign-source amounts.
  • Identify the amount, if any, relieved by the settlor's FIG claim.
  • Maintain the income and gain pools for later benefits to other people.

Applied analysis: Relief for the settlor in one year does not prove that the same underlying income disappears from every future matching calculation.

Practical Checklist

  • Draw the ownership, control and benefit structure.
  • Record settlor, transferor, participator and beneficiary residence.
  • Separate UK and foreign income and asset gains.
  • Maintain annual income, gains, benefits and capital-payment pools.
  • Review onward gifts and every later distribution before payment.
