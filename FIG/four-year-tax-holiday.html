The 4-Year Tax Holiday & Qualifying New Resident (QNR) Criteria

A technical analysis of the qualifying criteria for new arrivals claiming a 100% exemption on Foreign Income and Gains.

Defining the Qualifying New Resident (QNR)

To access the 4-year tax holiday, an individual must meet the strict statutory definition of a Qualifying New Resident (QNR). This is a binary test based purely on tax residency, completely divorced from historical concepts of permanent intent.

The Two-Part Test:

  • Part 1: The individual must become a UK tax resident under the Statutory Residence Test (SRT) for the tax year in question.
  • Part 2: The individual must have been a non-UK tax resident for a continuous period of at least 10 consecutive tax years immediately preceding that arrival year. This rule is codified in Finance Act 2025, Schedule 1.

For official validation procedures, refer to HMRC's interactive checker tool: Check eligibility for the 4-year FIG regime.

The Mechanics of the 4-Year Tax Holiday Window

The relief is structured as a fixed, continuous calendar window. Understanding how time is calculated is critical for CPD purposes:

The 4-Year Clock is Fixed and Continuous

The 4-year period begins on day one of the first tax year in which the individual becomes a UK tax resident and runs for 4 consecutive tax years.

Special transitional rules apply if a resident arrived in the UK up to three years before the framework changed on 6 April 2025. These individuals do not receive a fresh 4-year window. Instead, their 4-year timeline is calculated retroactively from their original arrival year, allowing them to claim FIG relief only for their remaining qualifying years (you will see the details as you progress).

Crucial Trap: The clock does not pause if the individual leaves the UK. If a QNR becomes a UK resident in Year 1, moves abroad for Year 2, and returns for Year 3, Year 2 still consumes one full year of their eligibility window. They cannot defer or bank the relief.

What is Exempt?

A valid claim under HMRC Helpsheet HS266 grants a 100% exemption on:

  • Foreign Income: Offshore dividends, foreign rental profits, foreign interest, and non-UK trade profits.
  • Foreign Capital Gains: Gains realized on the disposal of non-UK situs assets (e.g., selling offshore company shares or overseas property).

The Post-Arrival Remittance Freedom

The defining operational benefit of the 4-year holiday is the absolute freedom of capital movement.

Statutory Rule: Any foreign income or gains that arise during the 4-year holiday period, and are successfully relieved under the FIG regime, can be brought into the UK at any time—either during the holiday or years later—with zero UK tax liability.
