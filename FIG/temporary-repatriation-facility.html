The Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF) & Historic Mixed Funds

A technical guide to navigating the 3-year statutory window for cleaning pre-2025 unremitted offshore pools.

The Purpose of the TRF Window

Following the abolition of the remittance basis on 6 April 2025, individuals who previously used the old regime are left with unremitted historic pools of foreign income and gains (FIG). Ordinarily, bringing these funds into the UK would trigger harsh marginal tax rates of up to 45%.

To incentivize capital repatriation, Finance Act 2025 introduced the Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF). This creates a strict 3-year incentive window allowing taxpayers to make a "designation election" on their Self-Assessment return to pay a low, flat rate of tax on historic funds. Once designated and taxed, the funds are classified as clean capital and can be brought to the UK completely tax-free at any point in the future.

Comprehensive operational rules are located within HMRC Internal Manual RDRM71000.

The Tiered Rate Structure & The 2028 Cliff

The TRF is intentionally structured with a rising rate mechanism designed to force early compliance before the facility vanishes permanently.

Facility Phase Tax Year Window Statutory TRF Flat Rate Impact on Remittance Freedom
Phase 1 6 April 2025 to 5 April 2027 12% Maximum discount window; funds become permanently tax-free.
Phase 2 6 April 2027 to 5 April 2028 15% Reduced incentive rate; cost of designation rises by a quarter.
Phase 3 (The Cliff) 6 April 2028 Onward Standard Marginal Rates (Up to 45%) Facility closed. Legacy funds are taxed under standard remittance rules.

The Double-Taxation Trap: Unlike normal foreign reporting tracks, you cannot claim Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR) against the TRF charge. If offshore tax was already paid on that income, it cannot be used to reduce the 12% or 15% UK flat fee.

Mixed Funds De-pollution Priorities

Historically, when clean capital, foreign income, and foreign capital gains were blended into a single offshore account, they formed a "mixed fund". Under standard tracking provisions, any money taken out of a mixed fund is treated as being drawn from the most heavily taxed layers first (the strict statutory ordering rules).

The TRF breaks this tracking problem completely by introducing an artificial ordering shortcut:

Statutory Super-Priority: Any historic funds inside a mixed account that are successfully designated under the TRF are automatically granted top-tier priority. When a taxpayer withdraws money from that account, the TRF-cleared funds are treated as leaving the account first, ahead of all other un-designated income or capital layers.

This allows tax planners to effectively "drain" the taxable pollution out of a compromised account at a flat 12% rate. For precise administrative instructions on filling out your return, see HMRC Helpsheet HS264 (TRF Disclosures).

Critical Compliance Alert: Paying Tax with Offshore Money

If a taxpayer issues a payment to HMRC to clear their 12% TRF charge using un-designated funds from an offshore account, that transaction itself counts as an ordinary remittance. HMRC will tax that payment at standard rates up to 45%. To avoid this, tax planners must designate a large enough slice of the account to cover both the intended clean capital and the tax payment itself.
