Temporary Non-Residence and Returning to the UK

Why a short period abroad can defer, rather than eliminate, UK tax and does not restart FIG eligibility.

Different from the Ten-Year FIG Lookback

Scope: This page summarises the UK temporary-non-residence rules and their FIG interaction for returns from 6 April 2025, using official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

Temporary non-residence is an anti-avoidance regime for certain people who leave and resume UK residence after a relatively short absence. It is not the same as the ten consecutive non-UK-resident tax years required to become a qualifying new resident.

Broadly, the current rules examine whether the individual had sole UK residence in at least four of the seven tax years before departure, whether a period that was not sole UK residence falls between periods of sole UK residence, and whether that intervening period exceeds five years. The statutory definitions and split-year rules must be applied to the actual dates.

Amounts Taxed in the Year of Return

Where the conditions are met, specified gains realised during the temporary period can be treated as arising in the year UK residence resumes. The rules can also reach particular income, including certain close-company distributions, released participator loans, pension and insurance amounts, offshore income gains and remittances of pre-6 April 2025 foreign income.

Not every disposal during the absence is included. For example, gains on assets both acquired and disposed of during the absence are commonly outside the rule, subject to important exceptions for connected replacement, rollover and attributed gains.

Interaction with FIG and TRF

  • A non-UK-resident year within the original four-year FIG window cannot be used for a FIG claim and does not extend the window.
  • A split year of return can still be a qualifying year and occupies its normal place in the four-year period.
  • A short absence does not establish the ten consecutive non-resident years needed for a fresh FIG window.
  • A preserved remittance treated as arising in a TRF year may be capable of designation, subject to the TRF conditions.

Illustration

Marcus leaves the UK after many years of residence, realises a gain on a pre-departure investment while abroad and returns within five years.

  • Test his residence periods and prior seven-year history.
  • Determine whether the asset and gain fall within section 10A TCGA 1992.
  • Calculate the gain under the return-year rules.
  • Consider foreign tax relief and any preserved-remittance or TRF interaction.

Applied analysis: Non-residence when a gain arises is not the end of the analysis if the person later returns to the UK.

Practical Checklist

  • Prepare a tax-year residence and treaty-residence chronology.
  • Record acquisition, disposal, remittance and return dates.
  • Identify pre-departure assets and deferred gains.
  • Review company, trust, pension, insurance and offshore-fund amounts.
  • Check return-year reporting, foreign tax and TRF deadlines.
