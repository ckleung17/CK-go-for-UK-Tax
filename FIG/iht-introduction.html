Introduction to Inheritance Tax

Inheritance Tax can apply to a person's estate on death, lifetime transfers and chargeable events involving trusts.

IHT is generally paid from the estate by the personal representatives, although a gift recipient or trustees may be liable in particular circumstances. A beneficiary does not normally pay IHT merely because they inherit property.

Parties Involved

Transferor or donor

The person making a lifetime gift or another transfer of value. Residence status, earlier transfers, retained benefits and survival period can affect the result.

Recipient, transferee or donee

The person receiving a lifetime gift or transferred property. The recipient can become liable on certain gifts if the donor dies within seven years.

Deceased or testator

The deceased is the person whose estate is administered. A person who made a valid will is called a testator.

Personal representative

The executor appointed under a will or the administrator dealing with an estate where no executor can act. Personal representatives value and report the estate and normally arrange payment of IHT.

Estate beneficiary

A person or organisation inheriting under a will or the intestacy rules, normally after estate liabilities and IHT have been dealt with.

Settlor and trustees

The settlor provides property to a trust and the trustees administer it. They may have obligations for entry, anniversary, exit or other trust-related IHT charges.

One person can have different descriptions at different stages—for example, donor of a gift, settlor of a trust and later the deceased whose estate is administered.

Key Terminology

Estate

The property, money and possessions of a person who has died. The net estate generally reflects allowable liabilities.

Transfer of value

A disposition or action reducing the value of the transferor's estate, including a gift, transfer into trust or sale at an undervalue.

Potentially exempt transfer (PET)

Broadly, a qualifying lifetime transfer by an individual, commonly an outright gift to another individual. It becomes exempt if the donor survives seven years; otherwise it becomes chargeable.

Chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT)

A lifetime transfer that is neither exempt nor potentially exempt. A transfer into a relevant-property trust is a common example and can produce an immediate IHT charge.

Nil-rate band (NRB)

The part of cumulative chargeable transfers taxed at 0%. Earlier chargeable transfers can use some or all of the band available on death.

Residence nil-rate band (RNRB)

A possible additional band where a qualifying residential interest is closely inherited, subject to separate conditions, limits and tapering rules.

Exemption

A statutory rule removing a qualifying transfer from charge, such as certain transfers to a spouse, civil partner or charity and specified lifetime gift exemptions.

Relief

A statutory reduction in the taxable value of qualifying property or a transfer. Business Relief and Agricultural Property Relief are examples.

Gift with reservation of benefit

Property given away while the donor continues to use or benefit from it. The property may still be treated as part of the donor's death estate.

Market value

Broadly, the price property might reasonably be expected to obtain on a hypothetical open-market sale at the relevant date.

Situs

The legal location of property for tax purposes, used to distinguish UK property from foreign property.

Long-term UK resident

An individual satisfying the statutory IHT residence-history test. From 6 April 2025, this status generally determines whether foreign property falls within IHT.

Post-departure tail

The period after leaving the UK during which an individual can remain long-term UK resident for IHT, generally three to ten tax years depending on residence history.

Ten-year anniversary and exit charges

Charges that may arise under the relevant-property trust regime at an anniversary or when relevant property leaves the trust. They are not automatically charged at the 40% death rate.
