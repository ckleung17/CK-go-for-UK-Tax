Under general law, an individual can only have one country of domicile at any single point in time:

1. Domicile of Origin

Acquired automatically at birth, this status is almost always derived from the domicile of the father if the parents are married, or the mother if they are unmarried. It operates as a sticky baseline; it is highly resilient, exceptionally difficult to displace, and automatically revives under common law if an individual abandons a subsequent domicile without establishing a new one.

2. Domicile of Dependence

Applies to individuals who lack independent legal capacity—primarily minors under the age of 16. If the parent upon whom the minor is legally dependent changes their own domicile through choice, the minor's domicile shifts automatically in tandem.

3. Domicile of Choice

Acquired after reaching age 16 when an individual physically relocates to a new territory with the clear, unconditioned intention of making it their permanent or indefinite home. Establishing a domicile of choice requires robust evidential proof of severing primary ties with the country of origin.

4. Deemed Domicile

A pure statutory creation for UK tax purposes. Under the historical tax rules, even if an individual retained a non-UK domicile under common law, they were "deemed" UK domiciled for all taxes once they hit the long-term residency threshold of being a UK tax resident for at least 15 out of the preceding 20 tax years.

Tax Treatment Under the Legacy Domicile Rules

Under the historical framework (pre-6 April 2025), domicile status served as the primary gatekeeper determining whether an individual was taxed on their worldwide assets or granted preferential relief:

UK Domiciled Individuals

Taxpayers with a UK domicile (or deemed domicile status) were automatically taxed on the arising basis. Their worldwide income and capital gains were subject to UK tax the moment they were generated, regardless of where the assets were located or whether the funds were ever brought into the UK.

Non-UK Domiciled Individuals (Non-Doms)

Taxpayers who were resident in the UK but held a foreign domicile could elect out of the arising basis for their offshore assets by utilizing the remittance basis. This treated foreign income and gains under specific parameters:

  • Tax Shield: Foreign income and gains were entirely exempt from UK tax, provided they remained outside the UK and were never physically or constructively remittedTreated as remitted even though the funds did not physically enter the UK—for example, where untaxed foreign income held offshore was used to repay a debt arising from UK spending. to the country.
  • The £2,000 Threshold: If an individual's unremitted foreign income and gains were under £2,000 in a tax year, the remittance basis applied automatically without requiring a formal Self-Assessment claim.
  • The Forfeiture Trade-off: Making a formal remittance basis claim meant an automatic, mandatory forfeiture of the UK Personal Allowance and the Capital Gains Tax Annual Exempt Amount for that year.
  • The Remittance Basis Charge (RBC): Long-term residents had to pay a substantial annual statutory fee to retain the remittance basis: £30,000 if resident for 7 out of the previous 9 tax years, rising to £60,000 if resident for 12 out of the previous 14 tax years.

Non-UK Domiciled Individuals (non-doms) always had the right to bypass the remittance basis and choose to be taxed on the arising basis. Choosing the arising basis was highly common and often preferred if:

  • Their unremitted foreign income and gains were small, making the administrative tracking of mixed funds unnecessary.
  • They wanted to keep their UK Personal Allowance and Capital Gains Tax Annual Exempt Amount, which were lost when making a formal remittance basis claim.
  • They had been in the UK long enough to trigger the heavy Remittance Basis Charge (£30,000 or £60,000), making it cheaper to simply pay UK tax on their worldwide income as it arose.

Post-6 April 2025 Context:

Under the modern architecture, the remittance basis has been entirely abolished. The arising basis is now the mandatory default for all UK tax residents. Domicile is no longer a factor for income and capital gains tax; instead, individuals must explicitly claim the new 4-year Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) relief if they qualify and wish to exempt their foreign assets.
