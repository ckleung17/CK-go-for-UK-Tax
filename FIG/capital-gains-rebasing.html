Capital Gains Tax Rebasing for Personally Held Foreign Assets

A guide to the transitional rebasing rule that may apply when a qualifying individual disposes of a foreign asset on or after 6 April 2025.

Reading guide: If your interest is limited to the four-year FIG regime for qualifying new residents, and you did not make a remittance-basis claim before 6 April 2025, this rebasing topic will generally not apply to you. You may skip this topic and continue to Trusts and Inheritance Tax. Continue reading if you are a current or former remittance-basis user who held foreign assets on 5 April 2017, or if the older 2017 deemed-domicile rebasing provisions may apply to you.

Purpose and Effect of the Rebasing Rule

From 6 April 2025, the remittance basis was abolished and UK-resident individuals became taxable on their worldwide income and gains as they arise, unless a specific relief applies. Schedule 11 to the Finance Act 2025 provides a transitional Capital Gains Tax rebasing rule for certain current and former remittance-basis users. Its purpose is to provide a valuation starting point for qualifying foreign assets that were already held on 5 April 2017 when those assets are disposed of on or after 6 April 2025.

This rebasing rule forms part of the wider 2025 FIG reforms, but it is not four-year FIG relief and is not restricted to qualifying new residents. Four-year FIG relief concerns eligible foreign income and gains arising to a qualifying new resident during the four-year eligibility period. Rebasing instead changes the acquisition value used in the Capital Gains Tax computation for a qualifying asset.

  • New Schedule 11 route: This may apply to a current or former remittance-basis user who met the historical domicile, remittance-basis claim and asset conditions explained below.
  • Older 2017 route: A narrower rebasing rule was introduced for certain individuals who became deemed UK domiciled under the 15-out-of-20 rule from 6 April 2017. Finance Act 2025 preserves and modifies that separate route.
  • Long-standing UK-domiciled residents: An individual who was actually UK domiciled before 2025/26 cannot use the new Schedule 11 route and would not normally qualify for the older non-dom rebasing route. The normal allowable acquisition cost therefore applies.

Where the new rule applies, the asset is treated as having been acquired on 5 April 2017 for its market value on that date. This will often remove pre-6 April 2017 growth from the later gain, but it can also alter or create a loss, or produce a less favourable result where the original cost was higher. Rebasing is automatic if all the conditions are met, although the individual may make an irrevocable election to opt out for a particular disposal.

What the 5 April 2017 Rebasing Rule Does

Section 42 of the Finance Act 2025 and Schedule 11 provide a transitional rebasing rule for certain personally held foreign assets. The principal asset-level conditions are:

  • the individual held the asset on 5 April 2017;
  • the asset was not situated in the UK at any time from 6 March 2024 to 5 April 2025, unless a statutory exception applies; and
  • the disposal is made on or after 6 April 2025.

If all the conditions are met, the individual is treated as having disposed of and immediately reacquired the asset on 5 April 2017 at its market value on that date. The resulting market value replaces the earlier acquisition cost when calculating the gain or loss.

Rebasing applies automatically where all the conditions are satisfied. The individual may make an irrevocable election for it not to apply to a particular disposal. This may be beneficial where the asset's original allowable cost exceeds its 5 April 2017 market value. Contrary to a common misconception, Schedule 11 can produce either a gain or a loss using the rebased value.

Main Statutory Conditions

The conditions must be tested for the individual and the particular asset disposal.

1. Asset Held on 5 April 2017 and Still Held on 6 April 2025

The individual must have held the asset on 5 April 2017 and must still have held it when the new rules began on 6 April 2025. The qualifying disposal must be made on or after 6 April 2025. Schedule 11 contains special provisions for certain reorganisations and other transactions where the original holding is treated as continuing in a new holding.

An asset owned by a company or trust is not personally held by the individual. However, shares personally held in a foreign company are themselves assets and may qualify if the other conditions are met. Nominee arrangements and interests held through fiscally transparent partnerships require analysis of the individual's beneficial ownership.

Foreign assets acquired after 5 April 2017

An asset acquired after 5 April 2017 normally cannot qualify for Schedule 11 rebasing because the individual did not hold it on the required date. Any gain or loss is therefore generally calculated using its actual allowable acquisition cost, subject to the ordinary Capital Gains Tax rules.

A different result may apply where an asset held on 5 April 2017 was later replaced through a qualifying share reorganisation or another statutory no-disposal transaction. Schedule 11 contains continuity provisions that may treat the later holding as derived from the original holding.

This does not prevent a qualifying new resident from claiming four-year FIG relief for an eligible foreign gain. FIG relief and Schedule 11 rebasing are separate rules.

2. Domicile and Deemed-Domicile History

To qualify under the new rule, the individual's domicile history before it took effect on 6 April 2025 must satisfy a specific condition. The individual must not have been UK domiciled, or treated as UK domiciled under the former deemed-domicile rules, at any time in a tax year before 2025/26. It is not enough that the individual was non-UK domiciled immediately before 6 April 2025; an earlier period of UK domicile or deemed domicile can prevent the new Schedule 11 rebasing rule from applying.

For Example, An individual with a foreign domicile of origin who had been UK resident for 12 tax years and had not acquired a UK domicile of choice may satisfy this condition. By contrast, an individual who became deemed UK domiciled after meeting the former 15-out-of-20 test cannot use this new Schedule 11 route, even if that deemed-domicile status was later lost.

Financial effect of the new and older routes

The new and older rebasing routes do not normally produce different gain calculations. Both substitute the asset's market value on 5 April 2017 for its original allowable cost. Their main difference is who qualifies and when, rather than the amount of the rebased gain.

In this simplified example, either rebasing route removes £200,000 of pre-6 April 2017 growth from the later gain. At an illustrative 24% Capital Gains Tax rate, this could reduce the tax by £48,000 before allowable costs, losses, exemptions and reliefs. The new route is therefore not financially more generous for the same asset; it extends rebasing to a different group of former remittance-basis users. If the 5 April 2017 value produces a less favourable result, the individual may consider the irrevocable opt-out election available under the applicable route.

Separate older rebasing rule: Finance (No. 2) Act 2017 introduced a narrower rebasing route principally for individuals who became deemed UK domiciled under the 15-out-of-20 rule from 6 April 2017 and met its continuing conditions. Finance Act 2025 preserves and modifies that older route. An individual excluded from the new Schedule 11 route because of historical deemed domicile should therefore check the older provisions separately; the two routes have different personal and asset conditions even though both use a 5 April 2017 market value.

3. Historical Remittance-Basis Claim

The individual must have made a valid claim under section 809B of the Income Tax Act 2007 for at least one tax year from 2017/18 to 2024/25. A year in which the remittance basis applied automatically under section 809D or 809E, without a section 809B claim, does not satisfy this condition.

Example: Automatic remittance-basis treatment because unremitted foreign income and gains were below £2,000 is not, by itself, a qualifying claim.

4. Foreign-Situs Window

The asset must generally be situated outside the UK throughout the period from 6 March 2024 to 5 April 2025. Schedule 11 contains limited exceptions for certain temporary UK presence, including public display, repairs, sale, specified personal use, a low-value remittance and qualifying temporary importation. Each exception has detailed conditions and should be checked against the legislation.

This is a continuous foreign-location test, not another valuation period. The starting date, 6 March 2024, was the date on which the reform was announced at the Spring Budget, and the period ends immediately before the new rules began on 6 April 2025. The legislation tests the asset's location throughout the whole period rather than only on a single cut-off date. The effect is that an asset situated in the UK during the transition cannot normally qualify merely because it was moved abroad shortly before 6 April 2025.

Example: painting kept outside the UK

A painting held by the individual on 5 April 2017 and kept in France throughout the foreign-situs window may satisfy this condition. If it was brought into the UK during the window, rebasing would normally be unavailable unless one of the statutory exceptions applied. In either case, the relevant rebasing value remains its market value on 5 April 2017.

Automatic Rebasing, Opting Out and Records

Eligibility and the financial result should be reviewed asset by asset and disposal by disposal. If rebasing is disadvantageous, paragraph 3 of Schedule 11 permits an irrevocable election for paragraph 1 not to apply to that disposal. The election must follow the normal claims and elections procedure and time limits in sections 42 and 43 of the Taxes Management Act 1970.

The individual should retain evidence of ownership, the asset's location during the statutory window, the relevant remittance-basis claim and a supportable 5 April 2017 market valuation. The final computation must also account for ordinary Capital Gains Tax rules, allowable incidental costs, later enhancement expenditure, available reliefs and the sterling value of foreign-currency amounts at the relevant dates.

Rare Case: A Qualifying New Resident Who Also Qualifies for Rebasing

Qualifying-new-resident status does not itself provide Schedule 11 rebasing. The two regimes can overlap only in a narrow transitional case where the individual's four-year FIG period began before 6 April 2025 and the individual made a formal remittance-basis claim before that regime ended.

In practice, the individual would normally need to have become UK resident in 2022/23, 2023/24 or 2024/25 after at least 10 consecutive tax years of non-UK residence. They must also satisfy every Schedule 11 condition, including:

  • having made a formal section 809B remittance-basis claim for at least one tax year from their arrival to 2024/25;
  • having held the foreign asset on 5 April 2017;
  • meeting the historical domicile and foreign-situs conditions; and
  • disposing of the asset on or after 6 April 2025.

Example: Alex was non-UK resident for 10 consecutive tax years up to 2021/22 and became UK resident in 2022/23. He formally claimed the remittance basis for 2022/23 to 2024/25, had held qualifying foreign shares since 2015 and satisfied the other Schedule 11 conditions. If he sells the shares in 2025/26, he is in the fourth year of his qualifying-new-resident period and may also qualify for Schedule 11 rebasing.

By contrast, an individual who first becomes UK resident in 2025/26 will normally be unable to qualify for Schedule 11. Following 10 consecutive non-UK-resident tax years, that person could not have made the required UK remittance-basis claim for a tax year from 2017/18 to 2024/25.

A FIG claim does not preserve a rebased foreign loss by matching it against the relieved gain. If a foreign income claim, foreign gain claim or foreign employment election has effect for the disposal year, each qualifying foreign loss arising in that year is non-allowable in full, regardless of the amount of gains relieved, and cannot be carried forward.

Example: If rebasing produces a £1 million qualifying foreign loss and the individual claims FIG relief on a £100,000 foreign gain, the gain is relieved but the entire £1 million loss is denied. If the individual makes none of the relevant FIG claims or elections, the gain remains taxable, the loss may be allowable under the ordinary Capital Gains Tax rules, and—after offsetting £100,000 against the gain—the remaining £900,000 may generally be carried forward. The value of preserving the loss should therefore be compared with the tax saved by making the FIG claim.
