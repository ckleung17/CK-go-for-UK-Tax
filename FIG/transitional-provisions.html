Transitional Rules for Pre-2025 Arrivals & The 50% Income Policy Shift

An analysis of the structural bridging rules for existing remittance basis users transitioning into the arising basis framework.

For Business Investment Relief and other preserved rules applying to historical funds, see Legacy Remittance-Basis Reliefs after 6 April 2025.

Tail-End FIG Eligibility for Existing Residents

The introduction of the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime on 6 April 2025 did not exclude every individual who first became UK resident before that date. An individual whose first qualifying year was 2022/23, 2023/24 or 2024/25 may claim relief in any remaining tax year within the fixed four-year FIG period, provided they are UK resident, satisfy the qualifying new resident (QNR) conditions and make a valid claim for that year.

Potential Claim Windows for Pre-2025 Residents

  • First qualifying year 2022/23: May claim for 2025/26 only.
  • First qualifying year 2023/24: May claim for 2025/26 and 2026/27.
  • First qualifying year 2024/25: May claim for 2025/26, 2026/27 and 2027/28.

CPD Entry Prerequisite: The individual must have been non-UK resident for at least 10 consecutive tax years immediately before their first qualifying year and must satisfy the other QNR conditions. Relief is not automatic: a separate claim is required for each tax year and for the foreign income, foreign gains, or both, that the individual wishes to relieve.

The QNR conditions are set out in ITTOIA 2005, section 845B, as inserted by Finance Act 2025, Schedule 1. For practical guidance, see HMRC Manual RFIG44000 and HMRC Helpsheet HS266.

Critical Split-Year Rule: If an individual was UK resident under the Statutory Residence Test (SRT) in their first qualifying year and split-year treatment applied, that tax year still counts as the first full year of the fixed four-year FIG period. During the overseas part, the individual is generally treated as non-UK resident for UK tax purposes, so much foreign income and many foreign gains may already be outside the scope of UK tax, although exceptions can apply. The four-year period is not prorated or extended because the UK part began part-way through the year.

Study Guidance: For the wider four-year rules, see The Four-Year FIG Regime. For pre-6 April 2025 unremitted funds, designations and mixed-fund ordering, see the Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF).

Pre-6 April 2025 Unremitted Foreign Income and Gains

The abolition of the remittance basis does not extinguish the potential UK tax liability on foreign income and gains that arose before 6 April 2025 in a tax year when the individual used the remittance basis. Merely retaining those amounts offshore after that date does not trigger a new £30,000 or £60,000 Remittance Basis ChargeBefore 6 April 2025, the £30,000 charge could apply where the individual had been UK resident in at least 7 of the previous 9 tax years, and the £60,000 charge could apply after at least 12 of the previous 14 tax years. These were annual charges for claiming the remittance basis; they were not based on how long particular income or gains remained offshore. Paying the charge did not generally convert all unremitted funds into clean capital or extinguish the potential tax charge on a later remittance. No new Remittance Basis Charge arises from 6 April 2025., or an immediate Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax charge. However, they cannot be relieved under the four-year FIG regime and, if subsequently remitted to the UK, they are generally taxable at the usual Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax rates under the preserved remittance-basis rules.

An eligible former remittance basis user may instead designate qualifying amounts under the Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF). The TRF charge is 12% for amounts designated in 2025/26 or 2026/27 and 15% for amounts designated in 2027/28. Once the TRF charge has been paid, the designated amount may generally be remitted without a further UK tax charge. Amounts that are not designated remain subject to the preserved remittance rules when later remitted, so mixed-fund ordering and reliable records may remain important.

Clean capital: Genuine clean capital is not taxable merely because it is brought to the UK, but the individual should retain sufficient evidence to establish its source and distinguish it from foreign income and gains.

See HMRC Manual RDRM30005, RDRM71000 and RDRM76100.

The 50% Income Reduction Policy Reversal

One proposed transitional measure concerned former remittance basis users who would not qualify for the four-year FIG regime from 6 April 2025.

The Original Policy Proposal

In March 2024, the previous government proposed that individuals moving from the remittance basis to the arising basis on 6 April 2025, and who were not eligible for the four-year FIG regime, would be taxed on only 50% of their foreign income arising in 2025/26. The proposal applied to foreign income only and not to foreign chargeable gains. It was described in the April 2024 HM Treasury technical note.

The Final Position: The Proposal Was Withdrawn

At the Autumn Budget 2024, the government announced that the proposed 50% foreign-income reduction would not be introduced. The proposal was therefore withdrawn before enactment and does not form part of Finance Act 2025.

LEGISLATION ALERT: No 50% transitional reduction applies. From 6 April 2025, UK residents outside the four-year FIG regime are generally taxed on newly arising foreign income and gains under the normal arising-basis rules, subject to the usual exemptions, deductions, reliefs and any applicable Double Taxation Agreement.

The policy reversal is recorded in the Autumn Budget 2024 policy-costing document. The enacted FIG regime is contained in Finance Act 2025, Schedule 1.
