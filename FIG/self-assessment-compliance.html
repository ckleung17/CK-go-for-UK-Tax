Self Assessment Mechanisms: FIG Reporting and SA109 Claims

A technical guide to reporting qualifying foreign income and gains and claiming relief under the FIG regime.

Reporting Income and Gains Before Claiming Relief

FIG relief is not automatic. An eligible individual must make a claim in their Self Assessment tax return and identify the qualifying foreign income or gains for which relief is claimed. Each amount must be reported and quantified on a source-by-source basis.

The claimant reports the relevant income or gains on the appropriate supplementary pages and makes the FIG claim through SA109. This does not require a complete disclosure of every offshore asset, account balance or clean-capital holding. The claim applies only to the qualifying amounts identified and quantified in the return.

Full systemic filing parameters can be verified directly via HMRC Self Assessment Helpsheet HS266.

Step-by-Step Schedule Disclosures

A compliant FIG claim requires coordinating the relevant supplementary pages of the Self Assessment tax return:

Step 1: Report the Income and Gains

Report each source on the supplementary page appropriate to the nature of the income or gain:

  • SA103F: Foreign self-employment income.
  • SA104F: Foreign partnership income.
  • SA106: Foreign interest, dividends and overseas property income.
  • SA107: Foreign trust income, where applicable.
  • SA108 (Capital Gains Summary): Foreign disposals and gains.
  • Source-by-source identification: Each amount for which FIG relief is claimed must be separately identifiable.

Step 2: Submit the FIG Claim (SA109)

Make the claim through the SA109 Residence and foreign income and gains (FIG) regime etc. supplementary pages. Complete the relevant boxes:

  • Box 28: Place an 'X' in this box to make a formal claim for relief on foreign income.
  • Box 29: Place an 'X' in this box to make a formal claim for relief on foreign capital gains.
  • Box 30: Place an 'X' in this box if the claim includes UK income or gains treated as foreign under the qualifying asset holding company (QAHCA UK-resident company that meets the statutory eligibility, activity, investment-strategy and ownership conditions and elects into the special QAHC tax regime, which is designed for certain investment-fund structures.) rules; report the details on SA106 or SA108 as appropriate.

Why QAHC Amounts Can Affect an Individual's Tax Return

Box 30 does not report the QAHC's own profits: the company has a separate Corporation Tax position. It applies to specified amounts arising to an individual investment manager, such as interest or a distribution from a QAHC, or a gain on disposing of shares in it. Interest and distributions may fall within the individual's Income Tax calculation, while a share disposal may fall within Capital Gains Tax.

Where the statutory conditions are met, the QAHC look-through rules can treat a proportion of such an amount as foreign income or foreign gains. Broadly, this proportion reflects the QAHC's profits derived from foreign assets. The treatment is restricted to qualifying individuals, including an investment manager who acquired the relevant QAHC interest while providing investment-management services; it does not apply automatically to every investor.

Example: An eligible investment manager receives a £10,000 distribution from a QAHC. If 80% is attributable to the QAHC's foreign-source profits under the statutory calculation, £8,000 may be treated as foreign income for FIG purposes and the remaining £2,000 remains UK income. If the individual is eligible and claims FIG relief on the £8,000, they complete Box 28 and also mark Box 30.

Review the structural specifications for these boxes via HMRC SA109 Supplementary Pages & Notes.

Step 3: Quantify the Claim

Quantify the qualifying foreign income or each qualifying foreign gain for which relief is claimed. Foreign-income relief is deducted from qualifying foreign income when calculating net income, while foreign-gain relief is given for the qualifying gains identified in the claim. A claim may cover foreign income, foreign gains or both, and the claimant may choose which eligible sources or gains to include.

Overall tax position: Relief removes the claimed qualifying amount from the relevant Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax calculation. It does not necessarily reduce the individual's total UK tax liability to zero. Making a FIG claim also results in the loss of specified allowances, including the Personal Allowance and, where a foreign-gain claim is made, the Capital Gains Tax Annual Exempt Amount.

Claim deadline: The normal time limit is the anniversary of 31 January following the end of the relevant tax year—12 months after the ordinary online filing deadline. For example, the filing deadline for the 2025/26 return is 31 January 2027, but the normal deadline for making, amending or withdrawing a FIG claim for that year is 31 January 2028. Special rules may apply where HMRC issues a late notice to file, and late claims are subject to HMRC's late-claims policy.
