Reformed Overseas Workday Relief (OWR) under the FIG Framework

A practical guide to eligibility, qualifying foreign employment income, financial limits and the election and claim procedure.

For treaty residence, foreign tax credits and section 690D overlap, continue to FIG Claims, Double Taxation Agreements and Foreign Tax.

Who Can Claim OWR?

UK-residence requirement: OWR is a relief for an employee who is UK tax resident for the relevant tax year and, subject to the transitional rules, is a qualifying new resident. An employee who is non-UK resident for the whole tax year does not claim OWR; their overseas duties are considered under the ordinary rules for non-residents, although earnings relating to UK duties may remain taxable in the UK.

From the Remittance Basis to a Claim for Relief

Before 6 April 2025, Overseas Workday Relief was available to certain newly UK-resident, non-UK domiciled employees using the remittance basis. Broadly, employment income relating to duties performed outside the UK was taxed when remitted rather than when it arose.

From 6 April 2025, Overseas Workday Relief (OWR) continues as the commonly used name, while the legislation provides the relief as Foreign Employment Relief under sections 41M to 41T ITEPA 2003. Both names refer to the same relief. Qualifying foreign employment income is initially included in the Income Tax calculation, after which the quantified OWR claim is deducted at step 2 of the calculation under section 23 Income Tax Act 2007.

The legislation was introduced by Finance Act 2025, section 38 and Schedule 8. Current operational guidance is in HMRC's Employment Income Manual at EIM43550 onwards.

Key Operating Rules

The following rules determine whether relief is available and how much can be claimed:

1. Payment and Remittance of Earnings

Under the former rules, qualifying foreign earnings were generally taxed if and when they were remitted to the UK. Using a separate offshore account could assist with identifying the earnings and avoiding mixed-fund complications, but remitting part of the earnings did not necessarily make the entire amount taxable.

From 6 April 2025: The place where the earnings are paid, held or spent does not determine whether OWR (Foreign Employment Relief) is available. A validly relieved amount can be paid into or brought to a UK account without an additional Income Tax charge merely because of that payment or remittance.

Relief is not automatic: points 2 to 4 below explain the eligibility, qualifying-income and financial-limit conditions, while point 6 explains the election and claim procedure.

PAYE: An OWR claim does not itself authorise reduced withholding. After HMRC acknowledges a valid section 690D ITEPA 2003 notification, the employer may operate PAYE on the estimated taxable proportion; the final liability is reconciled through Self Assessment.

Overseas employer: Who operates PAYE depends on whether the employer has a UK tax presence or the employee works for a UK host business. If neither applies, the employee may need a PAYE Direct Payment arrangement or Self Assessment. See the PAYE and NIC responsibilities.

National Insurance contributions: OWR is an Income Tax relief and does not determine NIC liability, which must be considered separately under the applicable international social-security rules.

2. Eligibility and the 4-Year Window

Under the ordinary post-6 April 2025 rules, an employee must:

  • be UK resident for the relevant tax year;
  • have been non-UK resident for at least 10 consecutive tax years immediately before becoming UK resident, making them a qualifying new resident;
  • be within the resulting four-year FIG eligibility period and have an employment whose duties are performed wholly or partly outside the UK during the qualifying year.

The four-year period begins with the first tax year of UK residence after the 10-year absence. A split year is still treated as a UK-resident tax year for this purpose and uses the first year of the four-year period.

3. Qualifying Foreign Employment Income

OWR can apply to qualifying employment income arising from duties performed wholly or partly outside the UK during a tax year in which the employee is a qualifying new resident (the qualifying year). The qualifying categories are:

  • General earnings and amounts treated as earnings, including salary, bonuses and taxable benefits;
  • Employment-related securities income under Part 7 ITEPA 2003, such as taxable income from employee shares, share options or securities awards; and
  • Employment income provided through third parties under Part 7A ITEPA 2003, including taxable amounts arising under the disguised-remuneration rules.

Each category qualifies only to the extent that the income relates to duties performed outside the UK during the qualifying year, determined on a just and reasonable basis. Applicable employment deductions are allocated before the amount of relief and the financial limit are calculated.

Physical work location: Duties are performed overseas only when the employee is physically working outside the UK. Remote work from a UK home for an overseas employer is a UK duty; the employer's location, the payment account and the location of computer servers do not make it an overseas duty.

Example: Alex works online for a US company. He works 200 days from his London home and 20 days at the company's New York office. The 200 home-working days are UK duties. Only the income attributable to the 20 New York workdays may qualify for OWR, subject to the other conditions and the financial limit.

4. The Financial Limit

For each qualifying year, section 41R ITEPA 2003 ordinarily limits the cumulative relief available to the lower of:

  • £300,000; and
  • 30% of the relevant qualifying employment income for that qualifying year after the applicable deductions.

Income relating to one qualifying year may be charged to tax across more than one tax year, for example where a bonus is paid later. The financial limit then applies cumulatively to all OWR claims relating to that qualifying year, with relief already claimed taken into account.

Example: deferred bonus and separate annual limits

2025/26: Alex earns £500,000, of which 40% (£200,000) relates to overseas duties. The limit is the lower of £300,000 and 30% of £500,000 (£150,000), so Alex claims £150,000 OWR.

Bonus paid in 2026/27: Alex receives a £100,000 bonus relating to his 2025/26 duties, of which £40,000 relates to overseas duties. The cumulative employment income for the 2025/26 qualifying year is now £600,000, giving a revised limit of £180,000. After deducting the £150,000 already claimed, only £30,000 further OWR is available for the bonus.

Income relating to 2026/27: Alex also earns £600,000 for 2026/27, of which £240,000 relates to overseas duties. This has its own limit of £180,000, provided Alex makes a separate OWR election for 2026/27. The £30,000 claim relating to the earlier bonus does not reduce this separate limit.

Alex can therefore claim £210,000 in his 2026/27 return: £30,000 relating to 2025/26 and £180,000 relating to 2026/27. Each amount is tested against the limit for the qualifying year in which the relevant duties were performed.

5. Transitional OWR Rules for 2022/23 to 2024/25 Arrivals

These arrival years are addressed because the former OWR normally covered an employee's first three UK-resident tax years, while the new regime began on 6 April 2025 and can provide up to four years of relief. A 2023/24 or 2024/25 arrival still had part of the former three-year period remaining when the rules changed. A 2022/23 arrival had generally completed all three former OWR years by 5 April 2025, so the transition prevents the new regime from automatically adding a fourth OWR year. Earlier arrivals had already completed the former period, while arrivals from 2025/26 begin wholly under the new rules.

Arrivals in 2023/24 or 2024/25:

Employees covered by this protection are referred to here as eligible transitional employees where they:

  • first became UK resident in 2023/24 or 2024/25;
  • qualified for the former OWR under the pre-6 April 2025 rules; and
  • elected to use the remittance basis in at least one relevant tax year before 6 April 2025.

Nature of the transitional relief: For tax years beginning on or after 6 April 2025, an eligible transitional employee claims the new OWR—Foreign Employment Relief—rather than the former remittance-basis OWR. The transition preserves OWR eligibility and disapplies the normal £300,000/30% financial limit for eligible qualifying years ending before 6 April 2028. It does not continue the former legal regime, and relief remains limited to qualifying employment income relating to overseas duties.

The employee's total OWR period and the financial-limit treatment are then determined as follows:

  • if the employee is a qualifying new resident in 2025/26, OWR may cover the first four UK-resident years;
  • if the employee is not a qualifying new resident in 2025/26, the transition may preserve OWR for the first three UK-resident years; and
  • financial-limit protection: although relief from 2025/26 is obtained through the new OWR election and claim procedure, the £300,000/30% cap does not apply to the employee's eligible qualifying years ending before 6 April 2028. This preserves the uncapped treatment available under the former OWR.

Arrival in 2022/23: An employee who used the former OWR generally completed the old three-year period in 2022/23, 2023/24 and 2024/25. The transition does not provide an additional OWR year in 2025/26, so the employee cannot make an OWR election for that year. However, if the employee satisfies the qualifying-new-resident test, they may still claim other relief available under the four-year FIG regime.

6. Election, Claim and SA109 Reporting

The OWR election and the claim for relief are separate. The employee must make an election for the qualifying year before claiming relief on income relating to that year:

  • Box 40: Make the OWR election for the qualifying year.
  • Box 41: Indicate that an OWR claim is being made.
  • Boxes 44 to 49: Report the qualifying employment income, qualifying foreign employment income, financial limit and quantified relief.

A claim is made in the Self Assessment return for the tax year in which the qualifying foreign employment income is charged to tax. If a later bonus relates to overseas duties in an earlier qualifying year, relief may be claimed when the bonus is taxed only if the required election was made for that earlier year.

Time limit: The normal deadline for both the election and claim is the anniversary of 31 January following the end of the relevant tax year—12 months after the ordinary online filing deadline. For 2025/26, the normal deadline is 31 January 2028.

7. Consequences of an OWR Election

An OWR election causes the loss of specified allowances and reliefs for the qualifying year, including the Personal Allowance (PA), Blind Person's Allowance, certain tax reductions and transferable allowances for married couples and civil partners, specified life-insurance relief, and the CGT Annual Exempt Amount (AEA). It also restricts foreign qualifying capital losses, losses of qualifying overseas trades or property businesses and overseas-property finance-cost relief. See FIG Claims: Lost Allowances and Restricted Reliefs for the detailed rules.
