Illustrative cross-topic examples for claim choices, residence, employment, historical funds, offshore structures and IHT.

FIG Claim and Mobility Examples

Scope: These fictional examples illustrate the rules for 2025/26 onwards using official guidance checked on 17 July 2026. Actual results depend on all income, gains, residence, treaty and claim facts.

Example A: Source-by-Source Claim Choice

A qualifying new resident receives foreign interest on which overseas tax was paid and has a small UK capital gain.

They compare the UK liability after any available Foreign Tax Credit Relief with a FIG claim for the interest. A FIG claim prevents credit being claimed on the relieved interest and removes the CGT annual exempt amount even though no foreign-gain claim is made. The decision therefore uses the complete tax computation, not the foreign interest in isolation.

Example B: Non-Resident Year within the Four-Year Window

An individual first becomes UK resident in 2025/26, is non-UK resident throughout 2026/27 and returns in 2027/28.

2026/27 cannot support a FIG claim, but it still occupies the second year of the original four-year period. Subject to the other conditions, 2027/28 is year three. The non-resident year is not carried forward and does not create a fresh window.

Example C: OWR Financial Limit

A qualifying employee has £500,000 of relevant employment income, of which £200,000 relates to qualifying overseas duties.

Ignoring transitional treatment and other adjustments, the financial limit is the lower of £300,000 and 30% of £500,000, which is £150,000. OWR cannot exceed £150,000 even though the overseas-duty amount is £200,000.

Example D: Historical Funds, TRF and BIR

A former remittance-basis user holds identifiable untaxed foreign income from 2022/23 and considers a UK business investment during 2026/27.

They compare ordinary remittance tax, a TRF designation and Business Investment Relief before moving the money. Already-designated TRF capital cannot form a qualifying BIR investment, while an eligible investment of undesignated historical funds must satisfy the BIR conditions and claim deadline.

The Shift to a Residence-Based IHT System

The historical landscape of UK Inheritance Tax (IHT) relied heavily on an individual's common-law domicile status. Under the updated framework, domicile is replaced by a strict, statutory residence test. Individuals become subject to worldwide IHT once they meet long-term UK residency thresholds, fundamentally altering how offshore assets and trust structures are protected.

The 10-Year Long-Term Resident Tail

Scenario: An individual moves to the UK, resides there for 11 years, and then leaves the UK permanently to settle abroad.

Tax Treatment: An individual who leaves after 11 UK-resident tax years normally remains a long-term UK resident for the minimum three-tax-year tail, not ten years. During that period, foreign property can remain within IHT subject to the detailed chargeable-event and transitional rules.

Gift with Reservation of Benefit (GWR)

Scenario: A foreign national living in London transfers an offshore investment portfolio into a personal bare trust but continues to receive the monthly dividend income stream directly to their personal account.

Tax Treatment: Because the individual retains a direct benefit from the gifted asset, the Gift with Reservation rules apply. The entire value of the offshore portfolio remains inside their taxable estate for UK IHT purposes, regardless of its physical location outside the UK.

Dismantling of Non-UK Resident Trust Protections

Scenario: A non-domiciled individual settled an excluded property trust holding overseas assets prior to April 2025. The settlor is a beneficiary and is now a long-term UK resident.

Tax Treatment: The historic grandfathered protections for settled excluded property are broken. Because the settlor is a long-term resident and retains an interest, the trust assets lose their protection and fall squarely within the scope of the UK IHT relevant property regime (subject to ten-year anniversary and exit charges).
