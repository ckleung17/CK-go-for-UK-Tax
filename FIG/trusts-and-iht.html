The Impact on Non-UK Resident Trusts & Residence-Based Inheritance Tax (IHT)

A technical teardown of the sweeping long-term residency rules governing offshore settlements and worldwide estate liabilities.

The Demolition of Protected Trust Status

The introduction of the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime on 6 April 2025 radically altered the tax positioning of offshore settlements. Under the historical framework, non-UK resident trusts established by non-domiciled individuals enjoyed "Protected Trust" status. This isolated trust income and capital gains from the settlor, even if the settlor remained a UK resident.

The Modern Framework: Protected Trust status has been entirely abolished by Finance Act 2025. If a settlor is a UK resident but does not qualify for (or has exhausted) the 4-year FIG holiday, they are automatically taxed on the arising basis on all income and capital gains generated within the trust structure—regardless of whether any funds are distributed.

Review HMRC's updated procedural filing standards for trust disclosures via HMRC Helpsheet HS266 (Settlements & Trust Disclosures).

The Migration to Residence-Based IHT

Parallel to the income tax changes, the core mechanism determining Inheritance Tax (IHT) exposures moved away from traditional domicile, switching to a transparent, statutory test tracking time spent in the UK tax net.

The Long-Term Resident Metric (The 10-Year Test)

An individual is classified as a "Long-Term Resident" and brought into the scope of worldwide IHT if they have been a UK resident under the Statutory Residence Test (SRT) for at least 10 out of the 20 tax years immediately preceding the year of assessment or death.

The 10-Year Tail

Once an individual breaks into this 10-out-of-20-years window, they cannot easily shed their UK IHT exposure. If they exit the UK permanently, they face a 10-year IHT tail. Their worldwide estate remains fully liable to UK IHT at standard rates for up to 10 consecutive tax years post-departure.

For formal administrative updates regarding asset situs and charging rules, see HMRC IHT Technical Framework Note.
