The 10-Year Prior Non-Residence Rule & Split-Year Implications

A rigorous inspection of how historical residence gaps and mid-year arrivals affect an individual's eligibility for FIG relief.

The 10-Year Non-Residence Condition

To satisfy the residence condition for becoming a qualifying new resident (QNR), an individual must have been non-UK resident in each of the 10 consecutive tax years immediately preceding the first qualifying year. Separate conditions also apply concerning the individual's age and membership of the House of Commons or House of Lords.

CPD Trap: UK residence in any tax year within the 10-year lookback period breaks the required sequence. The individual would need to complete a new period of at least 10 consecutive non-UK-resident tax years before becoming eligible as a QNR on a later return to the UK.

The QNR conditions are set out in ITTOIA 2005, section 845B, as inserted by Finance Act 2025, Schedule 1. See also HMRC Manual RFIG44000 (Qualifying New Resident).

Interaction with Split-Year Treatment

Under the SRT, an individual is either UK resident or non-UK resident for the whole tax year. When an individual arrives in the UK part-way through a year and meets the statutory conditions, split-year treatment applies automatically and divides the year into an overseas part and a UK part for specified tax purposes. Its interaction with the FIG regime has several important consequences:

1. Impact on the Four-Year FIG Period

If split-year treatment applies in the year of arrival, that tax year is the individual's first qualifying year for FIG purposes, even though the UK part begins later in the year. It therefore counts as the first of the four consecutive tax years in the FIG eligibility period; the period cannot be extended or prorated because of a mid-year arrival.

2. Taxation in the "Overseas Part"

During the overseas part of a split year, the individual is generally taxed as a non-UK resident. Consequently, much foreign income and many foreign gains arising during that period will not be taxable in the UK, so no FIG claim is required for those amounts. Exceptions can apply; where qualifying foreign income or gains arising during the overseas part remain taxable, FIG relief may still be claimed.

For guidance on split-year treatment, see HMRC Manual RFIG21010 (What a Split Year Is), HMRC Manual RFIG44000 (Qualifying New Resident) and HMRC Helpsheet HS266.

3. Lookback Year Disqualification

Crucial Distinction: If an individual was a UK resident in a historical tax year that qualified for split-year treatment (e.g., when they originally left the UK years prior), that entire tax year counts as a year of UK residence. It cannot be counted as a year of non-residence toward the required 10-year gap.
