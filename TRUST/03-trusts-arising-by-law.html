Types of trust and how they arise

Express, resulting, constructive and statutory trusts

HomeTrust Index

Overview

The previous topic examined whether a deliberately created trust is valid. Classification asks a different question: what legal route produced the trust relationship? Intention, failed dispositions, equitable obligations and legislation can each produce different rights even where legal title is registered in the same way.

The principal starting classifications are express trusts, resulting trusts, constructive trusts and statutory trusts. These labels describe how the trust arises; further classification is still needed to identify the beneficial interests, trustees’ powers and tax treatment. Begin with the category based directly on manifested intention.

Express trusts

An express trust is deliberately created when a settlor manifests an intention that identified property is to be held on binding trust terms. It may be created during life by transferring property to trustees or by declaring oneself trustee, or it may arise under a will on death. A deed is common and often prudent, but the required method and formalities depend on the property and transaction.

The instrument normally identifies the trustees, property, beneficiaries or permitted purpose, dispositive provisions, administrative powers and duration. Its label is not conclusive: the validity tests in the previous topic must still be satisfied, and each asset must be properly constituted. If the intended beneficial disposition fails, the resulting ownership position may lead to the next classification.

Resulting trusts

A resulting trust reflects beneficial ownership returning to, or remaining with, a person rather than passing as the legal title appears to suggest. It may arise where an express trust or part of its beneficial disposition fails, leaving property undisposed of, or where a person contributes to acquiring property placed in another person’s name and the facts support an inference that no beneficial gift was intended.

The doctrine is fact-sensitive. Evidence of a gift, loan, common ownership arrangement, contract or other intention may change the result, and presumptions or counter-presumptions must not replace examination of the actual transaction. Because a resulting trust focuses on retained or returned beneficial ownership, it must be distinguished from an obligation imposed in response to conduct or circumstances.

Constructive trusts

A constructive trust arises by operation of equitable rules rather than because a settlor completed an express declaration. The label covers more than one context. It may describe obligations connected with unauthorised fiduciary gains, dealings with property subject to another person’s rights, or circumstances in which it would be legally unconscionable for the holder to deny the claimant’s beneficial interest.

Do not use “constructive trust” as a general fairness label. The claimant must identify the recognised legal basis, the relevant property or gain, when the obligation arose and the persons bound by it. The facts and remedy can differ substantially from a resulting trust. Neither category should be assumed where legislation itself supplies the trust.

Statutory trusts

A statutory trust arises because legislation imposes or defines the trust relationship. It does not depend on proving that a settlor intended to create an express trust. The statute identifies the triggering event, property, persons who hold it, beneficiaries and powers, although another instrument or court process may be needed to administer the arrangement.

Examples can arise in estate administration and intestacy, co-ownership and other regulated contexts. The precise statute must be identified rather than treating “statutory trust” as a single uniform form. Its provisions may modify ordinary trust rules and may also determine when the trust begins or ends. With all four routes identified, the classifications can be compared systematically.

Comparing the classifications

Ask first what creates the obligation: manifested intention for an express trust, retained or returned beneficial ownership for a resulting trust, a recognised equitable rule for a constructive trust, or legislation for a statutory trust. Then identify the property, trustees, beneficiaries, commencement date, powers and available remedies.

Classification affects the evidence required and may affect registration, reporting and tax ownership, but a private-law label does not by itself settle every tax question. Record alternative analyses where the facts are incomplete or disputed. That need for evidence and disciplined classification leads directly to the practical safeguards below.

Practical issues and safeguards

HMRC guidance explains HMRC’s approach to ownership evidence but does not decide private-law disputes. Where the parties disagree, or land ownership is material, obtain specialist legal advice and preserve evidence of funding, intention and conduct.

  • Preserve evidence of contributions, loans, gifts, agreements and later conduct.
  • State separately whether the asserted basis is express, resulting, constructive or statutory.
  • Refer disputed ownership or land claims for specialist legal review before tax reporting.

Points to check

  • Family deed: test whether the words, property, beneficiaries, formalities and transfer create a valid express trust.
  • Investment flat: examine the payment records and evidence of intention before deciding whether a resulting trust, gift, loan or another arrangement applies.
  • Secret commission: establish Daniel’s fiduciary role and the connection between that role and the benefit before applying the constructive-trust rule.
  • Intestate estate: apply the statutory scheme governing the personal representatives and beneficiaries rather than treating it as a trust deliberately created by deed.

Applied analysis: The classification depends on whether the obligation arises from expressed intention, retained beneficial ownership, a recognised equitable rule or legislation. The documents and facts must be proved, and the private-law label does not by itself determine the tax or registration result.
