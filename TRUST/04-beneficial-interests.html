Beneficial interests

Fixed rights, discretionary objects and future interests

HomeTrust Index

Overview

A beneficiary?s position must be read from the trust terms and applicable law. A person may own a fixed share, have an immediate right to income, hold a future or contingent interest, or be only an object of a trustee power. Those descriptions affect control, valuation, tax and disclosure.

The deed and surrounding law must show whether a person holds a fixed interest, an interest in possession, a remainder or reversion, or merely stands as a discretionary object.

Understanding beneficial interests

Fixed interest

A beneficiary has a fixed interest where the trust instrument determines who is to benefit and the share or amount to which that person is entitled. The trustees may still have administrative powers over investment, timing or the form in which property is transferred, but they cannot simply redirect the fixed entitlement to someone else unless the instrument contains a valid overriding power that permits that result.

A fixed interest does not necessarily mean immediate possession. Payment may be postponed until a specified age or event, and the interest may be vested or subject to a condition. The instrument must therefore be read to establish both the extent of the entitlement and when, or whether, the beneficiary can require payment or transfer.

Illustrative example: A deed states that Nia and Omar are each entitled to 50% of an investment fund, payable when they reach age 30. The trustees may manage and reinvest the fund, but cannot change the stated 50:50 division unless the deed gives them an overriding power. If entitlement depends on each beneficiary surviving to 30, the amount is fixed but remains contingent until that condition is satisfied.

Interest in possession

An interest in possession broadly gives a beneficiary a present right to current trust income or to present enjoyment of particular property. A common example is a life tenant entitled to the net income of an investment fund or permitted to occupy a trust property during life. Trustees receive and administer the assets, meet expenses properly chargeable against income and account for the balance to the beneficiary.

The income beneficiary does not necessarily own the underlying capital. Capital may be preserved for another beneficiary, and the instrument may contain powers affecting investment, occupation or advancement. Tax legislation also distinguishes particular qualifying interests, so the trust-law description must be tested separately for Income Tax, Capital Gains Tax and Inheritance Tax.

Illustrative example: A will trust requires the trustees to pay the net income from a share portfolio to Priya for life, with the capital passing to her children on her death. Priya has the present income interest, but she cannot demand that the portfolio itself be transferred to her merely because she receives its income.

Remainder or reversion

A remainder is a future beneficial interest intended to take effect after an earlier interest ends. For example, children may become entitled to the capital after a surviving spouse’s life interest terminates. A remainder may be vested, meaning the beneficiary is already identified with an existing interest, or contingent on survival, age or another condition.

A reversion is conceptually different: property returns to the settlor or the settlor’s estate when the trust ends or where the beneficial disposition does not exhaust the whole interest. The instrument, any default provisions and the circumstances of creation must be examined before deciding whether the future interest belongs to a named remainderman or remains with the settlor by way of reversion.

Illustrative example: A deed gives Elena the income from a fund for life and directs the capital to Marcus on her death. Marcus holds the remainder, although his right to possession is postponed while Elena’s interest continues. If the deed instead failed to dispose effectively of the capital after Elena’s death and contained no effective default provision, the undisposed beneficial interest might revert to the settlor or the settlor’s estate, subject to the instrument and applicable law.

Discretionary object

A discretionary object is within a class of persons whom trustees may consider for income or capital benefits, but has no fixed share merely by being included in that class. Until the trustees validly exercise their power, the object cannot ordinarily demand a particular payment or treat a specified part of the trust fund as their own.

The object nevertheless has enforceable rights concerning administration. Trustees must understand the class, consider relevant objects when exercising their discretion, disregard improper considerations, manage conflicts and act for the purpose for which the power was given. A valid appointment can then create a fixed entitlement or an immediate payment, whose legal and tax character must be recorded.

Illustrative example: Trustees may distribute income or capital among the settlor’s children and grandchildren. Before a decision is made, no member of the class owns a fixed percentage of the fund. If the trustees validly appoint £20,000 to one grandchild for university costs, that appointment creates an entitlement to the specified payment; it does not give that grandchild a fixed share of the remaining trust property.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not treat every life interest as a qualifying interest in possession for IHT. The creation date and statutory category, particularly after 22 March 2006, are material.

  • Map current income, capital, contingent, survivorship and discretionary rights.
  • Test vesting and possession separately for each fund and later addition.
  • For IHT, record creation date and statutory category rather than trust-law shorthand.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Fictional will trust: Maya is entitled to net income for life. After her death, half of the capital is to pass to Leo and half to Sara if she survives Maya. During Maya’s lifetime, the trustees may appoint some capital to Maya’s children or grandchildren for specified needs.

Leo argues that he and Sara already own the whole fund and can require the trust to end.

Points to check

  • Maya: has the present income interest, but not an automatic right to the capital.
  • Leo: may have a vested remainder, although the appointment power can reduce the capital he ultimately receives.
  • Sara: has a contingent remainder because her entitlement depends on surviving Maya.
  • Children and grandchildren: are discretionary objects of the appointment power and do not own fixed shares before it is exercised.

Applied analysis: The present income interest, vested or contingent remainders and discretionary power must be considered together. Leo and Sara cannot require the trust to end while other interests or powers remain, and any appointment must be authorised, documented and reviewed separately for tax.
