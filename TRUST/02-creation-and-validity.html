Creation and validity

Legal foundations — forming an enforceable trust

HomeTrust Index

Overview

Creation and validity asks whether a person deliberately established an enforceable trust and, if so, when each asset became subject to it. For an England-and-Wales analysis, work through four connected questions: are the trust terms sufficiently certain; did the person creating the trust have capacity and authority; were the required formalities satisfied; and was the trust properly constituted?

Each question performs a different function. Certainty identifies the obligation, property and beneficiaries; capacity and authority identify whether the creator could make the disposition; formalities determine whether the law requires a particular form or evidence; and constitution determines whether the chosen method of creation was completed. The first step is therefore to examine the three certainties.

The three certainties

Certainty of intention asks whether the words and conduct objectively show an immediate intention to impose binding trustee obligations. A wish, moral request, future plan or label is not conclusive. Read the document as a whole and consider the surrounding transaction rather than relying on the word “trust” alone.

Certainty of subject matter asks whether the trust property, and the beneficial shares where relevant, can be identified. The asset may be identified immediately or by a workable mechanism, but trustees and beneficiaries must be able to determine what is held on trust and what remains outside it.

Certainty of objects asks whether the beneficiaries or permitted purpose are described with the degree of certainty required for that form of trust. For a fixed trust, the beneficial entitlements must be ascertainable; for a discretionary trust, the class must satisfy the applicable test so trustees can determine whether a person is within it. If a private trust lacks the required certainty, the intended beneficial disposition may fail. The next question is whether the person making that disposition had capacity and authority to do so.

Capacity and authority

Capacity concerns whether the settlor understood and could make the relevant disposition. The required analysis depends on the person and transaction: an adult acting personally, a child, a person whose mental capacity is in question, a personal representative, trustee, attorney, deputy, company or other body may be subject to different rules.

Authority is distinct from capacity. A person may understand the transaction but still lack power to dispose of the asset. Check legal and beneficial ownership, constitutional documents, court orders, powers of attorney, estate or trust instruments, board approvals and any restrictions attached to the property. Jointly owned property also requires the authority of the persons whose title or interest is being affected.

Capacity and authority should be evidenced at the time of creation, particularly where vulnerability, conflict or representative action is involved. Once the correct person and power have been identified, examine whether the law requires particular formalities.

Formalities

There is no single formality for every trust. The requirements depend on the asset, method of creation and governing legislation. A lifetime trust of personal property may sometimes be declared without a deed, but evidential difficulty remains and other legal, tax or registration requirements may still make written documentation essential in practice.

Land requires special attention. Section 53 of the Law of Property Act 1925 contains writing requirements for declarations of trust respecting land and for dispositions of existing equitable interests, subject to its terms and exceptions. A trust created by will must also comply with the applicable testamentary formalities. Companies, registered shares, bank accounts and other assets may require their own execution, consent, transfer or registration steps.

Formal validity does not necessarily mean that the asset has reached the trust. A correctly executed deed may record the terms while a separate transfer remains outstanding. That distinction leads to constitution.

Constitution

Constitution asks whether the trust was completed by the method the settlor chose. If the settlor declares that they already hold identified property as trustee, no transfer to another trustee is needed, but the declaration must be sufficiently clear and satisfy any applicable formalities. If the settlor appoints other trustees, the legal title or necessary control must generally be transferred to them using the steps required for that asset.

The review is asset by asset. Cash may be paid into a trustee-controlled account; registered shares may require an executed transfer and registration; land may require a deed and registration; contractual rights may require an effective assignment. A schedule should record the intended property, the chosen method, each completion step and the date on which it occurred.

Do not treat an ineffective transfer as a self-declaration merely to rescue the transaction. A promise to make a later transfer is different from an immediate declaration that the owner now holds the asset as trustee. Once constitution has been tested asset by asset, the practical task is to preserve evidence of every declaration, transfer and registration step.

Practical issues and safeguards

A tax return, family understanding or later bookkeeping entry cannot by itself cure an invalid declaration or incomplete transfer. The consequences of failure may include continued beneficial ownership or a resulting trust.

  • Prepare an asset-by-asset constitution schedule.
  • Check execution, witnessing, delivery, capacity and asset-specific formalities.
  • Retain dated transfers, bank evidence and register updates rather than relying only on the deed.
