Commercial and specialist trusts

Trust structures — purpose-specific regimes

HomeTrust Index

Overview

A commercial or specialist trust uses trust ownership for a defined employment, pension, charitable, investment, financing or property purpose. The word “trust” does not identify the legal or tax result. The governing documents, activity, parties and regulatory status must be established before ordinary family-trust assumptions are applied.

An employee benefit trust holds value in an employment-related setting and may engage employment, corporation-tax, securities and Inheritance Tax rules. A pension trust operates within a specialist pensions and regulatory framework, so its registration and benefit rules must be identified rather than inferred from trustee ownership alone.

A charitable trust must satisfy the applicable charity-law purposes and public-benefit requirements, with regulator registration and HMRC recognition considered separately. A co-ownership or security trust instead holds title for beneficial owners, lenders or investors whose rights arise principally from property or finance documents. Each arrangement therefore requires its own legal, regulatory, tax and registration analysis.

Distinguishing commercial and specialist trusts

Employee benefit trust

An employee benefit trust (EBT) commonly holds cash or shares for employees, former employees or their families. The deed identifies the class and trustee powers, but the employment connection requires a separate map of the employer, funding company, trustee, employee, connected persons and every award, earmarking, loan or asset made available.

Part 7A of ITEPA 2003 can charge employment income when statutory conditions and a relevant step are present, subject to detailed exclusions and priority rules. Employment-related securities, Pay As You Earn, National Insurance contributions and corporation-tax deduction timing may also apply. IHTA 1984 section 86 treatment is conditional; calling an arrangement an EBT does not establish the exemption.

Illustrative example: A trading company pays £2 million to trustees who acquire company shares for future employee awards. Before earmarking shares for a director, the file tests Part 7A, the securities rules, PAYE and National Insurance, the employer’s deduction and whether the IHT employee-trust conditions remain satisfied.

Pension trust

Many occupational pension schemes use trustees, but their purpose is to provide retirement and death benefits under a specialist statutory and regulatory system. Establish whether the scheme is registered with HMRC, registrable with The Pensions Regulator, or an employer-financed or overseas arrangement. Identify the scheme administrator, sponsoring employer, trustees, members and benefit rules.

Registered status affects contributions, investments and benefits, while pensions tax and regulatory reporting should not be replaced by ordinary discretionary-trust assumptions. HMRC states that qualifying registered pension arrangements are excluded from relevant property under IHTA 1984 section 58(1)(d); a non-registered employer-financed arrangement requires a different analysis.

Illustrative example: An employer calls a new trust a “retirement reserve” and allows trustees to make benefits to selected senior employees. The label does not make it a registered pension scheme. The employer must establish its HMRC and regulatory status and test Part 7A and IHT before funding or promising benefits.

Charitable trust

A charitable trust must be established for exclusively charitable purposes and for public benefit under the law applying in its jurisdiction. The governing document should define the purposes, trustee powers, application of income and capital, conflicts, private benefit controls and destination of property on dissolution.

Registration with the relevant charity regulator and recognition by HMRC for charity tax relief are separate processes. Recognition does not make every receipt or activity exempt: trustees must distinguish primary-purpose trading, investment, fundraising, non-charitable expenditure and benefits to connected persons, and keep evidence that funds further the stated purposes.

Illustrative example: Trustees fund financial education for the public but also propose subsidised consultancy for the founder’s company. They test whether the activity advances the charitable purposes for public benefit, manage the conflict and private benefit, and determine the charity-law and tax treatment before contracting.

Co-ownership or security trust

A co-ownership trust separates registered title from the beneficial shares of the owners. The declaration, purchase funding and later contributions should identify who owns income and capital and whether the trustees and beneficiaries are the same persons. Those facts affect tax attribution and whether the specific Trust Registration Service exclusion applies.

A security trustee instead holds collateral or security rights for lenders or noteholders. Beneficial rights may follow the finance documents, priority waterfall and enforcement mechanism rather than a family-benefit discretion. The file must distinguish holding security from owning the borrower’s operating asset and examine perfection, registration, amendments, enforcement and release in every relevant jurisdiction.

Illustrative example: Four banks appoint a security trustee over a borrower’s English property and shares. The trustee acts for the finance parties under an agreed priority waterfall; it does not own the borrower’s equity for its own benefit. A refinancing must update the secured obligations, beneficiaries, registrations and releases rather than being treated as a family-trust appointment.

Practical issues and safeguards

A product name containing “trust” does not prove that ordinary trust taxation applies. Unit trusts, pension schemes and investment trusts can have materially different legal forms and statutory regimes.

  • Identify the legal and regulatory regime before ordinary trust taxation.
  • Map all employers, trustees, employees, operators, connected entities and benefits.
  • Check employment tax, corporation tax, securities, IHT and registration separately.

With those safeguards recorded, the illustration below brings the relevant findings together and tests whether the documents, chronology and proposed treatment remain consistent.

Illustration

Fictional group reorganisation: Northstar Ltd proposes four vehicles: an EBT to acquire shares for staff awards; a retirement trust for senior executives; a charitable trust for public digital-skills training; and a security trust for banks financing a new property. Management asks whether one standard discretionary deed and one tax analysis can cover them all.

The answer is no. Trust principles remain relevant, but each vehicle has a different purpose, beneficiary or member group, regulator, event sequence and tax framework.

How Northstar should proceed

  1. Write a separate purpose statement and transaction map for each proposed vehicle.
  2. Identify the governing jurisdiction, legal form, regulator and required registrations before drafting.
  3. Map settlors or employers, trustees, members or beneficiaries, controllers, service providers and connected persons.
  4. Test funding, earmarking, awards, benefits, investment, enforcement and release as distinct events.
  5. Apply the specialist tax regime first, then check ordinary trust, IHT, CGT and registration consequences that remain relevant.
  6. Use separate deeds, accounts, minutes, data and compliance calendars where the regimes and duties differ.
  7. Obtain specialist legal, tax and regulatory review before implementation and after any material change.

Applied analysis: A single standard deed would obscure material differences. The EBT is driven by employment reward, the retirement trust by pension status, the charitable trust by exclusive purposes and public benefit, and the security trust by creditor rights. Each requires a separate authority, decision and reporting trail.
