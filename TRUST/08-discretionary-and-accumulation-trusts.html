Discretionary and accumulation trusts

Trust structures — flexible benefit and retained income

HomeTrust Index

Overview

A discretionary trust gives trustees authority to choose which eligible beneficiaries benefit, what they receive and when. The discretionary class, exclusions and default provisions define the people and purposes the trustees must consider. Until a power is exercised, an object of the discretion normally has no fixed entitlement to a particular part of the fund.

A letter of wishes may explain the settlor’s preferences and family circumstances, but it does not ordinarily replace the trustees’ independent judgment. Accumulation concerns whether income may be retained and added to the trust fund rather than distributed, subject to the instrument and applicable law.

The tax framework depends on the legal character of each receipt, retention, payment or capital appointment. Trustee Income Tax, beneficiary tax credits, the tax pool, Capital Gains Tax, Inheritance Tax and reporting should therefore be considered separately after the trustees identify the power being used.

How discretion and accumulation operate

Discretionary class

The trust instrument should define the persons who may receive income or capital. A class might include the settlor’s descendants, their spouses and charities, while expressly excluding the settlor. Trustees must also distinguish discretionary objects from default beneficiaries, who take only if a power is not exercised, and from persons holding powers such as appointment or consent.

An object can require proper administration and genuine consideration, but cannot normally demand a specific distribution before an appointment is made. Trustees need not give equal amounts or conduct a mechanical survey of every person on every occasion. They should, however, understand the range of the class, obtain sufficient information for the decision being made, address conflicts and use the power for its proper purpose.

Illustrative example: A deed permits payments to any descendant of Priya and to registered charities. Priya’s adult son is an object, but he does not own one-third of the fund merely because there are three children. Before paying his university fees, the trustees consider the other branches of the family, available resources and the deed’s purposes.

Letter of wishes

A settlor may give trustees a letter explaining family circumstances and preferences. Unless the deed makes a direction binding, the letter does not amend the beneficial class or compel a result. Trustees should consider current wishes alongside the deed, later information and the interests of the whole class; they must not treat the settlor as the real decision-maker.

The file should record which version was considered and whether circumstances have changed. Confidentiality, disclosure and the reasons recorded in minutes require separate legal judgment. A formulaic resolution that simply copies the letter may suggest that the trustees failed to exercise their own discretion.

Illustrative example: Priya’s letter asks the trustees to prioritise her eldest child. Ten years later that child is financially secure and another child has substantial care needs. The trustees consider the letter but decide independently whether the changed evidence justifies a different distribution.

Accumulation

Accumulation means retaining income and adding it to the trust fund under an authorised power. The deed must be read for any duty to distribute, discretion to accumulate, duration or beneficiary right that restricts retention. For instruments governed by England and Wales, the Perpetuities and Accumulations Act 2009 changed the former restrictions for instruments taking effect within its scope, but older instruments and charitable trusts require their own analysis.

Trust accounts should still preserve the source, tax paid and date of accumulated income. Trust-law conversion into an accretion to capital does not erase the income’s tax history, and HMRC’s relevant-property guidance recognises that accumulated income may enter the IHT calculation by reference to when it became relevant property.

Illustrative example: The trustees receive £24,000 net rental and investment income in 2026/27. They pay £8,000 for an object’s training and validly accumulate the balance. The accounts identify the gross sources, expenses, trustee tax and retained amount instead of recording a single unexplained increase in capital.

Tax framework

Tax classification follows the legal event rather than the trustees’ everyday description. Trustees are generally charged on trust income under the accumulation-and-discretionary trust rules, subject to the source of income and current legislation. When they make a discretionary income payment, the beneficiary treatment and associated tax credit interact with the trust’s tax pool. HMRC states that trustees must keep a running record of tax entering and leaving that pool and may owe additional tax if available pool tax does not cover the credits attached to payments.

Capital appointments require a separate Capital Gains Tax review, including disposal value and any available hold-over relief. For IHT, many discretionary settlements fall within the relevant-property regime, under which ten-year anniversary and proportionate charges may arise; exceptions and reliefs must be tested rather than assumed. Trust Registration Service and Self Assessment duties are additional compliance questions.

Illustrative example: Trustees describe a £30,000 transfer as “help with a deposit”. The deed and resolution must show whether it is income, accumulated income or capital. That classification affects the tax pool, possible CGT disposal, any relevant-property exit charge, the beneficiary paperwork and the trust records.

Practical issues and safeguards

A beneficiary’s expectation, previous pattern of payments or inclusion in a letter of wishes does not create a fixed entitlement. Conversely, repeated predetermined decisions can raise questions about whether discretion was genuinely exercised.

  • Identify the whole class, defaults, accumulation period and each dispositive power.
  • Record circumstances, conflicts and purpose without treating wishes as binding.
  • Classify each payment legally and for tax before transferring value.

With those safeguards recorded, the illustration below brings the relevant findings together and tests whether the documents, chronology and proposed treatment remain consistent.

Illustration

Fictional family trust: Priya settled investments and a rental property in 2018 for her children, later descendants and charities. The trustees may distribute or accumulate income, appoint capital and consult a non-binding letter of wishes. Priya is excluded from benefit.

In 2026/27 the trust receives dividends and rent. Arun requests £35,000 for postgraduate study; Bea requests £120,000 towards an adapted home; and Priya’s letter asks that Arun should normally be favoured. The trustees are also approaching the 2028 ten-year anniversary.

How the trustees should proceed

  1. Read the dispositive, accumulation, exclusion, default and administrative provisions together.
  2. Update the class and conflict schedules and obtain proportionate evidence about both requests.
  3. Consider the letter of wishes without surrendering trustee judgment.
  4. Determine available income, accumulated income and capital from reconciled accounts.
  5. Resolve each request separately under the correct power and record the reasons and implementation conditions.
  6. Before payment, test Income Tax and the tax pool, CGT, relevant-property IHT and reporting consequences.
  7. Update accounts, minutes, beneficiary statements, Trust Registration Service information and the ten-year-anniversary file consistently.

Applied analysis: Neither applicant begins with an entitlement to a fixed sum. Priya’s preference is relevant but not decisive. The trustees may reach different outcomes for Arun and Bea if they consider the whole class, use their powers for proper purposes and document the evidence. Only after choosing the legal form of each benefit can the tax treatment be determined.
