Overview

Why form a trust? A trust separates the management and legal ownership of property from the right to benefit from it. It may be used to control and protect family assets, provide for a child or a person who cannot manage property personally, preserve assets for beneficiaries over time, or govern how property passes during the settlor’s lifetime or after death. A trust should be chosen for its legal and practical purpose, not on the assumption that it automatically saves tax.

When is a trust formed? The answer depends on the legal basis on which the trust is said to arise. A deliberately created trust must satisfy the validity and asset-transfer requirements examined in the next topic. Other trusts may arise under legislation or general legal and equitable rules without a deliberate declaration. The documents, events, type of asset and applicable law must therefore be identified before fixing the commencement date.

Formal solicitor-led route. In practice, a settlor establishing a significant lifetime trust will commonly instruct a solicitor to advise on the intended structure and draft a trust deed. The settlor and trustees then execute the deed with the required formalities, the trustees accept their appointment, and ownership or control of the identified assets is transferred or registered in the trustees’ names as appropriate. A solicitor may coordinate with tax advisers, investment providers, banks or the Land Registry and retain evidence that the trust was properly constituted. Using a solicitor is prudent for a bespoke or valuable trust but does not, by itself, create a valid trust: the deed, the parties’ actions and completion of any necessary asset transfer must all be examined. A will trust is usually documented in a professionally drafted will and takes effect through the estate after death.

A trust is therefore a relationship concerning identified property. Trustees hold or control the legal title and must exercise their powers for the beneficiaries or permitted purposes defined by the trust. The trust is not normally a separate legal person, although tax legislation often treats the trustees collectively as a taxable body. Players, trust property and overlapping roles

A trust is easier to analyse when each person and asset is placed in the correct legal capacity. The same name may appear in several places, but the rights and duties attached to each role remain distinct.

Settlor

The settlor is the person who creates or contributes property to the trust. The settlor chooses the initial terms of a deliberately created trust, identifies the first trustees and beneficiaries, and decides which property is to be committed. A later contributor may also be treated as a settlor for some legal or tax purposes, so additions should be recorded separately rather than attributed automatically to the original creator.

After creation, the settlor has only the powers and rights retained by the instrument or supplied by law. They do not own or control trust property merely because they created the arrangement. A power to appoint trustees, consent to decisions, amend provisions or benefit from the trust must be identified expressly and analysed separately.

Trustees

Trustees hold or control the legal title and administer the property under the trust terms and applicable law. They must identify the beneficiaries and their rights, safeguard and invest assets where appropriate, keep accounts and records, make authorised decisions, manage conflicts and complete the trust’s legal and tax obligations. Professional advisers and agents may assist, but responsibility remains with the trustees to the extent required by the instrument and law.

Where there is more than one trustee, authority and decision-making must be checked rather than assumed. The death, retirement or incapacity of an individual trustee does not normally make the beneficial interests disappear; the deed and applicable law determine who may continue acting, appoint a replacement and transfer or register title.

Beneficiaries

Beneficiaries are the persons entitled, or potentially entitled, to benefit. Their rights depend on the trust terms. A beneficiary may have a fixed share, a present right to income, a future or contingent interest, or only eligibility to be considered under a discretionary power. Those categories affect what the beneficiary can demand, what information may be available and how income, capital and tax consequences are analysed.

Being named within a discretionary class does not by itself give a fixed share of the fund. Conversely, a trustee cannot disregard an existing fixed or income entitlement merely because another outcome appears preferable. The exact interest must therefore be read from the instrument and subsequent valid appointments or variations.

Trust property

Trust property is the identifiable money, land, investments, contractual rights or other assets held subject to the trust. It is the subject matter connecting the trustees’ legal ownership or control with the beneficiaries’ rights. Records should distinguish trust assets from the trustees’ personal property and trace income, sale proceeds, reinvestments, substitutions and later additions.

The trust relationship applies only to property that has become subject to it. A deed may describe several intended assets while transfer or registration is completed for only some of them. That is why the next topic tests validity and constitution asset by asset.

Combining roles

The people involved do not always have to be different. A settlor may appoint themselves as one of the trustees and may, if the terms permit, also be a beneficiary. A beneficiary may serve as trustee, and the same individuals may act first as personal representatives of an estate and later as trustees. Each capacity must be identified separately because its powers, duties, ownership and tax reporting differ.

There must nevertheless be a genuine trust relationship concerning identifiable property. If one person becomes both the sole legal owner and the only person absolutely entitled to the entire beneficial interest, there may no longer be a meaningful separation between trustee and beneficiary. The precise result depends on the interests, powers and governing law; labels in a deed should not be assumed to preserve a trust after all legal and beneficial ownership has merged.

Multiple roles also increase conflict risk. The instrument should address decision-making, remuneration, conflicts and appointment powers, while tax analysis must separately test settlor-interested, benefit, attribution and reservation-of-benefit rules. With the players, capacities and property identified, the practical safeguards below show what evidence is needed before relying on the arrangement.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not infer a trust merely because one person manages money for another. Agency, debt, contract, co-ownership and corporate ownership have different legal consequences.

  • Obtain the instrument and evidence of how legal title was transferred or declared before classifying the relationship.
  • Identify each person’s capacity and whether each beneficiary has fixed, income or discretionary rights.
  • Keep trust assets, accounts and decisions separate from trustees’ personal property.
