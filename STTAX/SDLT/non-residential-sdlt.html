Non-residential SDLT

Scope: Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects the law and HMRC guidance checked on 16 July 2026.

Non-residential property means property that is not residential property. It may include shops, offices, warehouses, factories, agricultural land, commercial land, bare land, or other property that is not used or suitable for use as a dwelling.

Classification depends on the interest acquired at the transaction’s effective date. Agricultural land forming part of a dwelling’s garden or grounds is residential. Bare land may also be residential where construction of a dwelling is physically under way; planning permission alone does not normally mean construction has begun.

A transaction can also be mixed-use if it includes both residential and non-residential property. Mixed-use transactions are charged using the non-residential or mixed-use SDLT rates.

1. Residential, non-residential and mixed-use

For SDLT, residential property (FA 2003 s.116) includes a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is being constructed or adapted for use as a dwelling. It also includes land that forms part of the garden or grounds of the dwelling.

Non-residential property is property that is not residential property. A mixed-use transaction is one that includes both residential and non-residential property.

Cross-reference: See the separate page on mixed property for the boundary between residential grounds and genuine non-residential land.

2. Non-residential and mixed-use purchase rates

The current SDLT rates for freehold non-residential or mixed-use purchases, and for lease premiums, are:

Chargeable consideration SDLT rate
Up to £150,000 0%
Over £150,000 and up to £250,000 2%
Over £250,000 5%

Note: These rates apply to non-residential and mixed-use property. They do not apply to wholly residential purchases. Chargeable consideration generally includes any VAT payable on the transaction, even where the purchaser can recover it as input tax.

3. Illustrative example: freehold commercial property

Assume that, on 16 July 2026, a buyer purchases a fictional freehold commercial property for total chargeable consideration of £275,000. The amount is VAT-inclusive where VAT is payable, and there is no other chargeable consideration, linked transaction or relief. The SDLT is calculated using the non-residential rates.

Band Rate SDLT
First £150,000 0% £0
Next £100,000 2% £2,000
Remaining £25,000 5% £1,250
Total SDLT £3,250

4. Non-residential leases

For assignments, variations, surrender and regrant, holding over and overlap relief, see Leasehold transactions.

For a new non-residential lease, SDLT may need to be calculated on two separate elements:

  • any premium, which is the lump sum paid for the lease; and
  • the net present value of the rent, usually called NPV Net present value. For SDLT leases, this is a calculation that converts the future rent payable under the lease into a single present value figure. .

Note: Do not simply add the premium and rent together. SDLT on lease premiums and SDLT on rent are calculated under separate lease rules.

5. SDLT on non-residential lease premiums

The lease premium is charged using the same rates as a non-residential or mixed-use purchase:

Lease premium SDLT rate
Up to £150,000 0%
Over £150,000 and up to £250,000 2%
Over £250,000 5%

6. SDLT on non-residential rent

For rent under a new non-residential lease, SDLT is calculated by reference to the net present value of the rent.

Net present value of rent SDLT rate
Up to £150,000 0%
Over £150,000 and up to £5 million 1%
Over £5 million 2%

Memory point: Non-residential lease premium uses the purchase-rate table. Rent uses the NPV rent table.

7. Six or more dwellings

Where six or more separate dwellings are bought in a single transaction, those dwellings are treated as non-residential for SDLT purposes where the transaction involves the transfer of a major interest in, or the grant of a lease over, them (FA 2003 s.116).

This rule can be important for portfolio purchases, such as a block of flats or several residential units acquired together.

Note: This is not the same as Multiple Dwellings Relief. Multiple Dwellings Relief was abolished for transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024, subject to limited transitional rules.

8. Linked transactions

Non-residential transactions can also be linked. If transactions form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series between the same vendor and purchaser, or connected persons, the consideration may need to be aggregated.

Cross-reference: See the separate page on linked transactions.

9. Non-resident surcharge

The 2% SDLT non-resident surcharge normally applies to residential property purchases in England and Northern Ireland. It does not normally apply to purchases of non-residential property or mixed-use transactions.

Note: The non-resident surcharge is mainly a residential property rule. It does not normally apply to purely non-residential or mixed-use purchases.

10. Practical checklist

For non-residential SDLT, check:

  • is the property wholly non-residential, wholly residential, or mixed-use?
  • does any residential part form part of a mixed-use transaction?
  • is the transaction a freehold purchase, lease premium, rent-only lease, assignment or surrender?
  • for leases, is SDLT payable on the premium, rent NPV, or both?
  • are there linked transactions?
  • are there connected persons or market value rules?
  • does the six-or-more dwellings rule apply?
  • is the transaction notifiable even if the SDLT is nil?
  • is any relief available?

11. Memory point

Non-residential SDLT has its own rate table. Mixed-use transactions use the same non-residential rates. For leases, check both the premium and the rent NPV.
