Transfers of equity and non-sale transactions

Scope: SDLT applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

SDLT can apply when ownership changes even though there is no ordinary sale contract. Cash, assumed mortgage debt, released liabilities, exchanged land and special market-value rules can all affect the result.

Transfers between joint owners

Adding or removing an owner can involve an acquisition of a chargeable interest. Where the incoming owner pays cash or assumes responsibility for secured debt, that value can be chargeable consideration. Paragraph 8 Schedule 4 Finance Act 2003 contains special rules for the proportion of debt assumed when property moves into or out of joint ownership.

A change in legal title should be distinguished from a declaration that merely records an existing beneficial interest. The transfer deed, trust terms, mortgage consent, contributions and any payment between the parties must be reconciled.

Gifts, death and relationship breakdown

Gifts

A genuine gift with no chargeable consideration does not normally produce SDLT. The result changes if the recipient pays money, assumes mortgage debt or provides other value. Company market-value rules can also apply even where little or no consideration is given.

Death

Property passing under a will or intestacy is generally exempt under Schedule 3, but a later purchase from beneficiaries or variation involving consideration requires separate analysis.

Divorce or dissolution

Transfers made in connection with specified court orders or agreements relating to divorce, judicial separation or dissolution can be exempt. The statutory route and documents must be identified; transfers outside the exemption are tested under the ordinary rules.

Exchanges, partitions and trusts

On an exchange, each party acquires land and the special market-value rules may apply. A partition of jointly owned land can also contain chargeable consideration where one person gives money, debt relief or excess value to obtain a larger or different interest.

A bare trustee or nominee acquisition is generally looked through to the beneficial owner for SDLT, subject to statutory exceptions including the grant of a lease. For another settlement, the trustees are generally the purchasers and responsible for the return. Higher residential rates and the non-resident surcharge contain their own trust attribution rules.

Illustration

Alex transfers a half share of a mortgaged home to Sam. Sam pays no cash but becomes responsible for half of the outstanding mortgage.

  • Identify the beneficial share Sam acquires.
  • Calculate the debt treated as assumed under Schedule 4.
  • Test the residential rates, including higher-rate and non-resident rules.
  • Determine whether a return is required even if no tax is payable.

Applied analysis: Calling the transfer a gift does not remove the mortgage assumption from consideration. The title, mortgage and beneficial ownership documents must tell the same story.

Practical checklist

  • Identify the legal and beneficial ownership before and after.
  • Record cash, debt, indemnities and other value.
  • Check Schedule 3 exemptions and company market-value rules.
  • Apply trust, higher-rate and residence rules separately.
  • Coordinate SDLT with conveyancing, CGT and IHT advice without assuming their rules are identical.
