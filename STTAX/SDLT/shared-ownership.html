Shared ownership and staircasing

Scope: This page concerns qualifying shared-ownership arrangements in England and Northern Ireland and reflects law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

Shared ownership commonly involves a lease of a dwelling granted by an approved public body or housing provider, with the buyer paying a premium for an initial share and rent on the retained share. Schedule 9 Finance Act 2003 provides special SDLT rules where its conditions are met.

Two methods for the initial acquisition

Market-value election

The buyer can elect to calculate SDLT by reference to the market value of the dwelling at the initial transaction. This can accelerate tax but normally prevents further SDLT on later staircasing under the special regime. The election is irrevocable and should be modelled before the return is filed.

Paying in stages

Without a market-value election, the initial charge is based on the lease premium and, where applicable, the NPV of rent. A return can be required for a qualifying shared-ownership lease even where no SDLT is payable. Later staircasing is then considered under the special threshold rules.

First-time buyer relief may be available where the purchaser and transaction satisfy its current conditions. The relief, higher rates for additional dwellings and non-resident surcharge must each be tested rather than inferred from the shared-ownership label.

Staircasing

Staircasing is the acquisition of an additional share in the dwelling. Under the staged method, no further SDLT return or payment is generally required until the buyer’s share exceeds 80%. The transaction taking ownership above 80% and later acquisitions are then notifiable and the tax calculation takes account of the relevant linked staircasing transactions under Schedule 9.

Acquiring the freehold, extending the lease or departing from the qualifying shared-ownership terms can require a separate analysis. Retain the original lease, valuation, election and complete history of premiums and shares acquired.

Illustration

A first-time buyer acquires a 40% share in a qualifying dwelling and pays rent on the remaining share. They expect to buy further shares over several years.

  • Confirm that the lease and landlord meet Schedule 9 conditions.
  • Compare a market-value election with the staged method.
  • Calculate the initial premium and rent NPV separately.
  • Track each staircasing event and the point at which ownership exceeds 80%.

Applied analysis: The lowest initial charge is not necessarily the best lifetime outcome. The buyer should model the expected staircasing plan before choosing the initial return treatment.

Practical checklist

  • Verify the qualifying landlord, dwelling and lease terms.
  • Obtain the full market value and initial share valuation.
  • Model both statutory payment methods.
  • Test first-time buyer, higher-rate and non-resident rules separately.
  • Keep a cumulative staircasing and consideration schedule.
