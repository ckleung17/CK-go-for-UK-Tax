Options and pre-completion transactions

Scope: SDLT applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

Rights created before the final conveyance can themselves have SDLT consequences. Options and rights of pre-emption are land transactions, while assignments, subsales and other pre-completion arrangements are governed by section 45 and Schedule 2A Finance Act 2003.

Options and rights of pre-emption

An option gives a binding right to require a land transaction within its terms. A right of pre-emption generally restricts the owner from selling without first offering the property to the right-holder. The acquisition of either right can be chargeable on the amount paid for it.

Exercise is a separate land transaction. The option and exercise may be linked, and later tax can alter the amount due for the earlier transaction. A reservation deposit is not automatically an option; the rights created by the agreement must be examined.

Contracts and substantial performance

A contract to acquire land is normally charged on completion by conveyance. If it is substantially performed earlier, section 44 can treat a land transaction as occurring at that earlier time. Taking possession, paying a substantial amount or other statutory events can therefore accelerate the effective date, filing and payment.

The contract, conveyance, occupation, payment schedule and any nomination or assignment should be placed on one chronology before the return is prepared.

Pre-completion transactions

A pre-completion transaction arises where, before the original contract is completed or substantially performed, another agreement enables a different person to call for conveyance of all or part of the land. Schedule 2A distinguishes assignments of rights and free-standing transfers, including qualifying subsales.

  • The original purchaser can remain treated as entering into a land transaction.
  • The ultimate purchaser is charged under the special consideration rules.
  • Full or partial transferor relief may be available for qualifying assignments or subsales.
  • Relief must be claimed and can be restricted by the minimum-consideration and tax-advantage rules.

Novations, partial assignments, successive subsales and connected parties require specialist analysis; their commercial labels do not determine the statutory category.

Illustration

A contracts to buy development land from B. Before completion, A agrees that C may require B to convey the land directly to C.

  • Identify the original contract and whether it has already been substantially performed.
  • Classify the A–C agreement under Schedule 2A.
  • Calculate the land transactions and consideration attributed to A and C.
  • Test and claim any transferor relief.

Applied analysis: Direct conveyance from B to C does not by itself remove A from the SDLT analysis. All agreements, payments and connected arrangements must be reviewed together.

Practical checklist

  • Build a chronology from option or contract to final conveyance.
  • Identify possession, payment and substantial-performance events.
  • Read nomination, novation, assignment and subsale documents precisely.
  • Test linked transactions and section 75A separately.
  • File every required return and make relief claims expressly.
