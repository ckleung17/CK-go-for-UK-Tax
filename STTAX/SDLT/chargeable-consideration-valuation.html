Chargeable consideration and valuation

Scope: SDLT applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

Chargeable consideration is the money or money’s worth given directly or indirectly for a land transaction by the purchaser or a connected person. The contract price is the starting point, but it is not always the final taxable amount.

What can form part of the consideration?

Consideration can include cash, goods, works or services, release or assumption of debt, a lease premium, rent under a new lease and other value provided in return for the land interest. Value added tax is included where it is chargeable on an amount forming part of the consideration.

A purchaser’s own legal and survey costs are normally outside the consideration because they are paid for separate professional services. By contrast, a fee required by the seller as a condition of obtaining the property may form part of the consideration. Fixtures included with the land are normally part of the land transaction; genuinely removable chattels may be excluded only to the extent of a reasonable allocation.

Debt and mortgages

Assuming liability for an existing debt can be consideration under paragraph 8 of Schedule 4 Finance Act 2003. This includes a personal covenant, release of the transferor or an indemnity. For a transfer into or out of joint ownership, the statutory rules determine the proportion of debt treated as assumed; do not use the equity transferred as a substitute for that calculation.

Market value, exchanges and apportionment

Most transactions use the consideration actually given. Specific rules can substitute or bring in market value, including certain transfers to connected companies and exchanges involving a major interest in land. Each leg of an exchange is a separate acquisition and may be charged by reference to the greater of the statutory market-value amount and the ordinary consideration.

Where one amount relates to several assets, transactions or obligations, it must be apportioned on a just and reasonable basis. A contractual allocation is evidence but is not conclusive if it does not reflect the facts or value transferred.

Contingent, uncertain or unascertained amounts

A contingent amount depends on whether a future event occurs; an uncertain or unascertained amount cannot be fixed at the effective date. Section 51 Finance Act 2003 requires statutory assumptions when the initial return is prepared. When the contingency ceases or the amount becomes known, section 80 can require an amendment, repayment claim or further return using the rates in force at the original effective date.

Where qualifying contingent or uncertain consideration may become payable more than six months after the effective date, section 90 may allow an application to defer payment. The application is separate from the land transaction return and generally must reach HMRC within 30 days of the effective date. Tax on any amount already paid or payable within six months is not deferred.

Illustration

A buyer pays £600,000 for a commercial property, assumes a £200,000 secured debt and agrees to pay up to £100,000 more if planning permission is granted within two years.

  • Start with the cash and the debt assumed.
  • Apply the contingent-consideration rules to the possible additional payment.
  • Consider a section 90 deferral application for the qualifying later amount.
  • Revisit the return when the planning condition is resolved.

Applied analysis: The taxable amount and payment timing cannot be read from the cash price alone. The agreement, debt documents, valuation evidence and later event must reconcile with the SDLT returns.

Practical checklist

  • Identify everything given by the purchaser or a connected person.
  • Separate land, fixtures, genuine chattels and independent professional costs.
  • Check debt, exchange, connected-company and market-value rules.
  • Record valuation and apportionment evidence.
  • Diarise contingencies, ascertainment events, deferral applications and further returns.
