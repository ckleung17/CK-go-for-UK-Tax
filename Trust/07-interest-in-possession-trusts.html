Interest-in-possession trusts

Current enjoyment and future capital

HomeTrust Index

Overview

An interest-in-possession trust separates present enjoyment from future ownership of capital. A life tenant commonly has an immediate right to net income, occupation or use, while a remainderman holds the capital interest that follows. The instrument defines both interests and any trustee powers capable of changing them.

Income administration requires trustees to distinguish income receipts and expenses from capital transactions and costs. They must manage the fund fairly between successive beneficiaries without treating the life tenant as owner of the capital or the remainderman as entitled to current income.

The Inheritance Tax classification depends on the creation date, source and statutory category of the interest; the expression “life interest” is not enough. Trust-law rights should be established first and then tested separately for Income Tax, Capital Gains Tax and Inheritance Tax.

How an interest-in-possession trust operates

Life tenant

A life tenant is commonly entitled to net trust income for life or to occupy or use specified property during life. The right must be immediate rather than dependent on a future trustee decision. Trustees may hold legal title, manage investments and pay proper expenses, but they cannot accumulate or divert income that the instrument requires to be paid to the life tenant.

The expression does not mean that the beneficiary owns the capital. The instrument may give trustees powers to sell, reinvest, advance capital to other beneficiaries or terminate the interest. A power capable of defeating the interest does not necessarily prevent an interest in possession while the power remains unexercised, but a discretion to decide whether income is paid as it arises may do so.

Illustrative example: Trustees hold a share portfolio and must pay its net income to Leila for life. Leila can require the distributable income as it arises, but cannot demand the portfolio itself merely because she is the life tenant. If the trustees have a power to appoint capital to Leila’s children, that power must be considered separately from her present income right.

Remainderman

A remainderman holds the future capital interest that follows the life tenant’s prior interest. The right may be vested, so that an identified beneficiary already owns a future interest, or contingent on survival, age or another event. The deed or will must also be checked for substitutionary gifts, default beneficiaries and powers that can reduce or defeat the remainder.

A remainderman normally cannot require current income that belongs to the life tenant, but has an interest in proper preservation and administration of capital. Trustees must act impartially between the successive interests: this does not require identical financial outcomes, but it does require them to exercise powers for proper purposes and not favour one class simply because its needs are more immediate.

Illustrative example: A will pays income to Noah for life and gives the capital to Ava if she survives Noah, otherwise to Ava’s children. Ava has a contingent remainder because survival is required; her children hold substitutionary contingent interests. None is currently entitled to Noah’s income.

Income administration

Trustees collect income, identify receipts according to the instrument and trust law, deduct expenses properly chargeable against income and pay or account for the balance. Dividends, interest and rents may follow different tax computations, while capital proceeds, capital improvements and acquisition costs should not be treated as income merely because cash moves through the trust account.

The deed may allocate particular expenses, but statutory and general trust-law rules can also matter. Trustees should maintain separate income and capital ledgers, record apportionments, distinguish routine repairs from capital improvements and explain any management costs. A direct mandate of income to the beneficiary may simplify cash flow but does not remove the need for complete trust and tax records.

Illustrative example: Trustees receive £30,000 rent, pay £4,000 routine repairs and spend £20,000 replacing the property’s entire roof. The deed and applicable law must determine what reduces the life tenant’s current income and what is borne by capital; the bank account label does not decide the allocation.

IHT classification

For Inheritance Tax, the trust-law existence of an interest in possession is only the first stage. IHTA 1984 section 49 treats specified qualifying interests as property to which the beneficiary is beneficially entitled, but the legislation distinguishes older interests and particular post-21 March 2006 categories.

A post-2006 interest arising under a deceased person’s will or intestacy may qualify as an immediate post-death interest if section 49A’s conditions are met. Other estate-linked categories include specified disabled-person and transitional serial interests. A post-2006 lifetime interest that falls outside a qualifying category may instead sit within the relevant-property regime even though the beneficiary has a present income right.

Illustrative example: A spouse receives an immediate life interest under a will on death in 2026, while a friend received an otherwise similar income interest under a lifetime settlement created in 2024. The spouse’s interest may qualify as an immediate post-death interest; the friend’s cannot qualify merely because the wording and income entitlement look similar.

Practical issues and safeguards

A right to a fixed annual payment is not necessarily an interest in possession in all trust income. Read the charging language, powers and expense provisions rather than relying on “life interest” shorthand.

  • Read income, expense, accumulation, advancement and termination clauses together.
  • Separate the life tenant’s present right from the remainderman’s future interest.
  • Test the IHT category from creation circumstances and date, not the label life interest.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Fictional successive-interest file: Arun dies in June 2026. His will places a share portfolio and rental property on trust:

  1. Pay the net income to his spouse, Maya, for life and permit her to occupy the rental property rent-free.
  2. Allow trustees to appoint up to 40% of capital among Arun’s children for education, housing or serious financial need during Maya’s lifetime.
  3. On Maya’s death, divide the remaining capital between Arun’s children who survive her and reach age 30, with a substitutionary gift to the descendants of a child who dies first.
  4. Charge ordinary property expenses to income and structural improvements to capital, subject to the trustees’ powers and applicable law.

Arun also created a separate lifetime trust in 2024 paying portfolio income to his sister Nina for life, with capital to his nephews. The family assumes both income interests receive identical IHT treatment.

How the file is sorted out

  1. Read the dispositive provisions together. Map Maya’s income and occupation, the children’s default gifts, substitutionary interests and the appointment power.
  2. Identify when each right arises and ends. Record Arun’s death, Maya’s entitlement, every appointment, age and survival condition and the eventual termination of each interest.
  3. Separate income from capital. Classify receipts and expenses under the instrument and law before preparing tax computations or paying Maya.
  4. Overlay trustee powers. Determine how the 40% power can affect default remainders and whether conflicts or beneficiary needs require additional evidence.
  5. Classify each interest for IHT. Test Maya under sections 49 and 49A, and test Nina independently by reference to the 2024 lifetime creation.
  6. Model later events. An appointment, Maya’s death, a child reaching 30 or failure of a condition may change beneficial ownership and create separate CGT, IHT and reporting consequences.
  7. Reconcile records. Minutes, accounts, valuations, beneficiary statements, tax returns and Trust Registration Service information must reflect the same rights and events.

Legal guideposts

  • Pearson v IRC [1981] AC 753, discussed in HMRC CG36320, provides authoritative guidance on immediate entitlement to income or use and enjoyment when identifying an interest in possession.
  • IHTA 1984 section 49 identifies interests in possession treated as property to which a person is beneficially entitled for IHT purposes.
  • IHTA 1984 section 49A sets the statutory conditions for an immediate post-death interest.

Applied analysis: Maya may hold a present income and occupation interest qualifying as an immediate post-death interest, but the will, statutory conditions and valuation must be checked. The children combine contingent default remainders with discretionary eligibility under the appointment power. Nina’s similar-looking lifetime income interest requires a different IHT analysis. No payment, expense allocation or appointment should be reported until those layers are reconciled.
