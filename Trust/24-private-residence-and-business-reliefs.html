Private residence and business reliefs

Capital Gains Tax — relief through a beneficiary

Tax treatment

Study checkpoint: 2026/27. Trustees do not obtain relief merely because a beneficiary lives in trust property or operates a business. Section 225 Private Residence Relief requires occupation under an entitlement conferred by the settlement, and business-related reliefs impose their own beneficiary, asset, company and timing conditions.

The review must evidence the beneficiary’s entitlement to occupy and use as an only or main residence, while separately testing any hold-over interaction and relevant business reliefs.

The detailed analysis begins with Entitlement to occupy, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Connecting occupation, business use and relief

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Entitlement to occupy

The beneficiary must occupy under the settlement terms or a proper exercise of a dispositive power, not merely under trustees’ managerial tolerance.

That conclusion leads to the next question: only or main residence.

Only or main residence

The dwelling must genuinely be the beneficiary’s residence and the qualifying periods and restrictions must be computed.

That conclusion leads to the next question: hold-over interaction.

Hold-over interaction

Section 226A can restrict Private Residence Relief after certain earlier section 260 hold-over claims.

That conclusion leads to the next question: business reliefs.

Business reliefs

Business Asset Disposal Relief and gift relief require specific beneficiary and business connections; trust ownership alone is insufficient.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Charging rent does not necessarily prevent section 225 relief, while rent-free occupation does not guarantee it. The legal source of the occupation entitlement is critical.

  • Evidence the beneficiary’s legal occupation entitlement and actual main-residence facts.
  • Map acquisition, occupation, absence, letting and disposal periods.
  • Test hold-over restrictions and business relief conditions separately from occupation.

Illustration

Discretionary trustees permit a beneficiary to occupy a trust house under an express dispositive power for ten years. A section 225 claim may be possible on sale, but trustees must still test main-residence facts, non-qualifying use, final period, grounds and any earlier hold-over restriction.

Applied analysis: Locate the dispositive power granting occupation, assemble residence evidence and timeline, then calculate qualifying periods and test any earlier held-over gain restriction.
