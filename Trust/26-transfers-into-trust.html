IHT framework

Study checkpoint: chargeable events in 2026/27. A lifetime transfer into a relevant-property trust is normally an immediately chargeable transfer by the settlor, subject to exemptions and reliefs. The loss to the settlor’s estate, earlier chargeable transfers, who bears the tax and any retained benefit must be analysed before the deed and asset transfer are completed.

Compute the transfer of value, deduct the available nil-rate band, apply any lifetime rate and grossing, and retain the history needed if there is a death within seven years.

The detailed analysis begins with Transfer of value, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Computing a lifetime transfer into trust

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Transfer of value

IHT starts with the reduction in the settlor’s estate, not automatically the asset’s stand-alone value.

That conclusion leads to the next question: available nil-rate band.

Available nil-rate band

Chargeable transfers in the preceding seven years reduce the band available to the current transfer.

That conclusion leads to the next question: lifetime rate and grossing.

Lifetime rate and grossing

Tax on value within the charge is normally at the lifetime rate; if the settlor bears tax, grossing can increase the transfer of value.

That conclusion leads to the next question: death within seven years.

Death within seven years

The transfer is recalculated at death rates, with credit for lifetime tax and possible taper relief on tax, not on the transferred value.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not assume a transfer has no IHT because it is below £325,000 in isolation. Earlier chargeable transfers, related settlements, same-day additions, liabilities and relief restrictions can change the result.

  • Reconstruct seven-year transfer history, related settlements and same-day additions.
  • Value property and liabilities and test reliefs before applying the available nil-rate band.
  • Determine who bears tax and whether grossing, reporting or later death recalculation applies.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

A settlor transfers investments worth £500,000 into a new discretionary trust after making a £100,000 chargeable transfer four years earlier. The earlier transfer reduces the available nil-rate band. If the settlor pays the IHT personally, grossing must be considered because that payment further reduces the estate.

Applied analysis: Combine the earlier transfer with the new settlement, calculate the remaining band, test grossing if the settlor pays and retain the history for any later death review.
