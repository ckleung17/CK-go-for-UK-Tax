Overview

A will may create continuing trusts after estate administration, while intestacy legislation can impose statutory trusts. Family trusts can provide successive interests, protect younger or vulnerable beneficiaries and control the timing of capital. The personal-representative phase must be distinguished from the trustee phase.

Separate the estate administration period from the later will trust, then test any immediate post-death interest and provisions for younger beneficiaries under their specific rules.

The detailed analysis begins with Administration period, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

From estate administration to continuing trusts

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Administration period

Personal representatives collect assets, settle liabilities and establish the residue before trust property is fully ascertained.

That conclusion leads to the next question: will trust.

Will trust

The will defines the trustees, powers and interests, but tax provisions may treat events from death or from later administration dates differently.

That conclusion leads to the next question: immediate post-death interest.

Immediate post-death interest

A qualifying post-2006 interest in possession must satisfy statutory conditions; the label in the will is not conclusive.

That conclusion leads to the next question: younger beneficiaries.

Younger beneficiaries

Bereaved-minor and age 18-to-25 regimes have detailed statutory conditions and different IHT consequences.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not assume that every provision in a will is automatically outside the relevant-property regime or exempt from TRS. Statutory definitions, the two-year will-trust exclusion and later variations require separate tests.

  • Separate estate administration from the later trust and record the capacity change.
  • Classify each will interest for IHT and treat later variations as new events.
  • Test TRS exclusions, expiry and taxable-trust duties independently.

Illustration

A will gives the surviving spouse net income for life and capital to children on the spouse’s death. The estate first remains under administration; once the fund is established, the spouse’s interest must be tested as an immediate post-death interest and the trustees account for later income.

Applied analysis: Establish when residue passes to trustees, test the spouse’s interest and diarise reporting and the end of any TRS exclusion. The official sources below provide the starting points for checking that analysis against legislation and current HMRC interpretation.
