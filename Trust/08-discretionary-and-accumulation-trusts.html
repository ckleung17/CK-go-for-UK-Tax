Discretionary and accumulation trusts

Trust structures — flexible benefit and retained income

HomeTrust Index

Overview

A discretionary trust gives trustees a power to choose whether, when and how eligible beneficiaries receive income or capital. An accumulation power allows income to be retained and added to the fund. The deed, not the family’s informal expectations, defines the class, powers and limits.

The practical framework combines the discretionary class, any non-binding letter of wishes, the scope for accumulation, and the resulting tax framework.

The detailed analysis begins with Discretionary class, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

How discretion and accumulation operate

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Discretionary class

An object has a right to proper administration and genuine consideration, but no fixed share before an appointment.

That conclusion leads to the next question: letter of wishes.

Letter of wishes

May guide trustees but is normally non-binding; trustees must exercise their own judgment under the deed.

That conclusion leads to the next question: accumulation.

Accumulation

Income retained under a power may later become capital for trust-law purposes; accounts must preserve its origin and tax history.

That conclusion leads to the next question: tax framework.

Tax framework

Trustees may face special Income Tax rates and a tax-pool mechanism, while relevant-property IHT charges commonly require separate review.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

A beneficiary’s expectation, previous pattern of payments or inclusion in a letter of wishes does not create a fixed entitlement. Conversely, repeated predetermined decisions can raise questions about whether discretion was genuinely exercised.

  • Identify the whole class, defaults, accumulation period and each dispositive power.
  • Record circumstances, conflicts and purpose without treating wishes as binding.
  • Classify each payment legally and for tax before transferring value.
