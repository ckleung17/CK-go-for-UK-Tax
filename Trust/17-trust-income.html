Tax treatment

Study checkpoint: 2026/27. Trustees must preserve the legal and tax character of receipts. Savings, dividends, rents and trading profits follow different computation rules. For 2026/27, accumulation and discretionary trustees generally face 45% on non-dividend income and 39.35% on dividend-type income, subject to the statutory de minimis rules and special cases.

For each tax year, complete the source computation, check the de minimis amount, apply the special trust rates, and maintain the tax pool where relevant.

The detailed analysis begins with Source computation, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Building the trust Income Tax computation

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Source computation

Compute each source under its own rules before combining trust income; capital receipts and sale proceeds are not income merely because cash is received.

That conclusion leads to the next question: de minimis amount.

De minimis amount

HMRC guidance describes a normally £500 trust-income amount, divided across certain multiple settlements; if income exceeds the applicable amount, the exemption is lost rather than applied as an allowance.

That conclusion leads to the next question: special trust rates.

Special trust rates

Accumulation or discretionary income is generally charged at the special rates; interest-in-possession income uses different trustee rates.

That conclusion leads to the next question: tax pool.

Tax pool

Tracks specified trustee Income Tax available to support credits on discretionary income payments; it is a memorandum, not a bank account.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not deduct capital expenditure or private beneficiary costs as source expenses. Trust-management expenses have their own ordering and grossing rules and should not be confused with property or trading deductions.

  • Compute each income source before applying trust rates or management-expense rules.
  • Maintain the tax pool from tax actually available under the statutory rules.
  • Recheck current rates, de minimis treatment and beneficiary certificate requirements for the year.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

A discretionary trust receives £8,000 bank interest and £4,000 dividends in 2026/27. The trustees compute each source separately at the applicable special rate, then add qualifying tax to the pool. A later income payment must be grossed and supported by sufficient pool tax.

Applied analysis: Compute interest and dividends separately, identify allowable items, update the tax pool and test that any grossed distribution is fully supported before issuing the certificate.
