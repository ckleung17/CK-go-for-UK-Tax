Bare trusts and nominees

Absolute beneficial ownership behind legal title

HomeTrust Index

Overview

Under a bare trust, the trustee holds title for a beneficiary who is absolutely entitled to the property as against the trustee. The arrangement is commonly transparent for Income Tax and CGT, but legal capacity, land rules and Trust Registration Service requirements still need separate attention.

A bare-trust analysis starts with the beneficiary’s absolute entitlement and the trustee’s limited nominee function, before allocating Income Tax and Capital Gains Tax consequences.

The detailed analysis begins with Absolute entitlement, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

How a bare trust operates

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Absolute entitlement

The beneficiary’s rights are fixed; the trustee has no dispositive discretion over who benefits.

That conclusion leads to the next question: nominee function.

Nominee function

The registered holder acts on instructions and deals with third parties, but beneficial ownership remains with the principal or beneficiary.

That conclusion leads to the next question: income tax.

Income Tax

HMRC’s general guidance treats the beneficiary as responsible for tax on bare-trust income.

That conclusion leads to the next question: capital gains tax.

Capital Gains Tax

TCGA rules generally look through trustees where the beneficiary is absolutely entitled, subject to specific asset and statutory rules.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

A trust for a minor can involve absolute beneficial ownership even though the minor cannot personally give a valid receipt until reaching the relevant age. Check the instrument, governing law and facts rather than relying on the label “bare”.

  • Confirm absolute entitlement and whether any condition or discretion remains.
  • Trace income and proceeds to the beneficial owner and retain nominee instructions.
  • Separate a minor’s capacity to give receipt from beneficial ownership and taxation.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Shares are registered in a parent’s name but held absolutely for an adult child under a declaration of trust. Dividends and gains are normally analysed as the child’s, while the parent remains responsible for carrying out nominee instructions and maintaining evidence.

Applied analysis: Verify the declaration and the adult child’s unrestricted rights, report income and gains consistently, and preserve the parent’s nominee records.
