Bare trusts and nominees

Absolute beneficial ownership behind legal title

HomeTrust Index

Overview

Under a bare trust, a trustee or nominee holds legal title while the beneficiary has the fixed beneficial ownership. The arrangement may be straightforward, but the label “bare trust” is not enough. Ask four direct questions:

  1. Is the beneficiary absolutely entitled to the property? Check whether anyone else has a beneficial interest or whether a condition, power or liability prevents the beneficiary from requiring the asset.
  2. What is the nominee allowed to do? A nominee normally follows authorised instructions and deals with third parties rather than choosing who benefits.
  3. Who reports the income? The beneficiary is generally taxed on income from the underlying property, subject to any separate attribution or source rules.
  4. Who reports a disposal or gain? The CGT legislation generally looks through a bare trustee to the absolutely entitled beneficiary.

Legal title, beneficial ownership, Income Tax and Capital Gains Tax must still be recorded separately so that the nominee’s documents and the beneficiary’s returns describe the same events.

How a bare trust operates

Absolute entitlement

Absolute entitlement means that, as against the trustee, the beneficiary owns the whole beneficial interest in the relevant property. The trustee has no power to select a different beneficiary or alter the beneficial share. An adult beneficiary with capacity can ordinarily direct how the property is dealt with and, subject to proper liabilities and transfer formalities, require legal title to be transferred.

The test is asset-specific and must account for every interest and power. A survivorship condition, gift over, discretionary appointment power, another beneficiary’s share or unresolved administration may prevent present absolute entitlement. Several adult beneficiaries may be collectively entitled to the whole fund, but one beneficiary is not necessarily absolutely entitled to a particular asset merely because their fraction of the total fund is fixed.

Illustrative example: A nominee holds exactly 2,000 identified shares for Amira, an adult with capacity, under an irrevocable declaration giving no other person an interest and no dispositive power to the nominee. Amira can direct a sale or transfer, subject to company procedures and any proper nominee expenses. By contrast, a gift of the shares to Amira at 30 with a gift over if she dies earlier is not presently an unrestricted bare entitlement.

Nominee function

A nominee is the registered or contractual holder through whom the beneficial owner deals with a company, bank, Land Registry or other third party. The nominee may execute documents, receive cash, pass on information and carry out authorised instructions. Those functions do not give the nominee beneficial ownership or an unrestricted discretion to use the asset for personal purposes.

The arrangement needs clear authority, segregation and records. The nominee should identify the beneficial owner, reconcile each asset and receipt, retain instructions, disclose conflicts, account promptly and observe anti-money-laundering, registration and reporting duties. Security rights, liens or contractual restrictions should be documented because they may limit what can be transferred immediately without changing who ultimately owns the economic benefit.

Illustrative example: A nominee company is registered as shareholder for Jonah and receives dividends into a client account. It may submit voting instructions and transfer forms for Jonah, but it must not vote for its own advantage, lend the shares to another client or retain dividends unless the agreement and law authorise that action.

Income Tax

For a genuine bare trust, Income Tax normally follows the beneficiary’s beneficial ownership of the underlying asset. Interest, dividends, rent or trading income retain their source character and are generally included in the beneficiary’s tax position whether paid directly, retained temporarily by the nominee or transferred later. The nominee should provide statements sufficient for the beneficiary to report the correct gross income, tax deducted and expenses.

This treatment does not disapply other Income Tax rules. Parental settlements for minor children, settlements legislation, non-residence, property-income deductions, savings and dividend rules and reporting obligations must be considered independently. The person who funded the arrangement and the beneficiary’s age, residence and entitlement can therefore change the final reporting position.

Illustrative example: Shares held by a nominee for an adult beneficiary produce £6,000 of dividends. The nominee retains the cash pending investment instructions, but retention does not normally turn the dividends into the nominee’s income. The beneficiary records the dividend income for the relevant tax year, subject to their own circumstances.

Capital Gains Tax

TCGA 1992 section 60 generally treats acts of a trustee in relation to property as acts of the person absolutely entitled as against the trustee. A sale by the nominee is therefore normally analysed as the beneficiary’s disposal, using the beneficiary’s acquisition history, consideration, allowable costs, losses and available reliefs rather than computing a separate economic gain for the nominee.

A distinction is needed between property that has always been held bare and a settlement in which a beneficiary later becomes absolutely entitled. The later event can itself trigger the statutory disposal and reacquisition rules in section 71; a subsequent conveyance of legal title may then be treated differently. Identify the exact date absolute entitlement arose before calculating any later sale.

Illustrative example: A nominee sells Amira’s 2,000 shares for £90,000 after receiving her instruction. Amira, not the nominee, normally computes the gain using her base cost and disposal expenses. If the shares had first emerged from a discretionary settlement when appointed absolutely to Amira, the earlier appointment date would require its own section 71 analysis.

Practical issues and safeguards

A trust for a minor can involve absolute beneficial ownership even though the minor cannot personally give a valid receipt until reaching the relevant age. Check the instrument, governing law and facts rather than relying on the label “bare”.

  • Confirm absolute entitlement and whether any condition or discretion remains.
  • Trace income and proceeds to the beneficial owner and retain nominee instructions.
  • Separate a minor’s capacity to give receipt from beneficial ownership and taxation.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Fictional nominee portfolio: Northbridge Nominees Ltd holds assets connected with the Rahman family:

  1. 2,000 quoted shares are separately identified for adult Amira under an irrevocable declaration. She may direct sale or transfer at any time.
  2. £120,000 cash is recorded as belonging 60% to Amira and 40% to adult Bilal, but the money is held in one pooled client account and has not been appropriated to separate investments.
  3. A rental flat is registered in the nominee’s name for 17-year-old Cara. Her father supplied the purchase price, and the declaration says Cara is absolutely entitled.
  4. 1,000 shares are to pass to Daniel at age 30, but to his sister if he dies before 30. Daniel is 24.
  5. A further investment fund may be distributed among any of four grandchildren as the nominee’s directors consider appropriate for education.

The nominee plans to sell Amira’s shares, reinvest the pooled cash, pay the flat’s rent into Cara’s account and describe every arrangement as a bare trust.

How the file is sorted out

  1. Identify each asset and fund separately. Do not apply one label to the whole nominee account.
  2. Map every beneficial interest and condition. Record fixed shares, survivorship or age conditions, gifts over, discretionary powers, liens and proper liabilities.
  3. Test control against legal capacity. Distinguish beneficial ownership from the practical ability of a minor or other person to execute a transfer.
  4. Determine when absolute entitlement arose. Separate assets held bare from acquisition from assets appointed out of another settlement at a later date.
  5. Allocate income by source and taxpayer. Preserve dividends, interest and rent, then apply parental or settlor-attribution rules where relevant.
  6. Compute each CGT event. Identify whether section 60 looks through the nominee, whether section 71 applied earlier, and whether a later legal-title transfer is merely consequential.
  7. Reconcile compliance. Nominee statements, beneficiary tax returns, Trust Registration Service records, bank mandates and asset registers must agree.

Legal guideposts

  • Saunders v Vautier (1841) 4 Beav 115, discussed in HMRC CG34430, supports the principle that beneficiaries who are adult, capable and together absolutely entitled can require the trust property to be transferred and the trust brought to an end.
  • TCGA 1992 section 60 provides the principal CGT look-through treatment where a person is absolutely entitled as against the trustee.
  • TCGA 1992 section 71 addresses the occasion when a beneficiary becomes absolutely entitled to settled property.

Applied analysis: Only Amira’s identified shares are clearly conventional bare-trust property on the stated facts. The pooled cash requires asset-level analysis, Cara’s flat raises separate minor-child attribution, Daniel remains contingent and the education fund is discretionary. The nominee should not sell, reinvest or report the assets under a single tax treatment until every interest, instruction and commencement date has been reconciled.
