Integrated framework

Study checkpoint: law and guidance checked to 17 July 2026. A trust can hold shares in a trading company, a direct business, partnership interests, investment property or a beneficiary’s home. The trustees’ legal powers and fiduciary process must be coordinated with Income Tax, CGT, IHT, SDLT, VAT, financing and company or partnership law.

Start with the legal ownership structure, then analyse income and expenses, later capital transactions, and possible IHT reliefs for each asset and activity.

Analysing trust-owned businesses and property

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Ownership structure

Distinguish a business operated by trustees from shares, partnership interests or land merely held as investments.

That conclusion leads to the next question: income and expenses.

Income and expenses

Trading profits, dividends, rents and finance costs have separate source rules and may belong to different trust funds or beneficiaries.

That conclusion leads to the next question: capital transactions.

Capital transactions

Sales, incorporations, appointments and reorganisations can trigger CGT, SDLT and valuation rules even without external cash.

That conclusion leads to the next question: iht reliefs.

IHT reliefs

Business and agricultural relief depend on current statutory conditions, ownership periods, activity and post-6 April 2026 limits.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

A business described as “family trading” may contain substantial investment activity or excepted assets. Relief availability and rates changed from 6 April 2026 and require current, asset-specific review.

  • Separate trading, investment and excepted assets using current operational evidence.
  • Obtain asset-specific values and identify debt or consideration assumed on transfer.
  • Model CGT, IHT, SDLT, Income Tax and company consequences before appointment.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Discretionary trustees own rental flats and all shares in a family trading company. Appointing a flat to a beneficiary differs from appointing company shares: each needs market valuation, CGT and IHT review, while land may create SDLT consequences if debt or consideration is assumed.

Applied analysis: Value the flat and shares separately, analyse debt and land consideration, test trading-company relief asset by asset and compare the full tax cost of each appointment.
