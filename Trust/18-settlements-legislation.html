When one person has several roles

Trust-law roles and Income Tax charging rules answer different questions. A settlor may also be trustee or beneficiary, but that combination does not alone determine the charge. Identify who provided the property, who can benefit, which powers are retained or exercisable, and what income or benefit actually arises.

If the settlor or their spouse or civil partner can benefit, the retained-interest rules may attribute income to the settlor. Separate rules address income paid to or for a settlor’s minor unmarried child. A trustee who is also a beneficiary must distinguish fiduciary decisions from personal receipts and preserve resolutions, accounts and tax certificates showing the capacity in which each amount was paid.

Apply the rules source by source and tax year by tax year. Do not assume that a deed’s description, the absence of a cash distribution, or the use of an independent co-trustee prevents attribution.

Practical issues and safeguards

Changing the name on an asset or routing income through relatives does not decide who is the settlor. Reciprocal arrangements, indirect provision and retained benefits require examination of the whole arrangement.

  • Identify every provider of value, including indirect and reciprocal arrangements.
  • Map all routes by which the settlor, spouse or minor child may benefit.
  • Apply each attribution provision and exception by source and tax year.
