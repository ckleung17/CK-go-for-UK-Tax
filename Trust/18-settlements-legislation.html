Tax treatment

Study checkpoint: 2026/27. ITTOIA 2005 Part 5 Chapter 5 can treat settlement income as the settlor’s. The analysis begins with whether there is a settlement and who provided value, then tests retained interests, payments for minor children and other statutory provisions and exceptions.

The review identifies the settlement and settlor, tests any retained interest, and then applies the separate rules for minor children and spouse gifts.

The detailed analysis begins with Settlement and settlor, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Applying the settlements legislation

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Settlement and settlor

The statutory concepts extend beyond formal trusts and can include bounteous arrangements. Identify the real provider of property or value.

That conclusion leads to the next question: retained interest.

Retained interest

Section 624 can attribute income where settlement property or income may benefit the settlor or their spouse or civil partner, subject to exceptions.

That conclusion leads to the next question: minor children.

Minor children

Section 629 can attribute income to a parent-settlor where it belongs to or is paid or applied for an unmarried minor child; the £100 threshold must be tested per parent and child.

That conclusion leads to the next question: spouse gifts.

Spouse gifts

The outright-gift exception has conditions and does not protect a gift wholly or substantially of a right to income.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

When one person has several roles

Trust-law roles and Income Tax charging rules answer different questions. A settlor may also be trustee or beneficiary, but that combination does not alone determine the charge. Identify who provided the property, who can benefit, which powers are retained or exercisable, and what income or benefit actually arises.

If the settlor or their spouse or civil partner can benefit, the retained-interest rules may attribute income to the settlor. Separate rules address income paid to or for a settlor’s minor unmarried child. A trustee who is also a beneficiary must distinguish fiduciary decisions from personal receipts and preserve resolutions, accounts and tax certificates showing the capacity in which each amount was paid.

Apply the rules source by source and tax year by tax year. Do not assume that a deed’s description, the absence of a cash distribution, or the use of an independent co-trustee prevents attribution.

Practical issues and safeguards

Changing the name on an asset or routing income through relatives does not decide who is the settlor. Reciprocal arrangements, indirect provision and retained benefits require examination of the whole arrangement.

  • Identify every provider of value, including indirect and reciprocal arrangements.
  • Map all routes by which the settlor, spouse or minor child may benefit.
  • Apply each attribution provision and exception by source and tax year.

Illustration

A parent places dividend-paying shares in a bare trust for a 12-year-old child. Although the child is beneficially entitled, section 629 may treat income exceeding the statutory threshold as the parent’s. A gift from a grandparent requires a different settlor analysis.

Applied analysis: Trace who provided the shares, confirm the child’s age and income amount, apply the parental rule, and contrast the result if the grandparent was the true provider.
