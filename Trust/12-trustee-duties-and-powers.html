Trustee duties and powers

Trust administration — authority, care and loyalty

Overview

Trustees must distinguish duties they must perform from powers they may exercise. The deed is the starting point, supplemented by statute and fiduciary law. Investment, delegation, maintenance and advancement decisions require proper purpose, relevant information and attention to conflicts.

Trustee conduct should be reviewed through the duty of care, the scope of investment and delegation, and the overriding requirement of fiduciary loyalty.

The detailed analysis begins with Duty of care, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Connecting trustee duties and powers

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Duty of care

Trustee Act 2000 applies a statutory standard to specified functions, adjusted for a trustee’s knowledge, experience and professional status.

That conclusion leads to the next question: investment.

Investment

Trustees generally consider suitability and diversification and obtain proper advice unless reasonably unnecessary, subject to the deed and statutory regime.

That conclusion leads to the next question: delegation.

Delegation

Permitted functions, written policy, agent selection and continuing review must be distinguished from an abdication of responsibility.

That conclusion leads to the next question: fiduciary loyalty.

Fiduciary loyalty

Trustees must not misuse powers, profit without authority or allow personal interests to displace the trust’s purposes.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Who administers the trust?

The trustees are responsible for administration. They hold or control the trust property, follow the trust instrument and applicable law, keep the property separate, maintain accounts and records, manage investments and liabilities, make properly authorised distributions, deal with registration and tax compliance, and provide information or accounts when legally required.

Co-trustees normally exercise their powers collectively unless the trust instrument or legislation permits another method. A settlor, beneficiary, protector, solicitor or accountant cannot direct the trustees merely because they occupy that role. A reserved consent or appointment power must be found in the deed or law and used for its proper purpose.

Trustees may obtain legal, tax and investment advice and may appoint agents for functions that can lawfully be delegated. Trustee Act 2000 section 11 restricts delegation of specified core decisions, while sections 22 and 23 require appropriate review and intervention. Delegation therefore does not mean abdication: trustees must choose suitable agents, agree appropriate terms, monitor performance and remain accountable for their own compliance with the applicable duty of care.

Practical issues and safeguards

Having a power does not mean every exercise is valid. A decision can fail because trustees considered irrelevant factors, ignored relevant ones, acted for an improper purpose or failed to manage a conflict.

  • Locate the exact power and each care, advice or consent requirement.
  • Record relevant factors, alternatives, conflicts, liquidity and beneficiary interests.
  • For agents, document selection, terms, monitoring and retained trustee responsibility.
