Tax treatment

Study checkpoint: 2026/27. A payment to a beneficiary must first be classified under the deed and accounts. Interest-in-possession income, discretionary income payments, capital appointments, loans and benefits can have different tax consequences. Form R185 communicates specified trust income and tax information but does not determine the legal character.

Classify each receipt as IIP income, a discretionary income payment, a capital payment, or an amount producing a repayment position before deciding who reports it.

The detailed analysis begins with IIP income, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Classifying beneficiary receipts

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

IIP income

The beneficiary is generally taxed on their trust-income entitlement with credit for tax paid by trustees, subject to expenses and mandates.

That conclusion leads to the next question: discretionary income payment.

Discretionary income payment

For a UK-resident non-settlor-interested trust, the payment carries a trust-rate credit and is grossed for the beneficiary’s return.

That conclusion leads to the next question: capital payment.

Capital payment

Not automatically Income Tax-free: non-resident trust matching, settlor rules or anti-avoidance provisions may apply.

That conclusion leads to the next question: repayment position.

Repayment position

A beneficiary’s personal rate may permit repayment of some credit in ordinary cases, but ring-fenced credits in settlor-interested or non-resident cases can behave differently.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not choose “income” or “capital” after the event merely to obtain a preferred tax outcome. The power exercised, accounting source, trust law and applicable anti-avoidance rules all matter.

  • Identify the power exercised and whether the payment uses income, capital or mixed funds.
  • Reconcile discretionary income with the tax pool and issue accurate beneficiary information.
  • Test capital payments separately for gains matching, benefits and anti-avoidance rules.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

Trustees pay £5,500 net as a discretionary income distribution from a UK-resident trust. At a 45% trust rate, the beneficiary’s gross trust income is £10,000 with £4,500 tax treated as paid, provided the payment and pool are correctly supported. A capital appointment of £5,500 would require a different analysis.

Applied analysis: Verify the income resolution and available pool tax before grossing the payment; if capital is appointed instead, document the different legal source and matching analysis.
