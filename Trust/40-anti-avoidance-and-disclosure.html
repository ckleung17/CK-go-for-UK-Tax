Integrated framework

Study checkpoint: law and guidance checked to 17 July 2026. Trust arrangements can engage specific attribution rules, targeted anti-avoidance provisions, the transfer-of-assets-abroad code, employment-related Part 7A, the General Anti-Abuse Rule and disclosure regimes. Each provision has its own trigger, taxpayer, defence, counteraction and reporting process.

Work from the facts through the specific charging rules, then test the transfer of assets abroad, Part 7A employment income, and relevant disclosure and GAAR as distinct statutory or procedural regimes.

The detailed analysis begins with Specific charging rules, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Applying charging and disclosure regimes in order

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Specific charging rules

Settlements legislation and trust-gain matching often define the primary tax charge; they should be applied before assuming a general anti-avoidance rule.

That conclusion leads to the next question: transfer of assets abroad.

Transfer of assets abroad

Can charge UK-resident individuals connected with transfers producing income abroad, subject to detailed benefit, motive and EU-related provisions.

That conclusion leads to the next question: part 7a employment income.

Part 7A employment income

Third-party arrangements involving employee benefit trusts can create employment charges when relevant steps occur.

That conclusion leads to the next question: disclosure and gaar.

Disclosure and GAAR

DOTAS, DAC6 where applicable, CRS/FATCA and GAAR processes are distinct; disclosure does not determine whether tax is due.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

A trust deed and legal transfer do not establish tax effectiveness. Equally, tax planning is not automatically abusive. Conclusions require the exact statutory test, evidence of purpose and current specialist review.

  • Map all parties, steps, funding, benefits, purposes and circular flows.
  • Apply specific charging rules before broader anti-avoidance and disclosure regimes.
  • Preserve commercial evidence and obtain current specialist review before implementation or correction.

Illustration

An owner-managed company contributes funds to an offshore employee trust, which lends to a director’s family. Analysis begins with employment and Part 7A rules, then corporation tax, settlements, transfer-of-assets, IHT and disclosure—not with an assumption that the loan is tax-free.

Applied analysis: Trace the contribution, loan and family benefit, apply Part 7A and other specific regimes in order, then assess disclosure and GAAR with documented purpose and advice.
