Overview

A breach occurs when a trustee fails to comply with the trust terms, statute or fiduciary duties. The response depends on the duty, transaction, causation, loss or unauthorised gain, available property and possible defences. Tax correction and civil remedies may proceed on different tracks.

The remedial analysis may involve restoration and compensation, an account of profits, tracing and proprietary claims, and any available relief and protection; the appropriate route depends on the loss, gain and property involved.

The detailed analysis begins with Restoration and compensation, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Connecting breach, loss and remedy

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Restoration and compensation

The court may require restoration of the fund or equitable compensation, with the measure depending on the duty and causal analysis.

That conclusion leads to the next question: account of profits.

Account of profits

An unauthorised fiduciary profit may be recoverable even where the trust cannot show conventional loss.

That conclusion leads to the next question: tracing and proprietary claims.

Tracing and proprietary claims

Beneficiaries may seek substituted property or value, subject to evidential rules and third-party defences.

That conclusion leads to the next question: relief and protection.

Relief and protection

Consent, release, limitation, exclusion clauses, court relief and trustee insurance require careful fact and legal analysis.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Do not treat every poor investment outcome as breach, or every breach as producing the same measure of loss. Professional advice is essential before trustees admit liability or beneficiaries release claims.

  • Identify duty, breach, causation, loss and defences before selecting a remedy.
  • Preserve records, valuations, tracing evidence and limitation dates without premature admissions.
  • Review tax corrections, insurance, indemnities, releases and court approval separately.

The illustration below applies those safeguards to a concrete fact pattern and shows how the concepts work together.

Illustration

A trustee sells an asset to a connected company below market value without authority. Analysis may include setting aside the transaction, restoring value, accounting for profit, tracing proceeds and correcting tax returns; each remedy needs its own evidence.

Applied analysis: Obtain an independent valuation, trace proceeds and connected benefits, quantify restoration and profit claims, then correct tax reporting consistently with the legal remedy.
