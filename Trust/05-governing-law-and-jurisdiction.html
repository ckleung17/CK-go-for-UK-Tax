Overview

A trust can connect with several territories through its settlor, trustees, beneficiaries, assets, administration and governing-law clause. Governing law, court jurisdiction, recognition and tax residence are separate questions and may point to different countries.

Keep the trust’s governing law, its recognition in another country, the available forum, and its tax residence as four distinct questions.

The detailed analysis begins with Governing law, which provides the foundation for the concepts that follow.

Connecting governing law, forum and tax

This topic turns on four linked questions. Resolve them in sequence using the governing instrument, event chronology and supporting evidence, because the answer to one question can alter the next.

Governing law

Usually identified from the instrument and relevant choice-of-law rules; it governs matters such as validity, construction and administration.

That conclusion leads to the next question: recognition.

Recognition

The UK gives effect to the Hague Trusts Convention through the Recognition of Trusts Act 1987, subject to its scope and safeguards.

That conclusion leads to the next question: forum.

Forum

A governing-law clause does not always determine which court may hear a dispute or where enforcement is practical.

That conclusion leads to the next question: tax residence.

Tax residence

Trustee residence and statutory tax rules are not determined solely by the deed’s governing law or the location of a bank account.

Taken together, these concepts determine which records, checks and safeguards are needed in practice.

Practical issues and safeguards

Cross-border changes of trustee or governing law can create tax events and compliance duties. Obtain coordinated advice in every material jurisdiction before implementing a migration or appointment.

  • Tabulate governing law, trustee residence, asset situs, beneficiary residence and dispute forum.
  • Review trustee or governing-law changes before execution for migration and compliance effects.
  • Obtain coordinated advice where foreign land, treaties or local reporting rules are material.
