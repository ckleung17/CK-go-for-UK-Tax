Governing law and jurisdiction

Cross-border legal analysis

HomeTrust Index

Overview

A trust may be connected with several countries through its settlor, trustees, beneficiaries, assets and administration. Do not assume that one country provides every answer. Ask four separate questions:

  1. Which country’s law governs the trust? This law normally determines how the trust is interpreted and administered.
  2. Will another country recognise the trust? A country may need to decide whether it accepts the trustees’ ownership and the beneficiaries’ separate rights.
  3. Which country’s courts can hear a dispute? The appropriate court may be in a different country from the governing law.
  4. Where is the trust resident for tax? Tax residence follows the relevant statutory and treaty tests, not simply the law named in the deed.

For example, a trust may be governed by Jersey law, recognised in England, involved in proceedings before an English court and treated as UK-resident for a particular tax. Each conclusion requires its own test.

Connecting governing law, forum and tax

Governing law

Governing law is the system of trust law used to determine matters such as validity, interpretation, administration, trustee powers and the nature of beneficial interests. For a trust within the Hague Trusts Convention, an effective express choice is the starting point; if there is no effective choice, the Convention uses the law with which the trust is most closely connected. The precise Convention scope and the Recognition of Trusts Act 1987 must be checked.

A governing-law clause does not make every connected issue subject to that law. Transfer formalities, title to registered property, insolvency, succession, matrimonial rights, creditor protection, court procedure and taxation may engage mandatory rules of another country. The deed should therefore be read alongside the law of each place where property, parties or enforcement are located.

Illustrative example: An English-law deed appoints Swiss trustees to hold shares registered in Germany. English trust law may govern construction and administration, while Swiss regulatory questions and German share-transfer or registration rules require separate local analysis.

Recognition

Recognition asks whether another legal system will acknowledge the trust relationship and give effect to the separation between trustee title and beneficial interests. Under the Convention framework implemented in the United Kingdom, recognition can include treating trust property as a separate fund and recognising the trustee’s capacity to act, sue or be sued in that role.

Recognition is not the same as allowing every trust term to override local law. The Convention preserves specified mandatory rules, including rules in areas such as succession, matrimonial property, transfer of title, insolvency and protection of creditors or good-faith third parties. Local advice must establish both whether the trust is recognised and what consequences recognition produces for the particular asset or dispute.

Illustrative example: Jersey trustees claim ownership of an English asset under a Jersey-law trust. An English court may recognise the trust relationship under the 1987 Act, but must still apply any relevant mandatory English property, insolvency or third-party rules.

Forum

Forum concerns which court can and should hear a dispute or exercise supervisory powers. It is distinct from governing law: a court may apply foreign trust law, and the courts of the governing-law country may not be the only courts with jurisdiction. Relevant connections can include the wording of a forum clause, trustee and beneficiary locations, the place of administration, asset location, the remedy sought and whether a judgment can be enforced.

A trust forum clause deserves careful construction and substantial weight, but it is not analysed automatically as if every beneficiary had negotiated a commercial contract. The court may need to consider its supervisory responsibility, the interests of beneficiaries and the practical administration of the trust. Parallel proceedings and inconsistent orders should be identified before litigation or a contentious trustee decision begins.

Illustrative example: A Jersey-law deed states that Jersey is the forum for administration, but an English beneficiary seeks disclosure from a trustee living in London concerning English proceedings. The clause is important, yet jurisdiction, discretion to stay proceedings and enforceability must be analysed rather than assumed from the governing-law label.

Tax residence

Tax residence is determined under the legislation applicable to the particular tax and period, not by the deed’s governing law, the currency of the accounts or the location of one bank account. For UK Income Tax and Capital Gains Tax, current trustee-residence rules examine the composition and residence of the trustee body and, in specified mixed-trustee cases, additional settlor-related conditions. Trustee changes during a tax year have their own rules.

Separate questions may arise under a double tax treaty, including whether a trust or trustee body is treated as resident in both territories and where effective management occurs. The actual decision-making record can therefore matter as much as formal appointments. Residence must also be kept separate from the post-6 April 2025 long-term-residence tests used for some Inheritance Tax questions.

Illustrative example: A Jersey-law trust has a Jersey corporate trustee and a UK-resident individual trustee, while investment proposals are prepared offshore but routinely approved during meetings in London. The UK statutory result requires the full trustee and settlor history; any treaty analysis additionally requires evidence of where substantive decisions were actually made.

Practical issues and safeguards

Cross-border changes of trustee or governing law can create tax events and compliance duties. Obtain coordinated advice in every material jurisdiction before implementing a migration or appointment.

  • Tabulate governing law, trustee residence, asset situs, beneficiary residence and dispute forum.
  • Review trustee or governing-law changes before execution for migration and compliance effects.
  • Obtain coordinated advice where foreign land, treaties or local reporting rules are material.

Illustration

Fictional cross-border file: A trust was created in 2018 by a Hong Kong-resident settlor. The deed selects Jersey law and states that the courts of Jersey are the forum for administration. The current trustees are a Jersey trust company and an individual who moved to London in 2025. The trust owns shares in a UK company, a villa in France and investments held through a Swiss custodian.

In 2026 the trustees approve a substantial appointment to a UK-resident beneficiary during a meeting held in London. Another beneficiary living in England challenges the decision and seeks trust documents there. A French creditor claims rights against the villa, while the trustees assume that the Jersey clause resolves every question.

How the file is resolved

  1. Build one chronology. Record creation, additions, trustee appointments, residence changes, meetings, asset acquisitions, the appointment and commencement of each dispute.
  2. Separate each legal question. Prepare distinct schedules for governing law, recognition, forum, asset-situs rules, trustee authority, tax residence and beneficiary taxation.
  3. Read the clauses precisely. Determine whether the deed makes an effective governing-law choice and how the forum clause is expressed; do not paraphrase one as the other.
  4. Obtain local evidence and advice. Confirm Jersey administration law, French property and creditor rules, English procedural issues and Swiss custody constraints before implementation.
  5. Reconstruct decision-making. Identify who proposed, considered and approved the appointment, where each person acted and whether the minutes reflect the substance.
  6. Coordinate remedies and reporting. Avoid distributions, registrations or court steps in one country that conflict with preservation orders, trustee duties or reporting in another.

Case-law guideposts

  • Akers v Samba Financial Group [2017] UKSC 6 illustrates the interaction between Convention recognition and mandatory property or insolvency rules; recognition does not make the chosen trust law decisive for every issue.
  • Crociani v Crociani [2014] UKPC 40 addresses construction and treatment of a trust forum clause and explains why the trust context is not identical to an arm’s-length contractual jurisdiction bargain.
  • Smallwood v HMRC [2010] EWCA Civ 778 demonstrates that treaty residence and effective management require examination of the real decision-making arrangements rather than formal offshore steps alone.

Applied analysis: Jersey law may govern important trust questions, but the English recognition and forum issues, French villa, UK trustee residence, possible treaty position and beneficiary appointment each require their own legal and factual test. The trustees should pause implementation until the chronology, local-law advice, decision record and tax analysis have been reconciled.
