Private Residence Relief (PRR)

Capital Gains Tax relief for an individual’s only or main residence

Private Residence Relief can exempt all or part of a gain when an individual disposes of a dwelling that has been their only or main residence. The amount depends on how the property was used and which periods of ownership qualify.

Core conditions

Relief under sections 222 and 223 of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 is available to individuals. The dwelling must have been occupied as the individual’s residence; ownership alone is not enough. The permitted area normally includes the dwelling and up to 0.5 hectares of garden and grounds, although a larger area may qualify where it is required for the reasonable enjoyment of the dwelling having regard to its size and character.

  • Full relief may be available where the property was the only or main residence throughout the ownership period and no restriction applies.
  • Partial relief may be available where only part of the ownership period or part of the property qualifies.
  • If an individual has more than one residence, the facts and any valid main-residence nomination must be considered.
  • Spouses and civil partners living together can normally have only one main residence between them for PRR purposes.

Qualifying periods

Actual occupation as the only or main residence normally qualifies. Certain periods of absence can also be treated as occupation if the statutory conditions are met, including conditions about occupation before or after the absence. These rules are fact-sensitive and should not be assumed to apply merely because the property was retained as a home.

For most disposals, the final nine months of ownership qualify if the dwelling has been the individual’s only or main residence at some point. A 36-month final period can apply in specified circumstances involving disability or long-term residential care. The final period must not be counted twice if it overlaps another qualifying period.

Letting Relief is now generally limited to a period when the owner shared occupation of the home with the tenant. Exclusive business use, non-residential use, development motives and the size or use of the grounds can also restrict or prevent relief.

Homes outside the UK

PRR is not confined to UK property. However, for tax years from 6 April 2015, a dwelling in a territory where the individual is not tax resident is subject to the residence-day condition in section 222C. Broadly, the individual (or their spouse or civil partner) must spend at least 90 qualifying days in dwellings in that territory during the tax year. Proportionate rules apply where an interest is held for only part of a tax year.

A UK-resident individual is normally within UK Capital Gains Tax on gains from overseas property. Any foreign tax and possible double-taxation relief must be considered separately. Acquisition cost, disposal proceeds and allowable expenditure must be translated into sterling at the rates applicable on the relevant dates.

How partial relief is calculated

Where the whole property qualifies but only part of the ownership period is relieved, PRR is broadly the same proportion of the gain as the qualifying period bears to the total period of ownership:

PRR: gain × qualifying ownership period ÷ total ownership period

The computation may need further adjustment if only part of the property qualifies, the property had exclusive business use, or other statutory restrictions apply. Each joint owner calculates their own gain and relief by reference to their beneficial interest and circumstances.

Interaction with four-year FIG relief

A qualifying new resident may claim four-year foreign income and gains (FIG) relief for a selected eligible foreign gain. A claim can relieve the selected gain, but the claimant loses their Income Tax personal allowance and Capital Gains Tax annual exempt amount for that tax year. Claims are made by each individual through Self Assessment.

If FIG relief is not claimed for an overseas property gain, the normal Capital Gains Tax rules apply, including any PRR supported by the facts. The better result depends on the amount of PRR, other income and gains, losses, allowances and any other FIG claims. It must be assessed separately for each taxpayer and tax year.

Records and checks

  • Confirm beneficial ownership and the property’s acquisition and disposal dates.
  • Retain evidence of actual occupation and how every part of the property was used.
  • Determine tax residence for every relevant tax year.
  • Retain evidence of qualifying days where the overseas residence-day condition applies.
  • Translate acquisition cost, disposal proceeds and allowable expenditure into sterling using the rates applicable on the relevant dates.
  • Check other income, gains, losses and allowances before deciding whether to claim FIG relief.

Example B illustration

Example B assumes that the Hong Kong flat genuinely qualified as each sister’s only or main residence for 313 months and that the ordinary final nine months also qualify. This gives 322 qualifying months out of 342 complete months of ownership.

Total sterling gain: £500,000 − £301,587 = £198,413

PRR: £198,413 × 322 ÷ 342 = £186,809.90

Gain remaining: £198,413 − £186,809.90 = £11,603.10

Each equal joint owner’s share of the remaining gain is £5,801.55 before her own allowable losses and annual exempt amount. On the stated 2026/27 assumptions, each sister deducts a £3,000 annual exempt amount if she does not make a FIG claim that causes it to be lost. The example’s conclusion is illustrative and is not tax advice.
