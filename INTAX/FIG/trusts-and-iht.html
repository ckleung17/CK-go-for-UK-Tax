Non-UK Resident Trusts and Residence-Based Inheritance Tax (IHT)

This page explains two related but legally separate changes from 6 April 2025: the removal of the former income and gains protections for offshore trusts, and the replacement of domicile with long-term UK residence as the principal test for the IHT treatment of foreign property.

For the underlying settlements, Transfer of Assets Abroad and CGT matching rules, first review Offshore Attribution, Matching and FIG Relief.

How FIG Relates to Trusts and IHT

The four-year FIG regime does not create a new type of trust. It may allow a qualifying new resident to claim relief for eligible foreign income or gains arising within, or attributed from, a trust. The result still depends on the trust terms, the identity and residence of the settlor, trustees and beneficiaries, and the applicable attribution or matching rules. Review the introduction to trusts before applying those rules.

IHT is a separate regime. Although the residence-based IHT rules also began on 6 April 2025, a FIG claim does not exempt an individual's estate or trust property from IHT. Long-term UK residence generally determines whether foreign property falls within IHT, while separate rules govern lifetime transfers, death estates and trust charges. Review the introduction to IHT before considering the residence test.

Keep the Taxes Separate

The same trust may be considered under several different sets of rules. A conclusion under one set does not determine the result under another:

  • Income Tax: considers whether trust income is attributed to the settlor or whether a benefit is matched with accumulated income.
  • Capital Gains Tax (CGT): considers whether trust gains are attributed to a settlor or matched with capital payments received by beneficiaries.
  • Inheritance Tax (IHT): considers whether foreign settled property is excluded property or falls within the relevant-property or death-estate rules.

Trust residence, the settlor's residence and the identity of those who can benefit must therefore be reviewed separately for each tax.

1. Removal of the Former Trust Protections

Before 6 April 2025, qualifying foreign income and gains arising in certain non-UK resident trusts could receive the former trust protections. Broadly, those protections could prevent immediate attribution to a UK-resident settlor who satisfied the statutory domicile and remittance-basis conditions. The protections did not make the trust or its funds permanently exempt: later benefits, capital payments, onward gifts or remittances could still produce a UK tax charge.

From 6 April 2025: Finance Act 2025 removed those protections for foreign income and gains arising from that date. This does not mean that every receipt of every non-UK resident trust is automatically taxed on its settlor. The result depends on the trust terms and the relevant attribution provisions.

Income Tax

If a UK-resident settlor retains an interest because the settlor or their spouse or civil partner can benefit, section 624 of the Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005 can tax the settlor on settlement income as it arises, including foreign income. Other cases may instead fall within the transfer-of-assets-abroad rules or the rules matching benefits with accumulated income. See HMRC TSEM4705.

Capital Gains Tax

Trust gains require a separate CGT analysis. Depending on the facts, gains may be attributed to a UK-resident settlor or matched with capital payments received by UK-resident beneficiaries. The removal of the former protections does not replace those statutory tests with a single rule taxing every trust gain on the settlor.

Four-Year FIG Relief

A qualifying new resident may claim four-year FIG relief for eligible foreign trust income or gains. Relief is not automatic and eligibility must be considered for each source and each claim. Income or gains that do not qualify remain subject to the ordinary trust-attribution and beneficiary rules.

2. Income and Gains Arising Before 6 April 2025

The abolition of the protections does not retrospectively tax every protected amount accumulated before 6 April 2025. Those amounts remain in their historical trust pools and must be distinguished from income and gains arising from that date.

  • Pre-6 April 2025 pools: may become taxable when matched with a subsequent benefit, capital payment, onward gift or remittance under the applicable rules.
  • Amounts arising from 6 April 2025: are considered under the current arising-basis and attribution rules, subject to any valid four-year FIG claim.

Trustees and affected individuals should preserve records showing the source, year and character of each amount. See HMRC TSEM4720 for the treatment of benefits matched with historical income.

3. Residence-Based Inheritance Tax

For chargeable events from 6 April 2025, long-term UK residence generally replaces domicile and deemed domicile as the principal test for bringing an individual's foreign property within IHT. UK-situated property remains within the scope of IHT regardless of long-term-residence status, subject to the ordinary exemptions and reliefs.

The Long-Term UK Residence Test

Broadly, an adult is a long-term UK resident for a tax year if they were UK resident for either the previous 10 consecutive tax years or at least 10 of the previous 20 tax years. For a transfer or death, the test is applied by reference to the tax years preceding the tax year in which that chargeable event occurs. Special rules apply to individuals under age 20 and to certain people who were non-resident in 2025/26.

The Post-Departure Tail

Long-term UK residence can continue after an individual leaves the UK. The tail is not always 10 years: it generally ranges from three to ten tax years, according to the individual's UK residence history. After 10 consecutive tax years of non-UK residence, the 10-out-of-20 test resets for a later return.

4. Foreign Property Held in Trust

From 6 April 2025, whether foreign settled property is excluded property depends on the date of the chargeable event and the settlor's circumstances:

  • Settlor alive: foreign settled property is generally excluded property if the settlor is not a long-term UK resident at the chargeable event.
  • Settlor died on or after 6 April 2025: the settlor's long-term-residence status immediately before death generally fixes the treatment of the foreign settled property.
  • Settlor died before 6 April 2025: the former domicile test generally continues to determine whether the foreign settled property is excluded property.

For a relevant-property trust, a ten-year anniversary occurring while the settlor is a long-term UK resident can produce an IHT charge. A proportionate charge can also arise when the settlor ceases to be long-term UK resident and the foreign property becomes excluded property. The detailed calculation reflects the period for which the property was relevant property. See HMRC IHTM47052.
