The Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF) & Historic Mixed Funds

A technical guide to the three tax years in which eligible former remittance-basis users may designate qualifying overseas capital at temporary rates.

TRF should be compared with Business Investment Relief and preserved remittance rules explained in Legacy Remittance-Basis Reliefs after 6 April 2025.

The Purpose of the TRF Window

Following the abolition of the remittance basis on 6 April 2025, unremitted foreign income and gains arising before that date generally remain taxable at the usual Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax rates when remitted to the UK, unless a relief or exemption applies.

Finance Act 2025, Schedule 10 introduced the Temporary Repatriation Facility (TRF). An eligible individual may make a designation election through Self Assessment for 2025/26, 2026/27 or 2027/28 and pay the TRF charge on an amount of qualifying overseas capital. The TRF charge is a standalone charge calculated by reference to the amount designated; it is not itself Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax. The charge becomes payable on the amount that the individual designates, whether or not HMRC immediately examines its composition. Payment of the charge does not, however, validate an ineligible designation or guarantee the remittance exemption. HMRC may later enquire whether the amount met the statutory conditions; if it did not, the ordinary Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax liability may still arise and the TRF charge may not be repayable after the amendment deadline. The individual should therefore make the designation accurately and honestly, to the best of their knowledge and belief, and retain records supporting both the amount and its known or uncertain source.

An individual is eligible to make a designation only if they:

  • are UK resident in the tax year of designation under the Statutory Residence Test;
  • were subject to the remittance basis for at least one earlier tax year; and
  • have qualifying overseas capital available to designate.

Comprehensive operational rules are in HMRC RDRM71000, with eligibility explained at RDRM73200.

Example: A Former Remittance-Basis User Who Is Also a QNR

Alex became UK resident in 2022/23 after at least 10 consecutive tax years of non-UK residence. He remained UK resident and claimed the remittance basis for 2022/23, 2023/24 and 2024/25, leaving the relevant foreign income and gains outside the UK.

Although Alex arrived before 6 April 2025, the 2025/26 tax year is the fourth and final year of his four-year residence window. He may claim FIG relief for eligible foreign income and gains arising in 2025/26. That relief does not cover his foreign income and gains arising before 6 April 2025.

Assume Alex holds £100,000 of qualifying pre-6 April 2025 foreign income and gains and, separately, £250,000 of identifiable clean capital. Without a TRF designation, the £100,000 may remain taxable under the preserved remittance rules when remitted to the UK. The £250,000 of clean capital is not taxable merely because it is remitted and does not need to be designated.

Alex designates the £100,000 in his Self Assessment return for 2025/26 and pays the 12% TRF charge of £12,000. Although the return is submitted after the end of the tax year, the designation is treated as taking effect on 6 April 2025. The designated £100,000 is then known as designated qualifying overseas capital, or TRF capital. It may be remitted immediately or later without a further UK tax charge on the remittance. The FIG regime therefore applies separately to Alex's eligible income and gains arising on or after 6 April 2025.

See HMRC RFIG44000 for the transitional QNR rules, HMRC RDRM71000 for the purpose and scope of the TRF, and HMRC RDRM73100 for the designation process and effective date.

Definitions and Eligibility

The TRF and the four-year FIG regime use separate eligibility tests and apply to different amounts:

  • Four-year FIG regime: A Qualifying New Resident (QNR) may claim relief on eligible foreign income and gains arising on or after 6 April 2025 during their qualifying four-year window.
  • Temporary Repatriation Facility: A UK-resident individual who was subject to the remittance basis for at least one earlier tax year may designate qualifying overseas capital. This generally concerns unremitted foreign income and gains arising before 6 April 2025.

Key distinction: QNR status is neither required nor sufficient for TRF eligibility. A long-term UK resident who used the remittance basis may qualify for the TRF without being a QNR, while a QNR who never used the remittance basis will not qualify for the TRF. An individual may satisfy both regimes, but each requires a separate claim or designation and its own conditions.

TRF Rates, Designation Years and Deadlines

The applicable rate depends on the tax year for which the designation election is made. The election is made on the Self Assessment return for that year and may be submitted or amended within the statutory time limit.

Foreign tax: Qualifying overseas capital must be designated net of foreign tax paid or payable. Foreign Tax Credit Relief cannot be claimed against the TRF charge. See HMRC RDRM73340.

TRF Capital and Mixed-Fund Ordering

An offshore account or asset containing more than one category or tax year of income, gains or capital may be a mixed fund. Statutory ordering rules determine the composition of remittances and offshore transfers; the account holder cannot simply choose which component has been transferred.

If the Funds Are Not Mixed

If the designated amount is held in a separate offshore account containing no other funds, the account is not a mixed fund and the mixed-fund ordering rules do not need to be applied. A transfer to the UK, up to the remaining designated balance, is covered by the TRF remittance exemption and does not give rise to a further UK tax charge. Clear records should be retained to show the amount designated, the TRF charge paid and all subsequent movements into and out of the account.

Keeping identifiable clean capital in a separate account also preserves the practical distinction between the two categories. A remittance from the clean-capital account is not converted into TRF capital and does not use up the designated balance. However, if income, gains or another category of capital is later added to either account, the account may become a mixed fund and the ordering rules must then be considered.

Where a mixed fund contains designated qualifying overseas capital, the ordering rules give TRF capital priority when amounts are remitted to the UK:

Priority rule: A remittance to the UK from a mixed fund is treated as comprising TRF capital before the other categories of income, gains and capital in the fund. During the three TRF tax years, the temporary annualised basis may affect how remittances and offshore transfers are ordered and matched.

Example: Applying the Priority Rule

Assume Alex has one offshore mixed account containing £200,000 immediately before his only remittance in 2025/26:

  • £100,000 of TRF capital;
  • £40,000 of undesignated, untaxed foreign income; and
  • £60,000 of identifiable clean capital.

Alex remits £120,000 to the UK. He cannot choose to treat the transfer as £60,000 of clean capital and £60,000 of TRF capital. The priority rule treats the first £100,000 as TRF capital. The remaining £20,000 is then matched with the undesignated foreign income before any clean capital is treated as remitted.

  • The applicable TRF charge has already been paid on the £100,000 when it was designated; no further UK tax charge arises when that amount is remitted.
  • The £20,000 of undesignated foreign income is taxable at the applicable Income Tax rate, subject to any available relief such as Foreign Tax Credit Relief.
  • The £60,000 of clean capital has not yet been treated as remitted.

Simplifying assumptions: This example assumes there are no other remittances or offshore transfers in the tax year and no additional categories requiring further ordering. During the TRF period, the annualised mixed-fund rules must be applied where relevant.

The statutory priority order is explained at HMRC RDRM35240. Special rules also permit TRF capital to be transferred to a nominated TRF capital account, subject to detailed conditions. See HMRC RDRM75100, RDRM75500 and HMRC Helpsheet HS264.

Critical Compliance Alert: Paying Tax with Offshore Money

Paying the TRF charge from UK funds or capital that can already be remitted without a tax charge does not require an additional TRF designation. However, if pre-6 April 2025 foreign income or gains are transferred from overseas to pay the charge, the amount used for that payment must itself be designated. Otherwise, the payment is an ordinary remittance taxable at the applicable Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax rates. See HMRC RDRM73400.
