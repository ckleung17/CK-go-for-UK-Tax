FIG Claims: Lost Allowances and Restricted Reliefs

A practical assessment of the statutory consequences of making a claim under the 4-year FIG regime.

The Statutory Cost of FIG Relief

Claiming relief under the Foreign Income and Gains (FIG) regime is an annual choice with statutory consequences. An individual who makes a foreign-income claim, a foreign-gain claim or an Overseas Workday Relief (OWR) election loses specified allowances and reliefs for that tax year.

These consequences apply even if the individual claims relief for only one source or makes only one type of claim or election. The tax saved on the chosen qualifying foreign income or gains should therefore be compared with the value of the allowances and reliefs lost or restricted.

The statutory provisions are contained in Finance Act 2025, Schedule 1. HMRC explains the practical consequences in RFIG43000: Effects of Claim and Helpsheet HS266, section 5.

What Is Lost or Restricted?

The following consequences arise for a tax year in which a foreign-income claim, foreign-gain claim or OWR election is made:

1. The UK Income Tax Personal Allowance

The individual loses entitlement to the UK Personal Allowance, which is £12,570 for 2025/26 and 2026/27. Their UK income is therefore calculated without that allowance. This does not remove separate provisions such as the Personal Savings Allowance, the Dividend Allowance, deductible business or property expenses, or any other relief for which the individual remains eligible.

2. The Capital Gains Tax Annual Exempt Amount

The individual loses the Capital Gains Tax (CGT) Annual Exempt Amount, which is £3,000 for 2025/26 and 2026/27. Taxable gains are calculated without that exemption, but allowable losses on UK assets and unused allowable losses brought forward from years outside a relevant claim remain available under the normal rules.

3. Foreign Qualifying Capital Losses

A foreign qualifying capital loss is the loss-side statutory category corresponding to gains that can fall within the FIG foreign-gain rules. In the straightforward case of an asset disposal, it broadly means an allowable loss on an asset situated outside the UK where a corresponding profit could have been a qualifying foreign asset gain. A gain on such an asset is qualifying only if the asset is not at least 75% derived from UK land. Additional rules apply to attributed gains, trusts and qualifying asset holding companies.

The statutory treatment of gains and losses is different. A qualifying foreign gain is relieved only if the individual identifies and quantifies it in a valid foreign-gain claim. A foreign qualifying loss is not relieved or preserved: if it accrues in a tax year for which the individual makes a foreign-income claim, foreign-gain claim or OWR election, it ceases to be an allowable loss.

  • The loss cannot be set against taxable UK or foreign gains arising in that year.
  • The loss cannot be carried forward for use in a later tax year.
  • The restriction applies even where the individual makes only a foreign-income claim or only an OWR election and claims no relief for foreign gains.
  • Allowable losses on UK assets arising in the year remain available.
  • Unused allowable losses brought forward from years outside a relevant claim are also unaffected.

Example: Alex realises a £30,000 qualifying gain on one overseas investment and a £20,000 foreign qualifying loss on another. He makes a foreign-gain claim for the £30,000 gain. The claimed gain is relieved, but the £20,000 loss ceases to be allowable: Alex cannot use it against a UK gain or carry it forward. If Alex made no FIG claim and no OWR election for the year, both disposals would instead be dealt with under the ordinary CGT rules.

4. Other Allowances and Tax Reductions

The individual also loses entitlement to the Blind Person's Allowance, tax reductions for married couples and civil partners, the transferable tax allowance for married couples and civil partners, and the specified relief for payments for life insurance and related payments under sections 457 and 458 Income Tax Act 2007.

5. Foreign Trading, Property and Finance-Cost Reliefs

Loss relief is unavailable for a loss arising in the claim year from a trade, profession, vocation or property business carried on wholly outside the UK where any profits would be qualifying foreign income. The loss cannot be used in that year or carried to another year.

A qualifying loss carried forward from 2024/25 or an earlier year is treated differently: it may still be deducted from later profits before FIG relief is calculated, which can use up part of the carried-forward loss even where the resulting foreign profit is relieved.

Relief for relevant interest and finance costs relating to a dwelling-related loan for an overseas property business is also lost for the claim year, and the brought-forward amount for the following year is reset to nil.

6. Adjusted Net Income and Pension Contributions

Foreign-income relief is ignored when calculating adjusted net income. The relieved foreign income therefore remains relevant when assessing matters such as tax-free childcare and the High Income Child Benefit Charge.

A foreign-income claim may also restrict Income Tax relief on registered pension contributions where the claimed amount includes relevant UK earnings. HMRC states that the restriction does not reduce the maximum relievable amount below the statutory basic amount, currently £3,600.

Factors to Compare The UK tax saved on the qualifying amounts claimed. The UK tax on those amounts compared with the value of retained allowances, losses, tax credits and other reliefs.

The comparison must be performed separately for each tax year. It should take account of the particular amounts eligible for relief, applicable Income Tax and CGT rates, foreign tax credits, losses, allowances and the individual's wider tax position.
