FIG Claims, Double Taxation Agreements and Foreign Tax

How source-by-source claims interact with treaty residence, taxing rights and foreign tax relief.

Three Questions, Not One

Scope: This page concerns individuals using the UK FIG regime for 2025/26 onwards and reflects enacted law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

For each amount, decide separately whether the UK taxes it under domestic law, whether a Double Taxation Agreement changes the allocation of taxing rights, and whether a FIG claim or Foreign Tax Credit Relief reduces the UK liability. A treaty and a FIG claim are different routes and should not be treated as interchangeable.

Treaty Residence and FIG Eligibility

Domestic UK residence for the qualifying-new-resident test is determined under the Statutory Residence Test. If an individual is UK resident under that test, a treaty tie-breaker treating them as resident elsewhere does not turn that year into a non-UK-resident year for the ten-year lookback or four-year FIG window.

A treaty may nevertheless exempt an item, restrict the UK rate or require the UK or the other state to give credit. The relevant article, residence position, beneficial ownership and source rules must be applied to the particular income or gain.

Foreign Tax Credit Relief and FIG

HMRC's current FIG guidance states that Foreign Tax Credit Relief cannot also be claimed on foreign income covered by a FIG relief claim. Credit can be considered only for the appropriate part not covered by that claim. Because FIG relief can be selected source by source, the comparison should be performed for each source rather than by assuming that all foreign income must receive the same treatment.

Foreign tax is not automatically creditable merely because it has been paid. The ordinary statutory and treaty conditions, the identity of the taxpayer, the character and timing of the income or gain, and the maximum UK tax attributable to it must still be considered.

Employment Income, OWR and PAYE

A globally mobile employee can be both a qualifying new resident and treaty-resident elsewhere. Overseas Workday Relief and treaty relief must be calculated on their own terms. From 6 April 2026, section 690D ITEPA 2003 provides the statutory PAYE notification process for qualifying-new-resident and treaty-affected payments, but the same amount cannot be counted in both proportions.

A PAYE notification affects estimated withholding only. The final treaty, OWR and foreign-tax position is reconciled through Self Assessment.

Illustration

Elena is UK resident under the SRT and treaty-resident in another country. She receives foreign interest taxed overseas and employment income relating partly to duties outside the UK.

  • The UK-resident year still counts in her four-year FIG window.
  • The treaty is applied separately to the interest and employment income.
  • She compares a FIG claim with UK tax after any available foreign tax credit on the interest.
  • OWR, treaty relief and the PAYE notification are reconciled for the employment income without double counting.

Applied analysis: The lowest foreign rate does not necessarily identify the best UK claim. The comparison must include lost allowances and the amount of foreign tax that cannot be credited.

Practical Checklist

  • Confirm domestic and treaty residence separately.
  • Identify the relevant treaty article for every source.
  • Record foreign tax by source, taxpayer and payment date.
  • Model partial FIG claims and remaining foreign tax credits.
  • Reconcile PAYE estimates with the final Self Assessment position.
