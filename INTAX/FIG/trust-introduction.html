Introduction to Trusts

A trust is a legal relationship under which trustees hold and manage property for beneficiaries, or for another legally permitted purpose, in accordance with the trust terms.

It is commonly created by a trust deed during the settlor's lifetime or by a will on death. Trustees usually hold legal title while beneficiaries hold or may receive the economic benefit.

Parties Involved

Settlor

The settlor is the person who establishes the trust or provides property or value to it. A trust can have more than one settlor.

  • Creating the trust: the settlor may establish it by a trust deed during their lifetime or by a will on death.
  • Providing property: a person who adds money, investments, land or other assets may be a settlor in relation to that contribution.
  • Providing value indirectly: tax law may treat a person as the settlor where they are the real source of value, even if someone else formally transfers the property.
  • Retaining rights or being able to benefit: if the settlor keeps rights over the trust, or the trust can benefit the settlor or their spouse or civil partner, special rules may treat trust income or gains as theirs for tax purposes.
  • Residence: the settlor's residence history can affect the UK tax treatment, including whether foreign trust property is within the scope of IHT.

Trustee

The individual or corporate body that legally owns and administers the trust property under the trust terms and fiduciary duties. Trustee residence helps determine trust residence for Income Tax and CGT.

Beneficiary

A person or class of persons entitled, or potentially entitled, to trust income, capital or another benefit. A distribution, capital payment or other benefit may have tax consequences.

Protector

An optional person given specified supervisory or consent powers by the trust deed. Those powers may be relevant when determining who controls the trust and how it operates.

One person may occupy more than one role. A settlor may also be a trustee or beneficiary, but a trustee must still act under the trust terms and fiduciary duties.

Key Terminology

Trust property or settled property

Money, investments, land or other assets held subject to the trust.

Trust income

Returns such as interest, dividends or rent arising from trust property.

Trust capital and trust gains

Capital is the underlying trust fund. A gain may arise when trustees dispose of an asset for more than its allowable CGT cost.

Distribution, payment or benefit

Value provided to or for a beneficiary or another relevant person, including cash, an asset, use of property or payment of an expense.

Settlor-interested trust

Broadly, a trust in which the settlor or their spouse or civil partner can or may benefit. Special attribution rules can apply.

Discretionary trust

A trust under which trustees have discretion over which eligible beneficiaries receive income or capital, when and how much.

Interest in possession

An immediate entitlement to current income or enjoyment of specified trust property. Its tax treatment depends on the nature and date of the interest.

Non-UK resident trust

A trust that is not UK resident under the relevant Income Tax and CGT trustee-residence rules. Asset or bank-account location alone does not determine residence.

Relevant property

Broadly, settled property within the IHT relevant-property regime. Anniversary and exit charges may arise.

Excluded property

Property excluded from particular IHT charging provisions by statute. From 6 April 2025, foreign settled property's status can depend on the settlor's long-term UK residence.

Historical income or gains pool

A tax computation record of accumulated trust income or gains available for matching; it need not correspond to a separate bank account.
