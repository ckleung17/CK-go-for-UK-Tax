Legacy Remittance-Basis Reliefs after 6 April 2025

Business Investment Relief, preserved remittance rules and narrow transitional reliefs that continue after abolition.

Abolition Did Not Erase Historical Funds

Scope: This page concerns pre-6 April 2025 foreign income and gains of former remittance-basis users and reflects enacted law and official guidance checked on 17 July 2026.

The remittance basis cannot be claimed for 2025/26 onwards, but the preserved rules can still tax a later remittance of previously untaxed foreign income or gains. The four-year FIG regime does not relieve those historical amounts. TRF, Business Investment Relief and other transitional provisions must be considered separately.

Business Investment Relief

A former remittance-basis user can still use qualifying pre-6 April 2025 foreign income or gains to make an eligible UK business investment and claim Business Investment Relief. Under current law and HMRC guidance, no new qualifying investment can be made on or after 6 April 2028, although an existing qualifying investment can continue to benefit after that date.

The target company, investment form, timing and claim conditions must all be met. A disposal, extraction of value, company ceasing to qualify or other potentially chargeable event can cause a taxable remittance unless an available mitigation step is completed within its deadline.

Already-designated TRF capital cannot be used to make a qualifying BIR investment. Conversely, qualifying historical amounts already invested can in some circumstances be designated under the TRF, changing the disposal and mitigation analysis.

Preserved Remittances and Re-remittance Relief

Undesignated historical income and gains remain subject to the statutory remittance rules, including mixed-fund ordering, relevant-person rules and tracing through assets. Clean capital is not taxable merely because it is brought to the UK, but evidence is needed to establish its character.

Finance Act 2025 introduced a narrow re-remittance relief for specified repeated remittances. Among other conditions, the initial remittance must have occurred during a continuous period of more than five years' non-UK residence ending before 6 April 2024, a later relevant charge must have arisen before 6 April 2025, and the residence conditions for 2024/25 and 2025/26 must be met. It is not a general exemption for funds brought to the UK twice.

Illustration

Mei holds untaxed foreign income from 2022/23 and is considering investing it in shares of a UK trading company during 2026/27.

  • Confirm that the funds remain identifiable and have not already been designated under TRF.
  • Check the target company and investment against the BIR conditions.
  • Complete the investment and claim within the statutory periods.
  • Maintain a diary for disposals, extraction of value and mitigation.

Applied analysis: BIR can prevent the investment itself being a taxable remittance, but it does not convert the underlying historical income into ordinary clean capital.

Practical Checklist

  • Build a source-and-tax-year schedule for historical funds.
  • Compare ordinary remittance tax, TRF and BIR before moving money.
  • Obtain and retain BIR eligibility evidence.
  • Track potentially chargeable events and grace periods.
  • Do not assume post-6 April 2028 reinvestment remains available.
