Why losses and investment reliefs matter

Introduction and overview

Income Tax normally measures income one tax year at a time, but real economic activity does not always fit neatly into a profitable year. Parliament has therefore created reliefs that can recognise genuine losses and encourage individuals to provide new capital to smaller, higher-risk companies. The rules cover specified business, property, employment and share losses, as well as qualifying subscriptions for new shares. They can benefit people who bear an eligible loss or investment risk, the businesses that receive funding and, more broadly, economic activity. The intended result is a fairer allocation of taxable income and more investment in businesses that may otherwise struggle to raise finance—not a guaranteed refund or protection from investment loss.

This page is an introductory map for individuals for the 2026–27 tax year. Company loss rules are different.

Understanding loss relief

A loss does not automatically produce a tax repayment. The law first identifies the type of loss and then specifies the income, profits or gains against which it may be used. A deduction usually reduces the amount exposed to tax; this differs from an investment tax reducer, which is deducted later from the tax bill itself.

Choosing a route can change the outcome

Some claims use a loss against income without preserving unused personal allowances, and a claim may affect more than one tax year. Compare the permitted routes, ordering rules and future use rather than choosing only the largest immediate repayment. Claim deadlines vary.

The limit on certain Income Tax reliefs

For 2026–27, the total amount of certain reliefs that an individual may deduct from income is generally capped at the greater of £50,000 and 25% of adjusted total income. In broad terms, this is income before the affected reliefs, less specified deductions and with statutory adjustments. The cap can apply to sideways trade-loss relief, employment loss relief, ordinary share-loss relief and some other deductions. Carrying a trade loss forward against later profits of the same trade is outside the cap, as is qualifying Enterprise Investment Scheme or Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme share-loss relief.

Illustrative cap calculation

Assume a fictional taxpayer has adjusted total income of £70,000 and an otherwise qualifying £60,000 trade loss available against general income. Twenty-five per cent of £70,000 is £17,500, so the greater limit is £50,000. The claim is limited to £50,000; the remaining £10,000 may be carried forward against future profits of the same trade if the conditions are met. This shows the loss usable, not the cash tax saving.

Investment tax reducers

Separate rules encourage individuals to subscribe for newly issued shares that put money into qualifying smaller companies or venture capital trusts. These reliefs reduce the investor’s Income Tax bill rather than taxable income, but cannot reduce it below nil. The investment remains at risk, and relief may be withdrawn if the investor, company, shares or holding period fails the conditions.

Capital Gains Tax advantages and VCT dividends

The schemes can also affect Capital Gains Tax (CGT), but each relief has its own conditions and withdrawal rules:

  • EIS Deferral Relief: a chargeable gain on another asset can be deferred where an amount is invested in qualifying EIS shares. The deferred gain is normally brought back into charge when the EIS shares are disposed of or another chargeable event occurs. Income Tax relief is not required for this separate deferral claim.
  • SEIS Reinvestment Relief: where a gain is reinvested in shares qualifying for SEIS Income Tax relief, up to 50% of the reinvested gain can be exempt from CGT. With a £200,000 qualifying subscription limit, the maximum exempt amount is normally £100,000 for the year.
  • VCT disposal relief: a gain on disposing of qualifying ordinary VCT shares is fully exempt from CGT, but a corresponding loss is not allowable. The exemption is limited to VCT shares acquired within the £200,000 permitted maximum for that tax year and is subject to the other statutory conditions.
  • VCT dividends: an individual aged 18 or over pays no Income Tax on dividends from qualifying ordinary VCT shares acquired within the annual £200,000 permitted maximum. This dividend exemption, like VCT disposal relief, can apply to qualifying shares acquired second-hand; the upfront 20% Income Tax reducer applies only to subscriptions for new shares.

EIS and SEIS may also provide disposal relief on gains from the scheme shares themselves where the Income Tax relief and holding-period conditions are met. This is separate from EIS Deferral Relief and SEIS Reinvestment Relief.

Illustrative EIS calculation

Assume an individual subscribes £20,000 cash for qualifying EIS shares issued in 2026–27, meets all conditions and has at least £6,000 of Income Tax liability available. The reducer is £20,000 × 30% = £6,000. If only £4,000 of liability were available, the relief for that year could not exceed £4,000.

If qualifying shares lose value

If qualifying EIS shares cost £10,000, receive £3,000 of EIS Income Tax relief and are later disposed of for nothing, the starting allowable loss is generally reduced to £7,000 before the share-loss rules are applied. A negligible-value claim, withdrawn relief or value received can alter the result.

Working with relief claims

Fix the tax year and identify the legal source of the loss or the share issue date. Check eligibility, compare every permitted destination or carry-back, model the effect on allowances and other reliefs, and retain the calculation and evidence. Trading-loss claims are normally made in, or alongside, Self Assessment; investment claims require the relevant certificate.

A claim under section 64 ITA 2007 to use a trade loss against general income—often called sideways trade-loss relief—must normally be made within 12 months after the 31 January that follows the tax year of loss. For a loss arising in 2026–27, the normal deadline is therefore 31 January 2029. The supplementary claim against chargeable gains follows the same section 64 claim time limit. Other loss and investment reliefs have different deadlines, and statutory extensions can apply only in specified circumstances.
