Fictional educational examples. Ignore National Insurance, HICBC, student loans and tax deducted unless stated. Not advice.

1. England: earnings only

Alex earns £60,000 and has the standard allowance. Taxable income is £47,430. Tax is £37,700 x 20% plus £9,730 x 40% = £11,432.

2. Mixed income

Blair has earnings £45,000, interest £2,000 and dividends £5,000. Earnings tax is £6,486. Interest tax is £200 after the £1,000 PSA. Dividend tax is £916.26 after the £500 0% allowance: £2,770 x 10.75% plus £1,730 x 35.75%, each component rounded to pennies. Total £7,602.26.

3. Savings starting rate

Dev has pension income £14,000 and interest £4,000. After the Personal Allowance, taxable non-savings income is £1,430, leaving £3,570 of the £5,000 savings starting-rate band. The remaining £430 interest is within the £1,000 PSA. Income Tax is therefore £286, all on the pension slice.

4. Gift Aid and the allowance taper

Ellis has £105,000 income and makes a qualifying £4,000 net Gift Aid donation. Gross Gift Aid is £5,000, reducing adjusted net income to £100,000, so the full £12,570 Personal Allowance remains. The basic-rate limit extends to £42,700. Taxable income is £92,430: £42,700 x 20% plus £49,730 x 40% = £28,432. Gift Aid donor conditions must also be met.

5. Scottish earnings

Fiona is a Scottish taxpayer with £50,000 earnings and the standard allowance. Taxable income is £37,430. Applying the 2026/27 Scottish slices gives £753.73 + £2,597.80 + £2,968.56 + £2,661.96 = £8,982.05.

6. Claim choice

Gareth has a trading loss and other income. No result is calculated until the trade, commerciality, dates, available claims, relief cap and effect on allowances are established. This illustrates why a loss should not automatically be set against the current year.
