How Income Tax is calculated

An individual’s Income Tax liability is not found by applying one rate to total receipts. The computation classifies income, applies deductions and allowances at their prescribed stages, orders the resulting taxable slices, calculates tax at the rates applicable to each slice and then makes any later adjustments.

Income Tax Act 2007 (ITA 2007), section 23 provides seven steps:

  1. Total income
  2. Net income
  3. Allowances
  4. Calculate tax
  5. Total Step 4 tax
  6. Tax reductions
  7. Additional tax
  1. Step 1 - total income: identify the income charged to Income Tax for the tax year and add the amounts from each source.
  2. Step 2 - net income: deduct only the reliefs that the legislation allows at this step from total income, applying their limits and ordering rules. Do not confuse this with adjusted net income, which is a separate figure used to test particular limits and tapers and is not automatically the same as net income or taxable income.
  3. Step 3 - allowances and taxable income: deduct the allowances permitted at this step. The amount left is taxable income: the income to which the tax rates will be applied, divided into the relevant income categories and slices. Under ITA 2007, section 25, deductions must generally be allocated so as to produce the greatest reduction in liability.
  4. Step 4 - calculate tax: apply the relevant rates to the taxable non-savings, savings and dividend slices.
  5. Step 5 - total Step 4 tax: add together the amounts of tax calculated at Step 4.
  6. Step 6 - tax reductions: deduct tax reductions in the statutory order and subject to their own limits.
  7. Step 7 - additional tax and Income Tax liability: add any additional tax required by a provision listed in ITA 2007, section 30. The result after all seven steps is the Income Tax liability.

Ordering the taxable slices

For the ordinary stacking calculation, non-savings income is considered first, savings income above it and dividend income above savings income. This reflects ITA 2007, section 16, subject to provisions that can modify the normal ordering.

  1. Non-savings income Calculated first and uses the lowest available tax bands.
  2. Savings income Calculated second and uses the tax-band capacity left after non-savings income.
  3. Dividend income Calculated last and uses the tax-band capacity left after non-savings and savings income.

For an England, Wales or Northern Ireland taxpayer in 2026/27, the basic-rate limit is £37,700 and the higher-rate limit is £125,140. Non-savings and savings income are generally charged at 20%, 40% and 45% across those bands. Dividend income uses the same remaining band capacity but is charged at 10.75%, 35.75% and 39.35%.

A 0% rate band does not remove income from taxable income. The slice remains in the stack and uses rate-band capacity even though its tax rate is nil.

Notes to the computation

  1. Unused Personal Allowance: the Personal Allowance is not restricted to non-savings income. If any remains after covering non-savings income, allocate the unused amount against the person’s remaining income in the way that gives the greatest reduction in Income Tax. Depending on the facts, it may therefore cover savings income and then dividend income. Income covered by the Personal Allowance is removed before the taxable slices and 0% rate bands are calculated. In this example, the £14,000 net non-savings income uses all of Sam’s £12,570 Personal Allowance, so none is left.
  2. Starting rate for savings: for 2026/27, the maximum band is £5,000 at 0%. Reduce it by £1 for every £1 of non-savings income above the available Personal Allowance. Here, £14,000 − £12,570 = £1,430, so the band available is £5,000 − £1,430 = £3,570. It is nil when non-savings income reaches £17,570, assuming the full £12,570 Personal Allowance. Being a higher-rate taxpayer does not by itself remove this band: the calculation depends on non-savings income, although a person whose non-savings income alone reaches the higher-rate band will have no starting-rate band left.
  3. Personal Savings Allowance: this is a separate 0% rate band for savings income. In 2026/27 it is up to £1,000 for a basic-rate taxpayer, £500 for a higher-rate taxpayer and nil for an additional-rate taxpayer. Determine the taxpayer’s band using all relevant income, including savings income. The income covered remains taxable income and still uses tax-band capacity. Sam is a basic-rate taxpayer in this example, so £1,000 of the £1,430 savings income remaining after the starting-rate band is charged at 0%, leaving £430 charged at 20%.
  4. Dividend Allowance: for 2026/27, up to £500 of dividend income is charged at 0%. The allowance does not remove that income from taxable income: the £500 still uses tax-band capacity and can affect the rate applied to later dividend income. Sam uses the full £500 allowance, leaving £9,500 charged at the applicable dividend rate.

Scope limits and later topics

These examples deliberately exclude matters taught in later modules. Do not use them as universal templates where the facts include:

  • split-year treatment;
  • Gift Aid, relief-at-source pension contributions or rate-band extensions;
  • life-policy gains or top-slicing relief;
  • joint-income or settlements issues;
  • loss reliefs other than the stated trade loss, or investment reliefs;
  • trust or deceased-estate income;
  • adjusted-net-income calculations beyond the stated assumptions; or
  • Scottish non-savings rates or other devolved-rate questions.
