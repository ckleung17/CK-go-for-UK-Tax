The statutory computation

An individual’s Income Tax liability is not found by applying one rate to total receipts. The computation classifies income, applies deductions and allowances at their statutory stages, orders the resulting taxable slices, calculates tax at the rates applicable to each slice and then makes any later adjustments.

Income Tax Act 2007 (ITA 2007), section 23 provides seven steps:

  1. Step 1 - total income: identify the income charged to Income Tax for the tax year and total the amounts from each source.
  2. Step 2 - net income: deduct only the reliefs permitted at Step 2. The result, after the statutory limits and ordering rules, is net income.
  3. Step 3 - allowances: deduct the allowances permitted at Step 3 to arrive at the amounts on which tax is charged. Under ITA 2007, section 25, deductions must generally be allocated so as to produce the greatest reduction in liability.
  4. Step 4 - calculate tax: apply the relevant rates to the taxable non-savings, savings and dividend slices.
  5. Step 5 - special-rate amounts: add tax on amounts for which legislation requires a separate calculation.
  6. Step 6 - tax reductions: deduct tax reductions in the statutory order and subject to their own limits.
  7. Step 7 - final adjustments: add or deduct the further amounts required by legislation to reach the Income Tax liability.

Terms that must not be confused

Total income
The Step 1 total of income charged for the year, before Step 2 reliefs and Step 3 allowances.
Net income
Total income after the deductions authorised at Step 2.
Adjusted net income
A separate measure used for particular limits and tapers. It is not automatically the same as net income or taxable income.
Taxable income
The income remaining after the relevant deductions and allowances, split into the categories and slices to which rates are applied.
Income Tax liability
The result after completing all applicable steps, not merely the tax calculated at Step 4.

Ordering the taxable slices

For the ordinary stacking calculation, non-savings income is considered first, savings income above it and dividend income above savings income. This reflects ITA 2007, section 16, subject to provisions that can modify the normal ordering.

For an England, Wales or Northern Ireland taxpayer in 2026/27, the basic-rate limit is £37,700 and the higher-rate limit is £125,140. Non-savings and savings income are generally charged at 20%, 40% and 45% across those bands. Dividend income uses the same remaining band capacity but is charged at 10.75%, 35.75% and 39.35%.

A 0% rate band does not remove income from taxable income. The slice remains in the stack and uses rate-band capacity even though its tax rate is nil.

Detailed illustrative computation

Illustrative facts: Sam is an England, Wales or Northern Ireland taxpayer for 2026/27. Sam has non-savings income of £15,000, taxable savings interest of £38,000 and dividend income of £10,000. Sam is entitled to the standard £12,570 Personal Allowance, the applicable savings 0% rate bands and the £500 dividend allowance. Assume adjusted net income is £63,000 and there are no other deductions, allowances, reliefs, tax reductions, special-rate amounts, tax deducted at source or other Step 7 adjustments.

Total income

£15,000 + £38,000 + £10,000 = £63,000 total income.

Net income

There are no Step 2 deductions in this example, so net income remains £63,000.

Personal Allowance and taxable income

The £12,570 Personal Allowance is allocated against non-savings income:

  • Taxable non-savings income: £15,000 − £12,570 = £2,430.
  • Taxable savings income: £38,000.
  • Taxable dividend income: £10,000.
  • Total taxable income: £2,430 + £38,000 + £10,000 = £50,430.

Tax on non-savings income

The £2,430 non-savings slice falls within the basic-rate band:

£2,430 × 20% = £486 Income Tax.

Tax on savings income

  1. The maximum £5,000 starting-rate band for savings is reduced by taxable non-savings income of £2,430: £5,000 − £2,430 = £2,570 available at 0%.
  2. Sam is a higher-rate taxpayer, so the Personal Savings Allowance provides a further £500 savings slice at 0%.
  3. Savings remaining after the two 0% slices: £38,000 − £2,570 − £500 = £34,930.
  4. The stack has reached £5,500: £2,430 non-savings + £2,570 starting-rate savings + £500 Personal Savings Allowance.
  5. Basic-rate capacity remaining: £37,700 − £5,500 = £32,200. Tax: £32,200 × 20% = £6,440.
  6. The remaining £2,730 savings income is charged at 40%: £2,730 × 40% = £1,092.

Total tax on savings income: £6,440 + £1,092 = £7,532.

Tax on dividend income

  1. After non-savings and savings income, the stack has reached £40,430.
  2. The first £500 of dividends is within the dividend allowance and is charged at 0%, but it moves the stack to £40,930.
  3. The remaining dividends are £10,000 − £500 = £9,500.
  4. That slice is in the higher-rate band and is charged at the 2026/27 dividend upper rate: £9,500 × 35.75% = £3,396.25.

Total Income Tax

£486 + £7,532 + £3,396.25 = £11,414.25 total Income Tax.

Scope limits and later topics

This example deliberately excludes matters taught in later modules. Do not use it as a universal template where the facts include:

  • split-year treatment;
  • Gift Aid, relief-at-source pension contributions or rate-band extensions;
  • life-policy gains or top-slicing relief;
  • joint-income or settlements issues;
  • losses or investment reliefs;
  • trust or deceased-estate income;
  • adjusted-net-income interactions beyond the stated assumption; or
  • Scottish non-savings rates or other devolved-rate questions.

Computation control points

  • Classify each source before applying allowances or rates.
  • Reconcile total income, net income and taxable income at each stage.
  • Track the cumulative band position, including income charged at 0%.
  • Apply the rate for the income category, not merely the name of the band.
  • Keep Step 4 tax separate from later tax reductions, tax deducted and other final adjustments.
  • Recalculate examples independently and state rounding assumptions.
