When a tax year may be split

Under the Statutory Residence Test (SRT), an individual is either UK resident or non-UK resident for the whole tax year. If the individual is UK resident and the detailed conditions of a statutory case are met, split-year treatment divides the year into a UK part and an overseas part for specified tax purposes.

Split-year treatment is automatic when its conditions are satisfied. It is not an election and does not apply merely because an individual arrives in or leaves the UK. The facts for the whole tax year, and sometimes the preceding or following tax year, must be established first.

The eight statutory cases

Cases 1 to 3 concern departures from the UK:

  1. Starting full-time work overseas.
  2. The partner of someone starting full-time work overseas.
  3. Ceasing to have a home in the UK.

Cases 4 to 8 concern arrivals in the UK:

  1. Starting to have a home only in the UK.
  2. Starting full-time work in the UK.
  3. Ceasing full-time work overseas.
  4. The partner of someone ceasing full-time work overseas.
  5. Starting to have a home in the UK.

Each case has its own conditions, day or work limits and method for identifying the split date. A case heading is therefore only a starting point; the detailed statutory test must be applied.

Apply the cases in the correct order

  1. Determine residence for the whole tax year under the SRT.
  2. Establish residence in the relevant preceding and following years where the case requires it.
  3. Test every potentially applicable split-year case.
  4. If more than one case applies, use the statutory priority rules to determine the applicable case and split date.
  5. Allocate income and gains by applying the charging rules for the UK and overseas parts; do not simply apportion every annual amount by days.

For departure cases, Case 1 has priority over Cases 2 and 3, and Case 2 has priority over Case 3. Arrival cases have separate priority rules that can depend on which case produces the earliest split date.

Effect of split-year treatment

The overseas part is treated as a period of non-UK residence only for the provisions to which the split-year rules apply. UK-source income may remain taxable, and particular rules can produce a different result for employment income, Capital Gains Tax, attributed gains or income, and temporary non-residence.

Annual allowances and rate bands are not automatically divided according to the length of each part. The applicable charging provision must be used to determine which receipts, earnings, profits or gains belong in the UK calculation.

International and FIG interactions

A split year remains a full year of UK residence when testing whether an individual is a qualifying new resident under the foreign income and gains (FIG) regime. Treaty residence is also considered separately: a double taxation agreement may affect taxing rights without changing the domestic SRT result.
