Savings and dividend income

Savings income and dividend income are classified separately from non-savings income.

Savings income

Savings income can include interest from bank and building-society accounts, corporate bonds, ordinary debentures and private loans; peer-to-peer interest; interest distributions; purchased-life-annuity elements; and certain life-policy gains. Interest on cash held within an Individual Savings Account (ISA) is exempt from Income Tax and does not use the Personal Savings Allowance.

Deeply discounted securities

A deeply discounted security is a government or commercial debt security under which the amount payable on redemption can be substantially more than its issue price. The discount represents all or part of the investor’s return. If the statutory conditions are met, the profit made when the security is sold, redeemed, exchanged, gifted or converted is charged to Income Tax for the tax year of disposal instead of being treated as a capital gain. Broadly, the profit is the amount received less the amount paid, subject to detailed computational, exception and loss rules.

Accrued Income Scheme

An interest-bearing security may be sold between interest-payment dates. Part of its sale price may then represent interest that has built up since the previous payment date. The Accrued Income Scheme uses accrued income profits and losses to allocate that interest between the seller and buyer, so each is taxed by reference to the period for which they held the security rather than taxing the buyer on the whole of the next interest payment.

The scheme has exclusions. The frequently applicable small-holdings exclusion applies where the aggregate nominal value of all Accrued Income Scheme securities held at any time in the tax year or the preceding tax year did not exceed £5,000. It does not apply to deeply discounted securities, which are governed by their own rules.

Interest received by an ordinary investor is generally savings income. Different rules may apply where lending forms part of a financial trade, such as banking or moneylending, because the interest may instead form part of the business’s trading profits. Different rules can also apply where tax law treats the return on an unusually structured company security as a distribution. These specialist cases depend on the nature and terms of the investment.

Dividend income

Dividend income is generally a return paid by a company to a shareholder because the shareholder owns shares in the company. The most familiar example is a cash dividend, through which a company distributes part of its profits to its shareholders.

A shareholder may also be an employee, director or lender to the company. A payment for work is employment income, and interest on money lent to the company is savings income. A repayment of share capital may be a capital receipt. The reason for the payment must therefore be identified instead of assuming that every payment to a shareholder is a dividend.

Tax law also treats some less common amounts as dividend income, including taxable stock dividends and certain amounts arising when a close company releases or writes off a loan to a shareholder.
