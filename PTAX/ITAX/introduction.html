Introduction

Income Tax is an annual tax on income, but not every receipt is taxable and not every taxable amount is charged in the same way. A useful starting point is to identify:

  • Who receives the income
  • What type of income it is
  • Which tax year it belongs to
  • Which UK rules apply

Who receives the income

The main focus is the Income Tax liability of an individual. An individual may be an employee, a company director, a sole trader, a partner, a landlord, a saver, an investor, a pension recipient, or a beneficiary of a trust or deceased estate. One person can have several of these roles in the same tax year. Trustees and personal representatives can also have Income Tax responsibilities, but their own liabilities are covered separately; this study note explains how trust and estate income can enter an individual beneficiary’s calculation.

The individual’s UK residence status must also be established for the relevant tax year. It affects whether the starting scope generally includes worldwide income or only particular UK-source income. The Statutory Residence Test determines domestic-law residence; split-year treatment, double taxation agreements, temporary non-residence and the foreign income and gains regime may then affect the tax treatment.

What type of income it is

Common sources include employment earnings and benefits, self-employment profits, pensions and taxable state benefits, property income, savings interest, dividends, trust and estate income, foreign income, and certain gains that legislation treats as income. Some receipts are exempt, while allowances or reliefs can reduce the amount charged.

For the 2026/27 Income Tax calculation, taxable income is generally stacked in the following order:

  • Non-savings, non-dividend income, meaning income that is neither savings income nor dividend income, such as employment income, pensions, trading profits and property income.
  • Savings income, principally taxable interest. It also includes amounts that legislation specifically classifies as savings income, such as certain life-policy gains, profits from deeply discounted securities and accrued-income-scheme charges.
  • Dividend income, including taxable dividends and other qualifying company distributions.

Which tax year it belongs to

Income Tax is charged for a tax year running from 6 April to the following 5 April. The tax year 2026/27 runs from 6 April 2026 to 5 April 2027.

The correct year depends on the timing rule for the particular source. Cash receipt, entitlement, accrual, payment or another statutory event may determine the result. See Income recognition for the cash and accruals bases, source-by-source recognition rules and income beginning part-way through a tax year.

Which UK rules apply

These notes principally follow United Kingdom Income Tax rules. Scottish taxpayers use Scottish rates and bands for most non-savings, non-dividend income, while savings and dividends use UK-wide rates. Welsh Income Tax is partly devolved, although the combined Welsh rates for 2026/27 are aligned with those for England and Northern Ireland. Residence, foreign income, treaty and special-regime questions can require additional rules.
