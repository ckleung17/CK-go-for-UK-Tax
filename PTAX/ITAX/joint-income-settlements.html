Why ownership and income attribution matter

Introduction / overview

Income does not always belong for tax purposes to the person whose name appears on an account or who physically receives the money. Property may be owned by more than one person, or one person may provide property or income for somebody else. The tax system therefore needs rules that identify who genuinely benefits and who should include the income in their tax calculation.

The current framework combines general ownership principles with special rules for property held jointly by spouses or civil partners and for arrangements that redirect income to another person. It covers income from assets such as savings, investments and land, as well as income arising through gifts, trusts and wider family arrangements. The people affected can include co-owners, couples, parents, children, trustees and anyone who has provided the property.

The intended outcome is to tax income in line with real economic ownership, provide a simple default for many couples and prevent a person from avoiding tax merely by diverting income while retaining an interest in the underlying arrangement. This page explains the general Income Tax position for 2026–27; property law, trust terms and the facts of each transfer can change the result.

Start with beneficial ownership

Legal ownership records whose name the property is held in and who can deal with it. Beneficial ownership describes who is entitled to enjoy the property and its income. Income is normally taxed on the beneficial owner, unless a specific statutory rule attributes it to somebody else.

The account name, title register, purchase funding, written agreements and conduct of the parties can all be evidence. Who receives or spends the cash is relevant but does not by itself settle the question.

Illustrative example: joint owners who are not a couple

Assume two adult siblings hold an investment jointly. A written declaration records that Noor owns 70% of both the investment and its income, while Sam owns 30%. The investment produces £2,000 of income in 2026–27.

  1. Noor’s share: £2,000 × 70% = £1,400.
  2. Sam’s share: £2,000 × 30% = £600.
  3. Outcome: because they are not spouses or civil partners, the special 50:50 rule does not apply. On the stated assumptions, each reports the income matching their beneficial entitlement.

Evidence matters

This example assumes the document is valid and reflects the parties’ true rights. Simply placing an asset in joint names or arranging for income to be paid into another person’s account does not necessarily transfer beneficial ownership.

Spouses and civil partners living together

Where spouses or civil partners live together and hold property in their joint names, section 836 of the Income Tax Act 2007 generally treats them as entitled to the income in equal shares for Income Tax. This is commonly called the 50:50 rule. It can apply even where their underlying beneficial interests are unequal.

If the couple genuinely owns both the property and its income in the same unequal proportions, they can make a joint declaration to HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) using Form 17. The form does not create or change ownership and is not an election for any convenient percentage: it tells HMRC about the actual unequal interests that already exist.

Form 17 cannot be used while the couple holds the beneficial interest as joint tenants, because joint tenants do not own separate unequal beneficial shares. For land in England and Wales, the beneficial joint tenancy must first be validly severed and the couple must hold as tenants in common in documented unequal shares. Property-law terminology and severance procedures differ in Scotland and Northern Ireland, so local legal advice may be needed.

Form 17 is an Income Tax declaration, not a Capital Gains Tax (CGT) election. However, the actual beneficial ownership proportions evidenced with the form will normally also determine how a gain or loss is attributed between the owners on a later disposal. A later change in ownership, a no-gain/no-loss transfer between spouses or civil partners, and reliefs available on disposal must be considered separately.

Illustrative example: unequal rental-property interests

Assume spouses living together own a rental property as tenants in common, with valid evidence that Alex owns 75% of both the property and its income and Jo owns 25%. The property produces £12,000 of taxable profit in 2026–27.

  1. Without a valid Form 17 declaration: the 50:50 rule attributes £6,000 to Alex and £6,000 to Jo.
  2. Actual 75:25 entitlement: £12,000 × 75% = £9,000 for Alex; £12,000 × 25% = £3,000 for Jo.
  3. With a valid declaration: income arising on or after the declaration date is taxed using the evidenced £9,000 and £3,000 shares.

Form 17 has strict conditions

Both individuals must sign the declaration, provide evidence of the unequal beneficial interests and ensure that HMRC receives it within 60 days of the declaration date. HMRC has no power to extend that deadline. The income shares must match the property shares, the declaration applies only to the assets listed, and it does not normally change the treatment of earlier income.

When the 50:50 rule does not apply

Important exclusions include partnership trading or professional income, distributions from jointly held shares in a close company, property held with an additional owner, income to which neither person is beneficially entitled and income attributed differently by another tax rule. Couples who are permanently separated are generally taxed on their actual entitlement instead. Each exception has detailed conditions.

When the settlements legislation can redirect income

A settlement has a wider tax meaning than a formal trust. Section 620 of the Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005 includes dispositions, trusts, covenants, agreements, arrangements and transfers of assets. The courts have limited this broad wording by requiring an element of bounty: in plain English, the arrangement must contain gratuitous provision, such as giving value without full commercial consideration, rather than being an ordinary arm’s-length commercial transaction. The person who provides the bounty is called the settlor.

If the settlor, their spouse or their civil partner can benefit from the property or income, the settlor may have retained an interest. Section 624 of the Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005 can then treat the settlement income as the settlor’s for Income Tax, even if somebody else receives it.

An important exception can apply to an outright gift between spouses or civil partners. The recipient must receive the property and the whole income without conditions or any route for the property to benefit the giver, and the property must not be wholly or substantially only a right to income. Giving only rental income while retaining the property, for example, does not satisfy that exception.

Family-company shares and dividend waivers

In Jones v Garnett (the “Arctic Systems” case), the House of Lords accepted that the arrangements included bounty but held that an outright gift of an ordinary share to a spouse fell within the spouse exception. The share carried ordinary voting, capital and dividend rights and was not wholly or substantially only a right to income. This does not protect every family-company arrangement. A dividend waiver or special share structure that redirects dividends to a lower-taxed family member, particularly where the same dividend could not have been paid on all shares, may be a bounteous arrangement and the income may be attributed back to the person providing the benefit.

Income provided for a minor child

A separate rule can apply where a parent provides property and the resulting income belongs to, is paid to or is used for a child or stepchild who is under 18 and is neither married nor in a civil partnership. If the relevant settlement income exceeds £100, the income may be treated as the parent’s rather than the child’s.

Assume a parent gives investments to their 15-year-old child and the investments produce £120 of income in 2026–27:

  1. Relevant income: £120.
  2. Compare with the threshold: £120 exceeds £100.
  3. Outcome: if the parental-settlement rule applies, the full £120 is treated as the parent’s income, not only the £20 above the threshold.

The £100 rule applies to income arising from property provided directly or indirectly by a parent or step-parent. It does not apply merely because a grandparent, aunt, uncle, other relative or friend makes a genuine gift from their own resources. Income from such a non-parental gift normally belongs to the child and is assessed as the child’s income, so the child may use their own Personal Allowance and, where applicable, their Personal Savings Allowance or Dividend Allowance. Reciprocal gifts or arrangements that disguise a parent as the true provider can still be challenged.

Settlement cases are fact-sensitive

Bare trusts, discretionary trusts, interest-in-possession trusts, dividend waivers, family-company shares and reciprocal arrangements can produce different outcomes. The parental rule also depends on who provided the property, the child’s age and relationship, how the income is applied and whether another exception applies.

A practical review sequence

  1. Identify the income source and the asset that produces it.
  2. Establish the legal and beneficial owners using deeds, declarations, contribution records and other evidence.
  3. Check whether the spouses’ or civil partners’ 50:50 rule applies and whether a valid Form 17 declaration is needed.
  4. Consider whether a gift, trust or wider arrangement is a settlement and whether income must be attributed back to the settlor or parent.
  5. Report the income in the correct shares and retain the supporting documents.

When to seek human review

Obtain specialist tax and, where ownership is uncertain, legal review before relying on an unequal split involving land, trusts, company shares, dividend waivers, minor children, overseas assets or undocumented family arrangements.
