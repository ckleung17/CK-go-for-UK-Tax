What is devolved

For the tax year from 6 April 2026 to 5 April 2027, Scotland and Wales can set Income Tax rates for the non-savings, non-dividend income of their taxpayers. This category generally includes employment, pension, trading and property income. The UK Parliament continues to set the Personal Allowance, its withdrawal above £100,000 adjusted net income, and the rates for savings and dividend income.

Mixed-income ordering: under section 16 ITA 2007, non-savings income is treated as the lowest part of taxable income, savings income sits above it and dividend income is the highest part. Scottish or Welsh non-savings, non-dividend income therefore consumes the lower bands first and can push savings or dividend income into higher UK-wide rate bands. Devolution changes the rates applied to the non-savings, non-dividend slice; it does not create a separate stack of tax bands for that slice.

Taxpayer status follows statutory residence and connection rules, not nationality, an employer’s location or where work is performed. A person who moves within the UK, has more than one home or has no identifiable home may need a whole-tax-year test based on their main place of residence and, in some cases, day counting. For Scotland, the close-connection test in section 75A of the Scotland Act 1998 provides that, where an individual has places of residence in Scotland and elsewhere in the UK, Scotland is the close connection if their main place or places of residence are in Scotland for at least as much of the year as in any other single part of the UK and for more of the year than in at least one other part. If no close connection is established, the statutory day-counting test generally looks for more days in Scotland than in any other single part of the UK. These tests are fact-sensitive and include special rules. An S tax-code prefix normally indicates Scottish treatment and a C prefix Welsh treatment, but the underlying facts determine status; taxpayers should keep HMRC informed of address changes.

Scotland

Scottish rates apply only to a Scottish taxpayer’s non-savings, non-dividend income. HMRC publishes the following 2026/27 income ranges; the lower thresholds reflect the standard £12,570 Personal Allowance:

Band Published income range Rate
Starter£12,571 to £16,53719%
Basic£16,538 to £29,52620%
Intermediate£29,527 to £43,66221%
Higher£43,663 to £75,00042%

Pensions and Gift Aid: these use UK basic-rate mechanisms even for Scottish taxpayers. Under pension relief at source, the provider normally reclaims 20% basic-rate relief from HMRC and adds it to the pension pot. A charity likewise reclaims Gift Aid by reference to the UK basic rate, although Gift Aid is a separate regime rather than a pension relief-at-source arrangement. A Scottish starter-rate taxpayer taxed at 19% keeps the 20% pension relief without HMRC recovering the 1% difference; a Scottish basic-rate taxpayer receives the standard 20% relief. To obtain the full marginal relief available on qualifying gross contributions or donations, a taxpayer with income taxed at the intermediate, higher, advanced or top rate must claim the additional 1%, 22%, 25% or 28% respectively, limited to the amount taxed at that rate. The claim is made through Self Assessment where the individual files a return, or otherwise by contacting HMRC so that relief can be dealt with through the tax code or HMRC’s claim process. Gift Aid remains subject to the donor having enough Income Tax and/or Capital Gains Tax to cover the charity’s reclaim.

How to read the table: the statutory Scottish band limits are applied to income after allowances: £3,967, £16,956, £31,092, £62,430 and £125,140. The first four published entry points add the standard Personal Allowance, but that shortcut is not valid throughout the higher ranges. The Personal Allowance is reduced by £1 for every £2 of adjusted net income above £100,000 and is nil at £125,140. A different, transferred or tapered allowance therefore requires the statutory taxable-income calculation.

Illustrative Scottish calculation

Assume fictional taxpayer Isla is a Scottish taxpayer in 2026/27 with £50,000 employment income, the standard Personal Allowance and no other income, deductions or reliefs. Her taxable non-savings, non-dividend income is £50,000 − £12,570 = £37,430:

  1. Starter band: £3,967 × 19% = £753.73.
  2. Basic band: £12,989 × 20% = £2,597.80.
  3. Intermediate band: £14,136 × 21% = £2,968.56.
  4. Higher band: the remaining £6,338 × 42% = £2,661.96.
  5. Total Scottish Income Tax before any tax reductions: £8,982.05.

Wales

For 2026/27, the Senedd set each Welsh rate at 10 pence in the pound. Under the statutory Welsh Rates of Income Tax (WRIT) mechanism, the UK basic, higher and additional rates are each reduced by 10 percentage points for Welsh taxpayers and the corresponding Welsh rate set by the Senedd is then added. The 10p Welsh rates therefore produce the same combined rates as England and Northern Ireland for 2026/27. The Senedd must set the Welsh rates by resolution for each tax year, so maintaining that parity requires an annual vote rather than occurring automatically:

Band Published income range Combined rate
Basic£12,571 to £50,27020%
Higher£50,271 to £125,14040%
AdditionalOver £125,14045%

The Welsh rate is a component of the combined rate, not an extra 10% charge on top of 20%, 40% or 45%. The lower published entry points reflect the standard Personal Allowance. Above £100,000 adjusted net income, allowance tapering changes the taxable-income calculation, and a different or transferred allowance can also change the effective entry points.

Illustrative Welsh calculation

Assume fictional taxpayer Carys is a Welsh taxpayer in 2026/27 with £60,000 employment income, the standard Personal Allowance and no other income, deductions or reliefs. Taxable income is £47,430:

  1. Basic-rate band: £37,700 × 20% = £7,540.
  2. Higher-rate amount: £47,430 − £37,700 = £9,730.
  3. Higher-rate tax: £9,730 × 40% = £3,892.
  4. Total Welsh Income Tax before any tax reductions: £11,432.
