Why life-policy gains matter

Introduction / overview

Some life-insurance products combine financial protection with investments that may grow over many years. Because ordinary rules for bank interest or selling investments do not fit these products neatly, a separate tax regime developed and is now mainly contained in the Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005. Its purpose is to bring accumulated investment growth into the Income Tax calculation when value is taken from a policy or the policy comes to an end, while recognising that tax may already have been borne within a UK policy fund.

The rules cover certain long-term insurance and investment contracts and can be triggered when a policy ends, money is withdrawn or ownership rights are transferred for value. The person taxable on any resulting gain is the main taxpayer, the insurer normally supplies the information needed, and policy beneficiaries or other owners may be affected in more complex cases. The intended outcome is to tax investment growth at the appropriate time without taxing the life-protection element and, where several years of growth are taxed together, to offer eligible individuals relief from an increase in tax caused by bunching several years of growth into one tax year.

This page explains the general position for individuals in the 2026–27 tax year. It introduces the relevant terms only after this overview, then shows a simplified gain calculation and the relief that may apply. Policy terms, event dates, ownership and residence history can change the result.

When a chargeable event can arise

The rules can apply to life-insurance policies, investment contracts that pay a set amount at a future date without depending on a person’s death (capital-redemption policies), and certain contracts that provide regular payments for life (life-annuity contracts).

A chargeable event is an event specified by the legislation that can trigger a calculation under the special policy-gain rules. A positive result is called a chargeable-event gain. Despite the word “gain”, it is generally taxed as savings income under Income Tax rules, not as a capital gain under Capital Gains Tax rules.

Common chargeable events include:

  • the policy maturing or all rights under it being surrendered;
  • a death that gives rise to benefits under a life-insurance policy;
  • all policy rights being assigned to someone else for money or something else of value;
  • certain part surrenders, part assignments or policy loans; and
  • certain excess events and annual events for policies that let the holder select or influence the underlying investments (personal portfolio bonds).

The cumulative 5% tax-deferred allowance

For many non-qualifying policies, part surrenders are tested against a cumulative allowance based on 5% of each premium for each insurance year, for no more than 20 years for each premium. Unused allowance normally carries forward. Withdrawals within the available allowance do not create an immediate gain, but they are tax-deferred, not tax-free: they are taken into account when the policy ends. If cumulative part withdrawals exceed the available allowance, the excess normally creates a chargeable-event gain at the end of that insurance year. Assignments and transactions in the final insurance year can follow different rules.

Do not assume that an event always creates taxable income

The event starts a statutory calculation; it does not guarantee that the result is positive or taxable. Some long-term regular-premium policies are qualifying policies, meaning policies that meet detailed statutory conditions and may produce no chargeable-event gain in many circumstances. Part withdrawals, assignments, loans, policy variations and qualifying-policy status are fact-sensitive and should be checked against the insurer’s records.

How the gain enters the tax calculation

The insurer will normally issue a chargeable-event certificate showing the event, the gain, any Income Tax treated as paid and the number of years used for possible top-slicing relief. Use the certificate rather than treating the difference between cash received and premiums paid as the answer in every case.

For a straightforward maturity or full surrender, the broad idea is:

Total policy benefits − allowable premiums and other statutory deductions − earlier gains = chargeable-event gain

The detailed calculation can include earlier capital payments, policy loans, assignments and gains already brought into charge. On death, the calculation normally uses the policy’s surrender value immediately before death rather than the full death benefit, which excludes the life-protection element from the gain calculation.

Deficiency relief when a policy ends

A final surrender, maturity, death or other event that ends a policy can sometimes produce a negative result after earlier part-withdrawal or assignment gains are taken into account. This is called a deficiency. An individual may qualify for deficiency relief where earlier gains from the same policy were included in their income and they have income taxed at a relevant higher rate in the final year. The relief is limited by those earlier gains and reduces tax on specified higher-rate income; it does not turn the loss into a general deduction and cannot reduce tax charged at the additional or dividend additional rate.

Illustrative calculation

Assume an individual fully surrenders a UK investment bond in 2026–27. They paid one premium of £40,000, receive £55,000 on surrender and had no earlier withdrawals, loans, assignments or chargeable-event gains.

  1. Total benefit: £55,000.
  2. Less allowable premium: £55,000 − £40,000 = £15,000.
  3. Less earlier gains: none.
  4. Illustrative chargeable-event gain: £15,000.

This example is deliberately simple. The insurer’s certificate and the statutory calculation control the reportable figure.

UK and foreign policies

The full taxable gain generally enters the individual’s total income and adjusted net income for the year, even if top-slicing relief later reduces the tax bill. Adjusted net income broadly means total taxable income before Personal Allowances, less specified reliefs and with certain statutory adjustments. Including the full gain can trigger or increase the High Income Child Benefit Charge where the relevant conditions are met. It can also reduce the Personal Allowance once adjusted net income exceeds £100,000, with the allowance withdrawn by £1 for every £2 above that level. Gains from most policies issued by UK insurers are treated as if basic-rate Income Tax has already been paid. That notional tax cannot be repaid, even if the individual’s final liability would otherwise be lower. A foreign-policy gain will commonly have no UK tax treated as paid, although UK-branch and other exceptions exist.

Why top-slicing relief may help

A policy may grow gradually for years but produce one taxable gain when it ends. Adding the whole gain to a single year’s income can move part of it into a higher tax band even though an equal amount of growth arising each year might not have done so. Top-slicing relief is a tax reducer designed to mitigate that bunching effect for eligible individuals.

For a foreign policy, an individual who was non-UK resident for part of the relevant ownership period may first qualify for time-apportioned reduction (TAR). Broadly, TAR reduces the chargeable gain by the proportion of qualifying foreign days in the material-interest period. The detailed rules differ for some policies issued before 6 April 2013, assignments and shared ownership. TAR is applied before top-slicing relief, and the number of years used for the slice must also be adjusted for relevant periods of non-UK residence.

The calculation broadly:

  1. calculates the tax attributable to the full gain in the actual tax computation;
  2. divides the gain by the relevant number of years to find an annual equivalent, often called the slice;
  3. recalculates the notional tax result using that slice. If the full gain reduced or removed the Personal Allowance, the allowance is recalculated using total income with only the slice included, so some or all of it may be reinstated for this relief calculation. The starting rate for savings and personal savings allowance are also recalculated where the rules require; and
  4. compares the results to determine whether a tax reduction is due.

Illustrative slice

Assume a certificate shows a £50,000 gain and 10 relevant complete years.

  1. Full gain entering income: £50,000.
  2. Annual equivalent: £50,000 ÷ 10 = £5,000.
  3. Relief comparison: compare the tax attributable to the full £50,000 with the notional result based on a £5,000 slice.
  4. Outcome: any calculated relief reduces the Income Tax liability; the taxable gain reported for the year remains £50,000.

Important limits

Top-slicing relief is available only to individuals, not companies, trustees or personal representatives. It does not divide the gain across earlier tax years and does not reduce the full gain used for adjusted net income or other income-linked tests. Foreign policies and periods of non-UK residence can alter both the gain and the relevant number of years.

What to do when an event occurs

  1. Keep the insurer’s chargeable-event certificate and check the policy number, event date, gain, tax treated as paid and number of years.
  2. Confirm who is taxable, whether the policy is UK or foreign, and whether ownership, trust, assignment or residence issues apply.
  3. Report the figures in the appropriate Self Assessment section. Use the version of HMRC helpsheet HS320 for the relevant tax year for UK policies, or HS321 for foreign policies.
  4. Check whether the Self Assessment calculation has given top-slicing relief where appropriate. HMRC’s system calculates it automatically in most cases when the required figures are entered correctly.

Human review is recommended for complex cases

Seek specialist review for foreign policies, trusts, deceased policyholders, assignments, large or repeated part withdrawals, policy loans, qualifying-policy questions, periods of non-UK residence or a result that differs from the insurer’s certificate.
