Allowances and deductions - 2026/27

Home Income Tax Capital Gains Tax PAYE National Insurance Pensions Tax

After income has been classified and recognised for the tax year, the relevant deductions, allowances and tax reductions must be applied in the correct order. They do not all reduce the Income Tax calculation in the same way.

  • A deduction reduces income at the stage specified by its particular rule.
  • An allowance, such as the Personal Allowance, may be deducted when taxable income is calculated.
  • A 0% rate band, such as the Personal Savings Allowance or dividend allowance, leaves the income within taxable income but charges it at 0%. The income still uses rate-band capacity.
  • A tax reduction, such as Marriage Allowance for the recipient, is deducted from the Income Tax liability after the relevant income has been taxed.

This page introduces the principal personal, couple-related, savings and dividend provisions for 2026/27. Each provision has its own eligibility conditions and may interact with the taxpayer’s income level and other reliefs.

Allowances

  • Personal Allowance: £12,570, tapered by £1 for every £2 adjusted net income over £100,000.

    Example: Alex has adjusted net income of £110,000 for 2026/27, is entitled to the standard Personal Allowance before tapering and has no transferred allowance.

    1. Adjusted net income above £100,000: £110,000 − £100,000 = £10,000.
    2. Reduction in the Personal Allowance: £10,000 ÷ 2 = £5,000.
    3. Remaining Personal Allowance: £12,570 − £5,000 = £7,570.

    Alex can therefore deduct a Personal Allowance of £7,570 when calculating taxable income for 2026/27.

  • Marriage Allowance: allows one spouse or civil partner to give up £1,260 of their Personal Allowance for 2026/27 so that the other receives a tax reduction of up to £252. The principal statutory conditions are:
    • the individuals are married or in a civil partnership for all or part of the tax year;
    • the transferring spouse or civil partner is entitled to a Personal Allowance, makes the required election and, after the reduction, is not liable above the applicable permitted rates;
    • the recipient is also not liable above the applicable permitted rates; for a Scottish taxpayer, the starter, basic and intermediate rates are permitted, but the higher, advanced and top rates are not; and
    • neither person receives Married Couple’s Allowance for the same tax year.

    The maximum tax reduction is £252: £1,260 × 20%. The transfer does not increase the recipient’s Personal Allowance. Instead, the transferor’s Personal Allowance is reduced from £12,570 to £11,310. If the transferor’s net income is fully covered by the reduced allowance, the transfer creates no taxable income for them. If their net income exceeds £11,310, income previously covered by the allowance may become taxable. The election may still be legally valid provided the transferor satisfies the applicable rate conditions, although the overall financial benefit may be reduced or eliminated. The actual tax depends on the amount and type of income and any other available reliefs or 0% rate bands. HMRC’s simplified guidance says that the transferor will normally have income below the Personal Allowance.

    Married Couple’s Allowance is a separate relief for qualifying marriages or civil partnerships where at least one person was born before 6 April 1935. A person satisfying that birth-date condition is at least 91 throughout 2026/27. The individual receiving the relief can be younger because the birth-date condition may instead be satisfied by their spouse or civil partner. For 2026/27 the allowance’s maximum is £11,700 and minimum is £4,530, with relief at 10%.

  • Blind Person’s Allowance: £3,250. Eligibility differs across UK jurisdictions; unused allowance may be transferred to a spouse or civil partner under the conditions.

Savings allowances and rates

For 2026/27, there are two possible scenarios:

  • Unused Personal Allowance: If non-savings income does not use all the Personal Allowance, the unused amount can cover savings income. Only savings income left after the Personal Allowance is considered for the starting rate for savings and the Personal Savings Allowance.
  • Non-savings income remaining after allowances: The starting-rate band for savings is up to £5,000 and is reduced by £1 for every £1 of taxable non-savings income remaining after allowances. No starting-rate band is available if that taxable non-savings income is £5,000 or more.

After the starting-rate band, the Personal Savings Allowance charges up to £1,000 of savings income at 0% for a basic-rate taxpayer, £500 for a higher-rate taxpayer and nil for an additional-rate taxpayer.

Rate-band position: Savings income is taxed after non-savings income and therefore uses the basic, higher and additional-rate bands remaining after the non-savings income. For 2026/27, savings income is charged at 20%, 40% or 45% according to the band in which it falls.

Dividend allowance and rates

For 2026/27:

  • Dividend Allowance: £500, operating as a 0% rate band.
  • Rate-band position: Dividend income is taxed after non-savings and savings income and therefore uses the rate-band capacity remaining after both, but the separate dividend rates below apply.
  • Dividend ordinary rate: 10.75%.
  • Dividend upper rate: 35.75%.
  • Dividend additional rate: 39.35%.

Ordering and exemptions

Savings and dividend income generally form the highest slices of income, with dividend income above savings income. Amounts within the Personal Savings Allowance or dividend allowance still use rate-band capacity because the allowances operate as 0% rate bands. Qualifying Individual Savings Account income is exempt.

Scottish and Welsh rates do not apply to savings or dividend income. The UK savings and dividend rules apply after the individual’s income has been classified and ordered.
