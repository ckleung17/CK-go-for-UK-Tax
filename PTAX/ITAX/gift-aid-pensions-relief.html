Gift Aid, pension contributions and adjusted net income

Why Gift Aid and pension tax relief matter

Gift Aid is a scheme that allows a charity or community amateur sports club to reclaim basic-rate Income Tax on a qualifying cash donation. You give the charity a Gift Aid declaration, pay the cash amount, and the charity normally claims 25p from HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC) for every £1 you donate.

Relief at source (RAS) is a way of giving tax relief on personal pension contributions. You pay a contribution after tax, and the pension provider claims a basic-rate top-up from HMRC and adds it to your pension pot. It is used by all personal and stakeholder pensions and by some workplace pensions.

In both cases, the charity or pension receives an amount equivalent to basic-rate tax at 20%. If you pay Income Tax above 20%, you may be entitled to further personal relief. The tax calculation gives that further relief by extending the relevant tax bands. A qualifying payment can also reduce adjusted net income (ANI), meaning total taxable income before Personal Allowances, less specified reliefs and with certain statutory adjustments.

Important: scope of the rates on this page

The 20%, 40% and 45% examples use the 2026–27 non-savings, non-dividend Income Tax rates for England, Wales and Northern Ireland. Scottish taxpayers have different earned-income bands and rates, although Gift Aid and RAS pension contributions can still produce further relief. Savings and dividend income also use separate rates.

The mechanics: why payments are “grossed up”

The cash you pay is treated as the amount left after basic-rate tax has been taken from a larger amount. That larger figure is the gross amount: the value before the 20% basic-rate element. Grossing up puts your payment and HMRC’s top-up onto the same basis for the tax calculation.

At a 20% basic rate, your net payment represents 80% of the gross amount:

Net payment ÷ 0.8 = gross amount

Step Calculation Amount
1. You pay Net payment £800
2. Gross up £800 ÷ 0.8 £1,000
3. Basic-rate element £1,000 − £800 £200

For Gift Aid, the charity claims the £200. For an RAS pension, the pension provider claims it and adds it to your pot. The £1,000 gross amount, rather than the £800 cash payment, is used for the band and ANI adjustments described below.

How the gross amount can reduce your tax

Effect 1: extending the tax bands

A qualifying Gift Aid donation or RAS pension contribution increases the basic-rate limit and, where relevant, the higher-rate limit by its gross amount. This gives further relief to a taxpayer whose income would otherwise be taxed above the basic rate.

  • If £1,000 would otherwise be taxed at 40%, extending the basic-rate band can move it into the 20% band. The personal saving is £200: the 20 percentage-point difference.
  • If £1,000 would otherwise be taxed at 45%, extending the higher-rate limit can move it into the 40% band. The personal saving is £50: the 5 percentage-point difference.

The relief is limited by the amount of income actually taxed at the higher rate concerned. A payment does not create a repayment at 40% or 45% if you did not pay enough tax at those rates. Claim through Self Assessment if you file a tax return. If you do not, claim Gift Aid relief by contacting HMRC directly and asking it to amend your tax code; for an eligible current-year RAS pension claim, you can use HMRC’s online pension tax-relief service with the same sign-in details used for your Personal Tax Account, or contact HMRC.

Effect 2: lowering adjusted net income

ANI is used for several tax thresholds. In broad terms, start with income from all taxable sources before Personal Allowances, then deduct the gross amount of qualifying Gift Aid donations and RAS pension contributions. The detailed statutory calculation contains other deductions and an add-back, so ANI is not always the same as salary or taxable income.

Lower ANI can have practical benefits in 2026–27:

  • Personal Allowance: the standard allowance is reduced by £1 for every £2 of ANI above £100,000 and is fully withdrawn at £125,140. For example, reducing ANI from £100,800 to £99,800 with a £1,000 gross qualifying payment prevents the £400 allowance reduction that would otherwise arise.
  • High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC): this is a tax charge linked to the higher earner’s ANI where that person or their partner receives Child Benefit. For 2026–27, it starts above £60,000. Between £60,000 and £80,000, the charge is 1% of the total Child Benefit received for every £200 of ANI above £60,000; at £80,000 or more, it equals the full Child Benefit amount. Reducing ANI within this range therefore directly reduces the percentage charged. For example, reducing ANI from £60,800 to £59,800 can take the person below the starting threshold, subject to the full HICBC conditions.

Warning: do not deduct the same pension contribution twice

The separate ANI deduction described here is for grossed-up RAS contributions. Under a net pay arrangement, meaning the employer deducts an employee contribution before calculating Income Tax, the lower taxable pay is already reflected in the starting income figure. Under salary sacrifice, meaning the employee gives up contractual cash pay in return for an employer pension contribution, the reduced taxable pay is also reflected through payroll. Neither amount is grossed up and deducted again as an RAS payment.

Worked example A: extending the basic-rate band

This illustrative example assumes the taxpayer lives in England, Wales or Northern Ireland throughout 2026–27, has salary of £60,000 and no other income, deductions or reliefs, receives the standard £12,570 Personal Allowance, and makes an £800 qualifying Gift Aid donation.

  1. Gross the donation up: £800 ÷ 0.8 = £1,000.
  2. Calculate taxable income: £60,000 − £12,570 = £47,430.
  3. Without the donation: the first £37,700 is taxed at 20%, leaving £9,730 taxed at 40%.
  4. With the donation: the £37,700 basic-rate limit expands by £1,000 to £38,700. Only £8,730 is then taxed at 40%.
Income Tax calculation Without Gift Aid With Gift Aid
Tax at 20% £37,700 × 20% = £7,540 £38,700 × 20% = £7,740
Tax at 40% £9,730 × 40% = £3,892 £8,730 × 40% = £3,492
Total Income Tax £11,432 £11,232

Result: the taxpayer’s Income Tax falls by £200. Separately, the charity receives the £800 donation and claims £200 from HMRC, giving it £1,000 in total. The taxpayer’s personal relief is obtained through the tax calculation or a claim; the charity does not pass its £200 reclaim back to the donor.

Worked example B: Gift Aid warning for a non-taxpayer

Warning: the Gift Aid tax condition

The donor must pay enough UK Income Tax and/or Capital Gains Tax for the tax year to cover the tax reclaimed on all of their Gift Aid donations. It is the donor’s responsibility to tell the charities if they no longer pay enough tax.

  1. A non-taxpayer donates £800 and makes a Gift Aid declaration.
  2. The charity treats this as a gross donation of £800 ÷ 0.8 = £1,000.
  3. The charity claims £200 from HMRC.
  4. If the donor paid no qualifying UK Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax for that tax year, there is a £200 shortfall. HMRC may ask the donor to pay that amount.

Important: Gift Aid and pensions differ for non-taxpayers

Do not apply the Gift Aid warning automatically to an RAS pension. A person with no earnings can normally still receive basic-rate pension relief on a net contribution of up to £2,880 per tax year, producing a gross contribution of £3,600, provided the pension-relief conditions are met. Pension tax-relief limits and the annual allowance must be checked separately.

Check the pension contribution method

The “divide by 0.8” mechanism on this page applies specifically to RAS pension contributions. Check your payslip, pension statement, employer or provider before claiming further relief.

  • Relief at source: you pay the net amount after tax; the provider claims 20% basic-rate relief and adds it to the pension. Further relief may need to be claimed.
  • Net pay arrangement: your employee contribution is deducted from pay before Income Tax is calculated, so relief at your applicable rate is normally given through payroll.
  • Salary sacrifice: you agree to lower contractual cash pay and the employer pays into the pension. It is not your RAS contribution and must not be grossed up or claimed again.

Warning: check your circumstances before relying on the result

These examples are educational, not personal tax advice. Eligibility, the amount of further relief, ANI and the final tax result depend on the tax year, residence, income types, pension method, contribution limits and other circumstances. Gift Aid qualification rules and pension tax-relief limits must also be satisfied.
