Tax Avoidance, Abuse and Anti-Evasion

This study area distinguishes lawful tax planning, tax avoidance, abusive tax arrangements, tax evasion, civil investigation of suspected tax fraud and criminal tax offences. These expressions are not interchangeable: the legal result depends on the facts, the relevant tax legislation, judicial interpretation and the procedure used by HM Revenue and Customs (HMRC).

  • Lawful tax planning uses choices and reliefs in the way Parliament intended.
  • Tax avoidance generally describes arrangements that seek a tax advantage within the wording of legislation but may be defeated by purposive interpretation or anti-avoidance rules.
  • Abusive arrangements are the narrower statutory focus of the General Anti-Abuse Rule (GAAR), including its double-reasonableness test.
  • Tax evasion involves dishonest conduct to evade tax and may lead to criminal investigation and prosecution.
  • Civil tax-fraud investigation includes HMRC’s use of Code of Practice 9 and the Contractual Disclosure Facility; it is a civil investigation route, not a separate category of tax.
  • Criminal tax offences depend on the particular enacted offence and its required elements.

Status at 20 July 2026: this index covers enacted regimes and separately identifies proposals or provisions whose commencement must be checked. The UK Mandatory Disclosure Rules replaced most UK DAC6 reporting from 28 March 2023, but historic or otherwise residual DAC6 questions may still need review. Finance Act 2026 introduced further measures aimed at promoters; their commencement and any associated regulations must be checked before applying them.

Study modules

Interpretation and statutory counteraction

  • targeted anti-avoidance rules and statutory purpose tests;
  • the Ramsay line of authority and purposive statutory interpretation;
  • the GAAR, the double-reasonableness test, procedural safeguards, GAAR Advisory Panel opinions and counteraction; and
  • appeals and the relationship between specific anti-avoidance rules, purposive interpretation and the GAAR.

Disclosure and promoter regimes

  • Disclosure of Tax Avoidance Schemes (DOTAS), including notifiable arrangements, scheme reference numbers and promoter or user obligations;
  • Disclosure of Avoidance Schemes for VAT and Other Indirect Taxes (DASVOIT);
  • the UK Mandatory Disclosure Rules and residual or historic DAC6 obligations where relevant;
  • Promoters of Tax Avoidance Schemes (POTAS), including conduct notices and monitoring notices;
  • stop notices, universal stop provisions, promoter action notices and publication of promoters, suppliers and schemes;
  • information notices directed at avoidance promoters and connected persons;
  • civil penalties and enacted or proposed criminal offences for non-compliance, with the legal and commencement status checked for the relevant date; and
  • penalties for enablers of defeated tax avoidance arrangements.

Users of defeated arrangements and disputed tax

  • follower notices and the effect of relevant judicial rulings;
  • Accelerated Payment Notices and Partner Payment Notices;
  • the serial tax-avoidance regime and penalties for repeatedly defeated arrangements;
  • penalties for defeated arrangements;
  • disguised-remuneration and loan-charge rules; and
  • representations, payment consequences, appeals and other statutory safeguards.

Offshore compliance and international transparency

  • offshore asset moves and offshore-transfer penalties;
  • the Requirement to Correct and Failure to Correct;
  • penalties concerning offshore income, assets and gains;
  • the Common Reporting Standard, Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act information and automatic exchange of information; and
  • reasonable excuse, knowledge, due diligence, time limits and territorial scope.

Evasion, fraud and enforcement

  • the corporate offences of failure to prevent the facilitation of UK and foreign tax evasion;
  • interaction with anti-money-laundering supervision and suspicious-activity reporting duties;
  • publication of deliberate tax defaulters;
  • Code of Practice 9, the Contractual Disclosure Facility and civil investigation of suspected tax fraud;
  • HMRC criminal investigation policy, prosecution referrals and the distinction between civil and criminal routes;
  • HMRC information, inspection and data-gathering powers; and
  • privilege, taxpayer safeguards, statutory review, tribunal appeals and reasonable excuse.

Each module will identify the tax, person, conduct, territorial scope and relevant period. It will distinguish a disclosure duty from a rule that changes the tax result, and a payment-on-account mechanism from a final determination of liability.

Sources checked 20 July 2026. Legislation and binding judicial decisions determine the law. HMRC guidance and manuals explain HMRC’s interpretation and administration but are not themselves the legislation. Consultation documents and draft measures are not law unless and until enacted and commenced.
