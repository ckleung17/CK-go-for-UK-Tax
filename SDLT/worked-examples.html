Worked examples

This page collects practical SDLT examples showing how the rules apply to common transactions. Select an example below.

Example A: first home bought jointly by a couple

Peter is young and has been living away from his parents in rented accommodation for several years since he started working. In 2026, he has saved enough money to buy a new-build house near the town centre for £350,000 and expected to complete in April. He will also pay £1,500 legal fees and has budgeted£50,000 for furniture. The house will be jointly owned by Peter and Ann (who is an Itilian living in Rome), and they will live there as their home after marriage.

The SDLT calculation is based on the chargeable consideration for the land transaction. Peter’s own legal fees are not normally included because they are paid for legal services. The furniture budget is also not included if the furniture is bought separately or consists of free-standing furniture rather than fixtures forming part of the property.

Therefore, the SDLT calculation is based on the house price of £350,000. (For current rules, first-time buyer relief is only available where the purchase price is no more than £500,000. If the price is more than £500,000, HMRC says the buyer cannot claim the relief and must pay SDLT at the standard residential rates on the total purchase price.)

Assumption: Because the house is jointly owned, both Peter and Ann must be first-time buyers and must intend to occupy the property as their only or main residence. If Ann has previously owned a dwelling anywhere in the world, first-time buyer relief is not available.

Band Rate SDLT
First £300,000 0% £0
Remaining £50,000 5% £2,500
Total SDLT £2,500

If Ann is not a first-time buyer

If Ann has previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling, first-time buyer relief is not available for the whole purchase. The standard residential rates would apply instead.

Band Rate SDLT
First £125,000 0% £0
Next £125,000 2% £2,500
Remaining £100,000 5% £5,000
Total SDLT £7,500

If Ann is non-UK resident before marriage

Ann is Italian and lives in Rome before the marriage. Italian nationality does not itself decide the SDLT result. The question is whether Ann is UK resident for SDLT purposes at the effective date of the purchase.

If Peter and Ann complete the purchase before they are married, Ann is tested separately. If Ann has not spent enough days in the UK, the 2% non-resident surcharge may apply to the whole purchase price.

Element Calculation SDLT
First-time buyer relief £300,000 at 0%, plus £50,000 at 5% £2,500
2% non-resident surcharge £350,000 at 2% £7,000
Total SDLT payable on completion £9,500

Note: If Ann later moves to the UK and satisfies the SDLT 183-day residence test within the permitted period, Peter and Ann may be able to claim a refund of the 2% non-resident surcharge.

If they complete after marriage

If Peter and Ann are already married or civil partners at completion, Peter is UK resident for SDLT purposes, and they are not separated, the spouse or civil partner rule may prevent the 2% non-resident surcharge from applying.

Memory point: Marriage after completion does not automatically remove the surcharge. If the surcharge was paid, the practical follow-up is to check whether Ann later satisfies the SDLT 183-day test and then claim repayment.

Example B: moving from Hong Kong and selling the old home later

Two sisters have lived in Hong Kong since birth and jointly own a residential flat there as their only home. They bought a new-build house in England in November 2023 for £370,000. They moved to the UK in February 2024 and have lived in the UK house since then. In July 2026, they sold the Hong Kong flat for HK$5 million and made a 20% profit.

The UK house purchase in November 2023 was an SDLT transaction. The later sale of the Hong Kong flat is not an SDLT transaction, but it may allow them to claim a refund of the higher-rates element paid on the UK purchase.

SDLT position at completion in November 2023

The sisters were not first-time buyers because they already owned a residential flat in Hong Kong. Because they still owned the Hong Kong flat when they bought the UK house, the higher rates for additional dwellings applied initially.

Band Rate SDLT
First £250,000 3% £7,500
Remaining £120,000 8% £9,600
Higher-rates SDLT £17,100

Possible 2% non-resident surcharge

Because both sisters were living in Hong Kong at completion, the 2% non-resident surcharge may also have applied. The surcharge applies on top of the residential SDLT rates.

Element Calculation SDLT
Higher-rates SDLT As calculated above £17,100
2% non-resident surcharge £370,000 × 2% £7,400
Total SDLT paid at completion £24,500

Note: Visa status or passport type does not decide SDLT residence. The SDLT non-resident surcharge uses a physical day-count test.

Follow-up after moving to the UK

If each sister spent at least 183 days in the UK in a qualifying continuous 365-day period around the completion date, they may have been able to claim a repayment of the 2% non-resident surcharge. The claim must be made within 2 years of the effective date of the purchase.

Possible non-resident surcharge refund: £370,000 × 2% = £7,400.

Follow-up after selling the Hong Kong flat in July 2026

If the Hong Kong flat was their previous only or main residence, and the UK house became their new only or main residence, they may claim a refund of the higher-rates element because the old home was sold within 3 years of buying the new home.

The refund claim should normally be made within 12 months of the sale of the Hong Kong flat, because that is likely to be later than 12 months after the SDLT filing date for the UK house.

Refund Calculation Amount
Higher-rates refund £370,000 × 3% £11,100
Possible non-resident surcharge refund £370,000 × 2% £7,400

Final SDLT cost if both refunds are claimed in time

Item Amount
Initial SDLT paid £24,500
Less higher-rates refund (£11,100)
Less non-resident surcharge refund (£7,400)
Final SDLT cost £6,000

Memory point: The Hong Kong sale does not create SDLT, but it can trigger an SDLT refund claim because it was the sale of the previous main residence.

Separate tax point: the Hong Kong sale may need to be considered for UK Capital Gains Tax if the sisters were UK resident when they sold it.
