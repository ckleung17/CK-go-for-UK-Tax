Worked examples

These fictional examples apply Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) rules for land transactions in England. Each example follows the same sequence so that the reader can trace the facts, assumptions, tax analysis, calculation, deadline and final outcome. Rates and deadlines are applied at the stated effective date, not at today’s date.

  • Start with the effective date, purchaser, property type and chargeable consideration.
  • Test reliefs and surcharges before applying the relevant rate bands.
  • Calculate SDLT in slices and show any later repayment separately.
  • Check the filing, payment and repayment deadlines.
  • Treat every result as illustrative and verify the actual legal and factual documents.

Example A: first home bought jointly by a couple

Peter and Ann will jointly buy a new-build house in England for £350,000, completing on 30 April 2026. Peter will pay £1,500 for his own legal services and they budget £50,000 for separately purchased free-standing furniture. Ann is Italian and lives in Rome before the purchase. They intend to occupy the house as their only or main residence.

Assumptions

  • Both purchasers have never acquired a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world.
  • The £350,000 price is entirely for the house and its fixtures.
  • The furniture is acquired separately and is not part of the land transaction.
  • Peter is UK resident for SDLT; Ann has not yet satisfied the SDLT day-count test.
  • They are not married or civil partners at completion unless stated otherwise.

Analysis sequence

  • The transaction is a residential acquisition in England on 30 April 2026.
  • Both joint purchasers satisfy the first-time buyer conditions on the assumptions.
  • The £350,000 price does not exceed the relief ceiling of £500,000.
  • Peter’s legal costs and separately bought furniture are excluded.
  • Ann is tested separately for the 2% surcharge because they are not married at completion.

The SDLT calculation is based on the chargeable consideration for the land transaction. Peter’s own legal fees are not normally included because they are paid for legal services. The furniture budget is also not included if the furniture is bought separately or consists of free-standing furniture rather than fixtures forming part of the property.

Therefore, the SDLT calculation is based on the house price of £350,000. (For current rules, first-time buyer relief is only available where the purchase price is no more than £500,000. If the price is more than £500,000, HMRC says the buyer cannot claim the relief and must pay SDLT at the standard residential rates on the total purchase price.)

Assumption: Because the house is jointly owned, both Peter and Ann must be first-time buyers and must intend to occupy the property as their only or main residence. If Ann has previously owned a dwelling anywhere in the world, first-time buyer relief is not available.

Band Rate SDLT
First £300,000 0% £0
Remaining £50,000 5% £2,500
Total SDLT £2,500

If Ann is not a first-time buyer

If Ann has previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling, first-time buyer relief is not available for the whole purchase. The standard residential rates would apply instead.

Band Rate SDLT
First £125,000 0% £0
Next £125,000 2% £2,500
Remaining £100,000 5% £5,000
Total SDLT £7,500

If Ann is non-UK resident before marriage

Ann is Italian and lives in Rome before the marriage. Italian nationality does not itself decide the SDLT result. The question is whether Ann is UK resident for SDLT purposes at the effective date of the purchase.

If Peter and Ann complete the purchase before they are married, Ann is tested separately. If Ann has not spent enough days in the UK, the 2% non-resident surcharge may apply to the whole purchase price.

Element Calculation SDLT
First-time buyer relief £300,000 at 0%, plus £50,000 at 5% £2,500
2% non-resident surcharge £350,000 at 2% £7,000
Total SDLT payable on completion £9,500

Note: If Ann later moves to the UK and satisfies the SDLT 183-day residence test within the permitted period, Peter and Ann may be able to claim a refund of the 2% non-resident surcharge.

If they complete after marriage

If Peter and Ann are already married or civil partners at completion, Peter is UK resident for SDLT purposes, and they are not separated, the spouse or civil partner rule may prevent the 2% non-resident surcharge from applying.

Memory point: Marriage after completion does not automatically remove the surcharge. If the surcharge was paid, the practical follow-up is to check whether Ann later satisfies the SDLT 183-day test and then claim repayment.

Outcome comparison

Facts at completionRelief and surchargeFinal SDLT
Both first-time buyers; married and spouse rule applies First-time Buyers’ Relief; no 2% surcharge £2,500
Both first-time buyers; unmarried; Ann non-resident First-time Buyers’ Relief plus 2% surcharge £9,500
Ann previously owned a dwelling; none retained; married and spouse rule applies No First-time Buyers’ Relief; standard rates £7,500

Example B: moving from Hong Kong and selling the old home later

Two sisters lived in Hong Kong from birth. They jointly bought a residential flat for HK$3.8 million and occupied it from January 1998 as their only home. They bought a new-build house in England in November 2023 for £370,000. They moved to the UK in February 2024 and have lived in it as their home since then. Each sister receives annual taxable pension income equal to 80% of the UK personal allowance. In July 2026, they sold the Hong Kong flat for HK$5 million.

Assumptions

  • Each sister owns an equal 50% beneficial interest in both homes.
  • The Hong Kong flat was each sister’s only or main residence until February 2024.
  • The English house became their replacement only or main residence.
  • No non-resident surcharge repayment was claimed by the November 2025 deadline.
  • Each sister qualifies for the four-year FIG regime in 2026/27.
  • The sterling values, ownership months and absence periods used below are illustrative.

Analysis sequence

  • Calculate SDLT at the November 2023 rates, including both residential surcharges.
  • Test the later Hong Kong sale for the replacement-main-residence repayment.
  • Check the separate, earlier deadline for the non-resident surcharge repayment.
  • Compute each sister’s foreign gain in sterling, not by subtracting HK dollar figures.
  • Compare FIG relief with Private Residence Relief after allowing for forfeited allowances.

The UK house purchase in November 2023 was an SDLT transaction. The later sale of the Hong Kong flat is not an SDLT transaction, but it may allow them to claim a refund of the higher-rates element paid on the UK purchase.

SDLT position at completion in November 2023

The sisters were not first-time buyers because they already owned a residential flat in Hong Kong. Because they still owned the Hong Kong flat when they bought the UK house, the higher rates for additional dwellings applied. They were also non-UK resident for SDLT because neither had been present in the UK for 183 days during the 12 months before completion.

Band Rate SDLT
First £250,000 3% £7,500
Remaining £120,000 8% £9,600
Higher-rates SDLT £17,100

SDLT paid at completion

The 2% non-resident surcharge applied on top of the 2023 higher residential rates.

Element Calculation SDLT
Higher-rates SDLT As calculated above £17,100
2% non-resident surcharge £370,000 × 2% £7,400
Total SDLT paid at completion £24,500

Note: Visa status or passport type does not decide SDLT residence. The SDLT non-resident surcharge uses a physical day-count test.

Follow-up after moving to the UK

Each sister appears capable of satisfying the 183-day test after moving to the UK in February 2024. However, a repayment application had to be made within 2 years of the November 2023 effective date. The comparison below assumes that no claim was made by November 2025, so the £7,400 repayment is no longer available. If a timely claim was made, reduce both final columns by £7,400.

Practical check: confirm from the conveyancer and HMRC records whether the non-resident surcharge repayment was claimed before the deadline.

Follow-up after selling the Hong Kong flat in July 2026

If the Hong Kong flat was their previous only or main residence, and the UK house became their new only or main residence, they may claim a refund of the higher-rates element because the old home was sold within 3 years of buying the new home.

The higher-rates refund claim must reach HMRC by the later of 12 months after the sale and 12 months after the SDLT return filing date. On these facts, the deadline will normally be the exact date in July 2027 that is 12 months after sale.

Refund Calculation Amount
Higher-rates refund £370,000 × 3% £11,100
Possible non-resident surcharge refund £370,000 × 2% £7,400

The SDLT result is the same under both Capital Gains Tax routes. For this illustrative comparison, assume the HK$3.8 million acquisition cost converts to £301,587 in January 1998 and the HK$5 million disposal proceeds convert to £500,000 in July 2026. Assume no deductible costs and equal ownership by the two sisters. Also assume each sister is a qualifying new resident in her fourth UK-resident tax year after at least 10 consecutive tax years of non-UK residence.

The assumed ownership-period breakdown is:

  • Total ownership period: January 1998 to July 2026, treated as 342 complete months.
  • Occupation period: 313 months of the ownership period.
  • Final-period exemption: the final 9 months ending on the July 2026 disposal date.
  • Total period qualifying for Private Residence Relief: 322 months (313 occupation months plus the final 9 months).

Exact acquisition, occupation and disposal dates are needed to confirm the periods and number of qualifying months. Actual UK Capital Gains Tax must use the exchange rate and allowable costs applicable on each relevant date.

Private Residence Relief applies to the qualifying part of the total gain. The 313 months of occupation and the final 9 months give 322 qualifying months out of 342 complete months of ownership.

Example C: husband buys UK home before moving from Hong Kong

A Hong Kong couple and their young son planned to move to the UK in 2024. The husband bought a new-build house in England in his sole name for £450,000 and completed the purchase on 30 October 2024. The wife and son moved to the UK in November 2024, but the husband stayed in Hong Kong for some time to deal with his business. On 15 March 2025, the husband sold their former main home in Hong Kong and moved to the UK.

Assumptions

  • The husband is the sole purchaser and beneficial owner of the English house.
  • The Hong Kong home was his previous only or main residence.
  • The English house was acquired as his replacement only or main residence.
  • He owned no other dwellings after selling the Hong Kong home.

Analysis sequence

  • At completion he owns the Hong Kong home, so the then 3% additional-dwelling rates apply.
  • He has not met the SDLT day count, so the separate 2% surcharge also applies.
  • Selling the former main home within three years can support a higher-rates refund.
  • His later UK day count can support a separate non-resident surcharge repayment.
  • Each repayment has its own deadline and must be claimed separately.

SDLT position at completion

The UK house purchase is an SDLT transaction. The husband was not a first-time buyer because he already owned a residential home in Hong Kong. Because he still owned the Hong Kong home when he completed the UK purchase, the higher rates for additional dwellings applied initially.

Band Rate SDLT
First £250,000 3% £7,500
Remaining £200,000 8% £16,000
Higher-rates SDLT £23,500

2% non-resident surcharge

At completion, the husband was still living in Hong Kong and had not yet spent enough days in the UK to be treated as UK resident for SDLT purposes. Because he was the sole purchaser, the wife and son moving to the UK later does not by itself remove the non-resident surcharge.

Element Calculation SDLT
Higher-rates SDLT As calculated above £23,500
2% non-resident surcharge £450,000 × 2% £9,000
Total SDLT paid at completion £32,500

Note: Family members moving to the UK does not decide the SDLT residence position where the husband is the sole purchaser. The key point is the husband’s own UK day count.

Follow-up 1: claim refund of the higher-rates element

In March 2025, the husband sold the Hong Kong residential home. If that home was his previous only or main residence, and the UK house was intended to be his new only or main residence, he may claim a refund of the higher-rates element.

Refund Calculation Amount
Higher-rates refund £450,000 × 3% £13,500

Deadline outcome: the later deadline was normally 15 March 2026. As at 16 July 2026, treat the £13,500 refund as unavailable unless the claim was submitted in time or HMRC accepts that a statutory exception applies.

Follow-up 2: claim refund of the 2% non-resident surcharge

After moving to the UK on 15 March 2025, the husband should keep records of his UK presence. If he spends at least 183 days in the UK in a qualifying continuous 365-day period within the permitted SDLT window, he may claim a refund of the 2% non-resident surcharge.

Refund Calculation Amount
Non-resident surcharge refund £450,000 × 2% £9,000

Evidence: keep flight records, passport entry records, boarding passes, bank or card records, utility bills, work records and other evidence showing the husband’s presence in the UK. If the 183-day test is satisfied, the amendment must be made by 30 October 2026.

Final outcome comparison

Outcome Refunds Final SDLT
No claims made by 16 July 2026 £0 £32,500
Higher-rates claim made by 15 March 2026 only £13,500 £19,000
Non-resident claim made by 30 October 2026 only £9,000 £23,500
Both claims made within their deadlines £22,500 £10,000

Memory point: At completion, the husband pays SDLT based on the facts at that date. Later UK residence and sale of the old Hong Kong home may allow separate refund claims.

Date warning: If completion was on 31 October 2024 or later and no transitional rule applied, the additional-dwelling surcharge would be 5%, not 3%.

Example D: takeover of Chinese takeaway with mixed-use lease

Wong’s family keeps their residential building in Hong Kong before moving to the UK. They arrive in the UK in March 2024 and decide to take over an existing Chinese takeaway business in Southampton in October 2024.

The deal includes an assignment of the lease of a 3,000 square feet two-storey building. The kitchen and reception areas are on the ground floor, and the residential area is on the upper floor. The two floors are more or less the same size.

Wong’s family pays £300,000 to take over the lease for the remaining 30 years. The annual rent is £1,000. They also pay £100,000 for kitchen equipment, furniture and fixtures, including an external kitchen chimney with an estimated residual value of £5,000. They move in and carry on the business in November 2024.

Assumptions

  • The effective date of the lease assignment is 1 November 2024.
  • The ground floor is commercial and the upper floor is suitable for use as a dwelling.
  • The £300,000 premium is paid for the existing lease assignment.
  • For this illustration, the £5,000 chimney is assumed to pass with the lease as a fixture; the other £95,000 is assumed to be genuine moveable chattels.
  • No VAT is payable because the transfer-of-a-going-concern conditions are met.

Analysis sequence

  • Classify the land as mixed residential and non-residential property.
  • Use the mixed-use rates; residential surcharges do not apply.
  • Separate the lease premium and fixtures from chattels on a just and reasonable basis.
  • Apply the bands to the resulting £305,000 chargeable consideration.
  • Recalculate if the fixture allocation or VAT treatment changes.

Type of SDLT transaction

This is an assignment of an existing lease. The property is mixed-use because it includes both commercial takeaway premises and residential accommodation. The non-residential / mixed-use SDLT rates apply.

Note: The family’s Hong Kong residential property does not make this UK transaction subject to the residential higher rates, because the UK transaction is mixed-use rather than wholly residential.

Chargeable consideration

For an assignment of an existing lease, SDLT is normally calculated on the lease assignment price. The ongoing annual rent is not separately charged using the NPV rent rules.

Payments for genuine moveable chattels, such as ordinary furniture or removable equipment, are not normally part of SDLT chargeable consideration. However, payments for fixtures forming part of the land must be included.

For this illustration, the external kitchen chimney is treated as a fixture. In practice, attachment alone is not decisive: the degree and purpose of annexation, the lease terms and any tenant’s right to remove a trade fixture must be examined. On the stated assumption that the remaining equipment and furniture are moveable chattels, the chargeable consideration is:

Item Amount Included for SDLT?
Lease assignment price £300,000 Yes
External kitchen chimney fixture £5,000 Yes
Moveable kitchen equipment, furniture and chattels £95,000 No, assuming they are genuine moveable chattels
Chargeable consideration £305,000 Used for SDLT calculation

SDLT calculation

The non-residential / mixed-use SDLT rates are applied to the chargeable consideration of £305,000.

Band Rate SDLT
First £150,000 0% £0
Next £100,000 2% £2,000
Remaining £55,000 5% £2,750
Total SDLT £4,750

If more items are fixtures

If more of the kitchen equipment or fittings are legally fixtures, their value must also be included in SDLT chargeable consideration. For example, if the whole £100,000 is treated as fixtures, the chargeable consideration would be £400,000.

Band Rate SDLT
First £150,000 0% £0
Next £100,000 2% £2,000
Remaining £150,000 5% £7,500
Total SDLT £9,500

Follow-up action

  • File the SDLT return for the lease assignment.
  • Pay SDLT within 14 days of the effective date.
  • Keep the lease assignment deed, completion statement and rent details.
  • Keep evidence that the building is mixed-use.
  • Keep a clear valuation split between the lease, fixtures, chattels, stock and goodwill.

Outcome and sensitivity

ScenarioChargeable considerationFinal SDLTReason
Stated assumption£305,000 £4,750Premium plus £5,000 fixture
All equipment is fixtures£400,000 £9,500No chattels exclusion
VAT payable on land considerationFact-dependent RecalculateLand-related VAT is included for SDLT

Memory point: Mixed-use lease assignment uses non-residential SDLT rates. For an assigned lease, SDLT is calculated on the lease price. Rent NPV is normally relevant to new leases, not ordinary assignments of existing leases.

VAT point

VAT must be checked separately from SDLT. The key question is whether Wong’s family is buying separate assets, or whether they are taking over the takeaway business as a transfer of a going concern, often called TOGC Transfer of a Going Concern. This means a business is transferred as a continuing business. If the TOGC conditions are met, the transfer is normally outside the scope of VAT and the seller should not charge VAT. .

If the TOGC conditions are met, the seller should not normally charge VAT on the transfer of the business assets. This may be relevant because Wong’s family takes over the existing takeaway business and continues trading from the same premises.

Land and buildings have additional TOGC conditions. If the seller’s interest would be taxable because of an option to tax, the buyer may need to opt to tax, notify HMRC and give the seller the required notification by the relevant date. Business continuity alone does not establish TOGC treatment.

If TOGC does not apply, VAT may be charged on some parts of the transaction. For example, kitchen equipment and business assets may be standard-rated if sold by a VAT-registered seller. The lease assignment or business premises may be exempt from VAT unless the seller has opted to tax the property. The residential accommodation is usually VAT exempt.

Note: For SDLT, any VAT payable on the land transaction is included in the SDLT chargeable consideration. Therefore, if VAT is charged on the lease assignment or fixtures that form part of the land transaction, the SDLT calculation may increase. VAT on genuine moveable chattels does not normally increase SDLT if those chattels are properly excluded from the land transaction.

Example E: partnership buys office and residential flats, then incorporates

Jack and Jones each contributed £50,000 to start a partnership 6 years ago. They share profits equally. The business became very profitable in the last two years.

In March 2024, the partnership purchased an office unit in a multi-storey building for £500,000. In January 2025, the partnership purchased two residential flats: a smaller flat for £350,000 and a larger flat for £510,000. The seller gave a 5% discount because both flats were bought in one lot. One flat is rented out for £1,500 per month, and the other is used as staff accommodation for the sales manager.

Jack and Jones plan to incorporate the business by January 2026, with each holding the same percentage of shares in the new company as they held in the partnership.

Assumptions

  • Jack and Jones are the only partners and share income profits 50:50 throughout.
  • The office and flats are acquired for the partnership business in England.
  • The flat prices are pre-discount amounts and the 5% discount applies proportionately.
  • The flats are acquired under two contracts and conveyances forming one arrangement with the same seller; they are therefore assumed to be linked transactions rather than one land transaction.
  • The contracts were exchanged on or after 31 October 2024, so the transitional 3 percentage-point higher-rates supplement does not apply.
  • No Multiple Dwellings Relief transitional contract applies.
  • No figure is assumed for January 2026 market values, secured debt or incorporation consideration.

Analysis sequence

  • Separate the cash contribution, office acquisition, flat acquisitions and incorporation.
  • Apply non-residential rates to the office purchase.
  • Aggregate the linked residential consideration and apply the January 2025 higher rates.
  • Apportion the resulting flat SDLT on a just and reasonable basis.
  • Do not calculate incorporation SDLT until the Schedule 15 facts and market values are known.

Transaction 1: contribution of cash to the partnership

Jack and Jones each contributed £50,000 cash to start the partnership. This is not an SDLT transaction because no land or property interest was transferred.

Transaction 2: office purchase in March 2024

The office unit is non-residential property. The SDLT is calculated using the non-residential rates. Current HMRC non-residential rates are 0% up to £150,000, 2% on the next £100,000, and 5% on the amount above £250,000.

Band Rate SDLT
First £150,000 0% £0
Next £100,000 2% £2,000
Remaining £250,000 5% £12,500
Total SDLT on office £14,500

Transaction 3: two residential flats in January 2025

The two residential flats are dwellings. They were bought together from the same seller with a special 5% discount for buying them in one lot. This suggests that the flat purchases are linked transactions. SDLT should therefore be considered by reference to the aggregate discounted consideration.

The original prices were £350,000 and £510,000, giving a total of £860,000. After the 5% discount, the total chargeable consideration is:

Item Calculation Amount
Original total price £350,000 + £510,000 £860,000
5% discount £860,000 × 5% (£43,000)
Discounted consideration £860,000 - £43,000 £817,000

Note: If the stated prices of £350,000 and £510,000 are already the final discounted prices, then the aggregate consideration would be £860,000 instead of £817,000.

Why the higher residential rates apply

The flats are not being bought as Jack or Jones’s main residence. One flat is rented out and the other is used as staff accommodation. First-time buyer relief is not available because the purchaser is the partnership, and the flats are not being bought by individuals as their own main residence.

The higher residential rates are likely to apply because the partnership is acquiring residential dwellings for partnership purposes, not replacing a main residence. HMRC guidance says that a partner is treated as a joint purchaser of land bought by or on behalf of the partnership, and if the higher rates apply to any partner, the whole purchase is liable to the higher rates.

Note: Multiple Dwellings Relief is not available for this January 2025 purchase because it was generally abolished for transactions completing or substantially performed on or after 1 June 2024.

SDLT calculation on the linked flat purchases

On the assumption that exchange occurred on or after 31 October 2024, the higher-rate surcharge was 5%. The relevant higher residential rates were therefore 5% on the first £250,000 and 10% on the amount above £250,000 up to £925,000.

Band Rate SDLT
First £250,000 5% £12,500
Remaining £567,000 10% £56,700
Total SDLT on linked flats £69,200

Alternative calculation: if the £350,000 and £510,000 figures were already the final discounted prices, the linked consideration would be £860,000 and the SDLT would be £73,500.

Transitional sensitivity: if the contracts were exchanged before 31 October 2024 and all transitional conditions were met, the former 3 percentage-point supplement would apply. SDLT on aggregate consideration of £817,000 would then be £52,860, rather than £69,200.

Apportionment between the two flats

If separate SDLT entries are needed for the two flats, the SDLT should be apportioned on a just and reasonable basis. A simple method is to apportion by the discounted consideration for each flat.

Flat Discounted price Approximate SDLT apportionment
Smaller flat £332,500 £28,163
Larger flat £484,500 £41,037
Total £817,000 £69,200

Transaction 4: planned transfer to a company in January 2026

The planned incorporation is another SDLT-sensitive step. If the partnership transfers the office and the two flats to a new company, that transfer is a land transaction. The SDLT result is not decided simply by saying that Jack and Jones own the company in the same 50:50 proportions.

Partnership transactions are subject to special SDLT rules in FA 2003 Sch 15. The chargeable consideration is determined using a technical calculation called the sum of the lower proportions A Schedule 15 calculation based on relevant owners, corresponding partners and the partnership shares attributable under the statutory rules. .

The company, not its shareholders, acquires the land. Matching Jack and Jones’s 50:50 profit shares to their 50:50 company shareholdings does not establish the sum of the lower proportions or any particular SDLT outcome. The relevant-owner, corresponding-partner, connection, control and attributable-share rules must be applied separately to each chargeable interest, using the partnership and property history.

Note: The 17% corporate residential rate must be considered by reference to the company’s January 2026 acquisition, including the interest transferred, its then market value and the Schedule 15 chargeable consideration. The flat’s January 2025 list price is not determinative. Relief may be available if the property is used in a qualifying property rental business or as qualifying employee accommodation, but the conditions must be checked carefully.

Follow-up action

  • File and pay SDLT for the March 2024 office purchase within 14 days of the effective date.
  • File and pay SDLT for the January 2025 residential flat purchases within 14 days of the effective date.
  • Treat the two flat purchases as linked transactions unless the facts clearly show otherwise.
  • Keep evidence of the 5% discount and how the price was allocated between the two flats.
  • Keep evidence of the use of each flat: rental flat and staff accommodation.
  • Before incorporating in January 2026, obtain specialist SDLT advice on FA 2003 Sch 15, connected persons, market values, debt, 17% corporate-rate relief, and anti-avoidance.
