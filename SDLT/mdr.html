Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR)

Scope: Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects the law and HMRC guidance checked on 16 July 2026.

Important update: MDR was abolished for transactions with an effective date on or after 1 June 2024, subject to limited transitional rules for certain contracts entered into on or before 6 March 2024.

MDR is no longer available for new transactions. Understanding the former relief remains useful when reviewing pre-abolition transactions and contracts within the transitional rules.

How MDR Used to Work

Before its abolition, MDR could apply where two or more dwellings were acquired in a single transaction or linked transactions. Broadly, SDLT attributable to the qualifying dwellings was calculated using their average chargeable consideration and then multiplied by the number of dwellings. Detailed rules also covered non-dwelling consideration, linked transactions and a minimum rate.

Because residential SDLT uses progressive bands, MDR could reduce the tax due. The result depended on the transaction date, purchaser, applicable surcharges and whether each property met the statutory dwelling test. An annexe or other part of a property did not qualify merely because it had a separate label: its use or suitability for use as a single dwelling was fact-sensitive.

Illustrative example: effective date 31 May 2024

Assume an individual buys two qualifying dwellings for total chargeable consideration of £800,000, allocated equally. Assume ordinary residential rates apply, with no higher-rates or non-resident surcharge, no lease rent and no other relief:

  • Without MDR: £250,000 at 0% plus £550,000 at 5% = £27,500.
  • With MDR: average consideration is £800,000 ÷ 2 = £400,000. Tax on that average is £250,000 at 0% plus £150,000 at 5% = £7,500. Multiplying by two gives £15,000.
  • Illustrative saving: £27,500 − £15,000 = £12,500.

This fictional example explains the former calculation only. It does not establish that particular accommodation was a separate dwelling or that MDR was available.

Why Was It Abolished?

The government abolished MDR after an external evaluation found no strong evidence that it was meeting its original objective of supporting investment in residential property and concluded that it was not cost-effective. The government's published reasons included:

  • Limited behavioural effect: the evaluation found little evidence that MDR influenced the number of dwellings purchased or the amount invested.
  • Cost: the tax cost of MDR was estimated at approximately £700 million in 2022–23.

Current Post-Abolition Rules

MDR has not been replaced by a general relief. Two property-classification rules may nevertheless affect a transaction:

  • Six or more dwellings: where six or more separate dwellings are the subject of one transaction involving the transfer of a major interest in, or grant of a lease over, them, section 116(7) of the Finance Act 2003 treats those dwellings as non-residential property. The current non-residential rates have a highest marginal rate of 5%.
  • Mixed property: a transaction involving both residential and non-residential property is charged at non-residential rates. Classification depends on the land and buildings actually acquired; a label or intended future use does not by itself make residential property non-residential.

Transitional rules: MDR can remain available where the transaction completed or was substantially performed before 1 June 2024. It can also remain available for a contract entered into on or before 6 March 2024 even if completion occurred later, unless an exclusion applies. Exclusions include certain post-6 March variations, assignments, exercises of options or pre-emption rights, and subsales or other transactions that change who can call for the conveyance. Special rules apply to linked transactions spanning abolition. Eligibility is fact-sensitive and should be checked against the legislation and HMRC guidance.
