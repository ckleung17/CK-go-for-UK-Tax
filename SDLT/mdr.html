Multiple Dwellings Relief (MDR)

Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) Guidance

IMPORTANT UPDATE: Multiple Dwellings Relief has been completely abolished. The relief was scrapped for all property transactions completing (or substantially performed) on or after 1 June 2024.

While MDR is no longer available for new purchases, understanding how it worked remains helpful because it frequently appears in property histories, ongoing discussions, or older transitional contracts.

How MDR Used to Work

Before its abolition, if you bought more than one residential dwelling in a single transaction (or in a series of linked transactions), MDR allowed you to calculate your SDLT based on the average value of the dwellings, rather than their combined total value.

Because SDLT rates are progressive (the tax rate increases as the property price rises), splitting a large purchase price across multiple smaller averages significantly reduced the total tax bill.

Pre-June 2024 Example:

Imagine buying a large house that included a self-contained "granny annexe" or a separate cottage in the grounds for a total of £800,000:

  • Without MDR: You paid standard residential SDLT on the full £800,000 as a single, high-value purchase.
  • With MDR: HMRC allowed you to treat the transaction as two distinct dwellings worth £400,000 each. You calculated the SDLT for a £400,000 property and multiplied that tax by two, routinely saving tens of thousands of pounds.

Why Was It Abolished?

The government withdrew the relief following an external evaluation which concluded it was not achieving its original purpose (incentivising large-scale investment into the private rented sector). Instead, it faced two major issues:

  • Mass Abuse by Reclaim Agencies: The property market became flooded with unregulated SDLT refund firms targeting regular homebuyers. They convinced individuals to claim retroactively that ordinary parts of a house—such as a detached garage, a large utility room, or a basement—counted as a "separate dwelling" to trigger massive tax refunds, leading to endless legal disputes with HMRC.
  • Cost to the Treasury: The relief was costing the government roughly £700 million a year in lost tax revenue, largely driven by private residential annexe claims rather than commercial housing developments.

Current Post-Abolition Alternatives

Now that MDR is gone, buyers looking to purchase multiple properties or multi-unit buildings generally rely on the remaining statutory rules:

  • The "6 or More" Rule: If you buy six or more separate residential dwellings under a single contract, the transaction automatically qualifies for Non-Residential (Commercial) SDLT rates, which cap out at a maximum of 5% and avoid residential surcharges.
  • Mixed-Use Classification: If the purchase combines residential units with a genuine, integrated commercial element (like a shop with flats above), the entire transaction uses the lower non-residential rates.

Transitional Exception: If a buyer exchanged contracts on or before 6 March 2024, they may still be eligible to claim MDR upon completion, provided the contract has not been modified or varied since that date.
