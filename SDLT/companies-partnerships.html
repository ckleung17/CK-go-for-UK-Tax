Companies and partnerships

Companies transactions

SDLT can apply differently where the purchaser is a company, partnership, trustee, collective investment scheme, or other non-natural person. In particular, a company buying a residential property is generally treated more strictly than an individual buyer. If a company buys a residential property for £40,000 or more, it will usually pay the company higher residential rates, which are the standard residential rates plus a 5% surcharge. If the dwelling costs more than £500,000, a flat 17% rate on the whole purchase price may apply instead, unless a relief is available.

If the company is non-UK resident for SDLT purposes, or if a UK company is controlled by non-UK resident persons, a further 2% non-resident surcharge may also apply to residential property purchases in England and Northern Ireland. This 2% surcharge is added on top of the normal company residential rates.

Reversionary interests and the 21-year lease rule

A reversionary interest is the landlord’s interest in a property where another person already has the right to occupy under an existing lease. The buyer owns the landlord’s interest now, but the right to full possession only comes back later, when the lease ends.

For example, if a company buys the freehold of a flat that is already subject to an 80-year lease, the company has bought a reversionary interest. The leaseholder keeps the right to occupy the flat during the lease, and the company’s full possession only returns when the lease ends.

This matters because the normal company higher residential rates do not usually apply where the company buys an interest that is subject to an existing lease with more than 21 years left to run.

Example: company buys a residential flat for £400,000

Company A buys a residential flat for £400,000. The flat is residential property, the price is more than £40,000, and the interest acquired is not subject to a lease with more than 21 years left. The company higher residential rates normally apply. If the company is non-UK resident for SDLT purposes, the 2% non-resident surcharge is added on top.

Group relief

Group relief (FA 2003 Sch 7) may be available for certain transfers of land and buildings between companies in the same group, where the statutory conditions are met.

Broadly, companies must be in the same 75% group at the effective date of the transaction. The relief must be claimed, and it is subject to restrictions and anti-avoidance rules.

Group relief can also be withdrawn in some cases if the group relationship changes within the relevant period.

Note: Group relief is not automatic. Check the group relationship, commercial purpose, anti-avoidance rules, and possible withdrawal.

Partnership transactions

The Baseline Rule

For Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) purposes, a partnership is not a separate legal entity. HMRC treats a partnership as a transparent collection of individual partners. When a partnership buys property, the transaction is treated as being made by the individual partners collectively.

Standard Acquisitions from Third Parties

When a partnership buys a residential property from an unrelated seller, the SDLT rules depend entirely on who the partners are:

  • Mixed or Corporate Partnerships: If at least one partner is a limited company, the entire partnership is automatically hit with the corporate surcharges. This means they pay the 5% higher rates surcharge under £500,000, or the flat 17% rate over £500,000 if no commercial relief applies.
  • Purely Individual Partnerships: If all partners are individuals, standard residential rules apply. The 5% surcharge for additional properties kicks in only if any single partner already owns a residential property anywhere else in the world.

Transactions Between Partners and Partnerships

Special transparency rules under HMRC SDLT Partnership Guidance (Schedule 15) apply when moving property in or out of a partnership. Instead of market value, HMRC uses a specific formula called the Sum of the Lower Proportions (SLP) to calculate tax.

Moving Property IN (Transfer from Partner to Partnership)

  • The Mechanism: A partner transfers a personally owned property into the partnership portfolio.
  • The Tax Treatment: SDLT is calculated only on the proportion of the property effectively "sold" to the other partners.
  • The Formula: If you own 100% of a property and move it into a partnership where your profit share is 60%, you have only effectively transferred 40% of the asset. SDLT is only charged on that 40% portion of the market value (or relevant consideration).

Moving Property OUT (Transfer from Partnership to Partner)

  • The Mechanism: The partnership distributes a property out to an individual partner.
  • The Tax Treatment: Similar to transfers in, SDLT is reduced by the receiving partner's existing partnership share percentage. If a 25% partner takes full ownership of a partnership property, they only pay SDLT on the 75% they did not previously "own" through the partnership structure.

Transferring Partnership Interests (The Indirect Tax Trap)

You cannot bypass SDLT by simply selling shares or interests in a partnership that owns land.

HMRC applies an anti-avoidance rule: if a partner transfers their partnership interest to a new or existing partner, and that partnership is mainly a property investment partnership, the transaction can trigger a proportionate SDLT charge based on the underlying value of the land.

Partnership incorporates into a company

When a property-owning partnership incorporates into a limited company, a special statutory mechanic under Schedule 15 of the Finance Act 2003 where the Sum of the Lower Proportions (SLP) applies to allow the transaction to be executed with 0% SDLT.

Normally, transferring property into a connected company triggers SDLT on the full market value. However, the partnership rules override this and can reduce the "chargeable consideration" to completely nil.

The 0% SDLT Calculation

The relief works because HMRC applies the Sum of the Lower Proportions (SLP) formula to transfers from a partnership to a connected company. If the individuals who own the partnership transfer the properties to a new limited company, and their shareholdings in the new company exactly mirror their original partnership profit-sharing ratios, the proportion of the property effectively "sold" to outsiders is 0%. Consequently, the SDLT liability drops to zero.

Linked transactions and connected persons

Company and partnership transactions often require a linked transaction check. Transactions may be linked where they form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series involving the same vendor and purchaser, or connected persons (FA 2003 s.108).

Connected persons can include companies under common control, shareholders and their companies, partners, relatives, spouses or civil partners, and trustees in some circumstances. Using different companies or connected persons does not necessarily prevent aggregation or anti-avoidance rules from applying.
