Companies and partnerships

Transfers without an ordinary sale may engage debt or market-value rules; see Transfers of equity and non-sale transactions. Corporate reconstructions and other less common claims are mapped in Specialist reliefs.

Company transactions

SDLT can apply differently where the purchaser is a company, partnership, trustee, collective investment scheme, or other non-natural person. In particular, a company buying a residential property is generally treated more strictly than an individual buyer. If a company buys a residential property for £40,000 or more, it will usually pay the company higher residential rates, which are the standard residential rates plus a 5% surcharge. If the dwelling costs more than £500,000, a flat 17% rate on the whole purchase price may apply instead, unless an exclusion is available (FA 2003 Sch 4A).

If the company is non-UK resident for SDLT purposes, or if a UK company is controlled by non-UK resident persons, a further 2% non-resident surcharge may also apply to residential property purchases in England and Northern Ireland. This 2% surcharge is added on top of the normal company residential rates. Where the flat 17% corporate rate applies, the non-resident surcharge increases that single rate to 19% (FA 2003 Sch 9A).

Reversionary interests and the 21-year lease rule

A reversionary interest is the landlord’s interest in a property where another person already has the right to occupy under an existing lease. The buyer owns the landlord’s interest now, but the right to full possession only comes back later, when the lease ends.

For example, if a company buys the freehold of a flat that is already subject to an 80-year lease, the company has bought a reversionary interest. The leaseholder keeps the right to occupy the flat during the lease, and the company’s full possession only returns when the lease ends.

This matters because the normal company higher residential rates do not usually apply where the company buys an interest that is subject to an existing lease with more than 21 years left to run.

Example: company buys a residential flat for £400,000

Company A buys a residential flat for £400,000. The flat is residential property, the price is more than £40,000, and the interest acquired is not subject to a lease with more than 21 years left. The company higher residential rates normally apply. If the company is non-UK resident for SDLT purposes, the 2% non-resident surcharge is added on top.

Group relief

Group relief (FA 2003 Sch 7) may be available for certain transfers of land and buildings between companies in the same group, where the statutory conditions are met.

Broadly, companies must be in the same 75% group at the effective date of the transaction. The relief must be claimed, and it is subject to restrictions and anti-avoidance rules.

Group relief can also be withdrawn in some cases if the group relationship changes within the relevant period.

Note: Group relief is not automatic. Check the group relationship, commercial purpose, anti-avoidance rules, and possible withdrawal.

Partnership transactions

Baseline rule

For SDLT purposes, an acquisition by or on behalf of a partnership is generally treated as an acquisition by the partners jointly. Schedule 15 then provides special rules for acquisitions by partnerships and for transfers of property or partnership interests involving partners.

Standard acquisitions from third parties

When a partnership acquires residential property, the partners are treated as joint purchasers for relevant SDLT purposes. If any partner would cause the higher rates to apply, the whole acquisition may be charged at those rates. A corporate partner can also bring an acquisition of a higher-threshold interest within the flat 17% corporate regime, but only if the statutory threshold and other conditions are met and no exclusion applies. The result is therefore not determined solely by one partner's legal form.

Transactions between partners and partnerships

The special rules in FA 2003 Schedule 15 apply to certain transfers of property into or out of a partnership. They use market value and a statutory formula involving the sum of the lower proportions (SLP) to calculate chargeable consideration.

Moving property into a partnership

Where FA 2003 Schedule 15 paragraph 10 applies, chargeable consideration is generally a proportion of market value calculated as (100 − SLP)%, where SLP is the sum of the lower proportions. SLP is derived from the statutory rules for relevant owners, corresponding partners, connected persons and partnership shares; it cannot safely be inferred from a single stated profit share.

Moving property out of a partnership

Transfers from a partnership use the separate rules in Schedule 15 paragraphs 18 to 20. The chargeable proportion is not necessarily the simple inverse of the recipient partner's profit share, so the full statutory calculation is required.

Transferring partnership interests

A transfer of an interest in a property-investment partnership may be deemed to be a land transaction under Schedule 15 paragraph 14. The charge depends on whether the transfer is Type A or Type B and on the relevant partnership property. Transfers of interests in other partnerships are generally not land transactions, subject to specific provisions such as paragraphs 17 and 17A.

Partnership incorporation into a company

Incorporating a property-owning partnership into a company is a fact-sensitive Schedule 15 transaction. The FA 2003 Schedule 15 partnership rules , including the sum of the lower proportions, must be applied to the actual ownership and partnership facts. Mirroring the partners' profit shares in the company does not by itself guarantee nil consideration or 0% SDLT. Connected-person rules, relevant-owner and corresponding-partner definitions, anti-avoidance provisions and any available relief must all be considered.

Note: Partnership incorporation can materially affect SDLT liability and requires specialist review before implementation. No nil-liability outcome should be assumed without a complete statutory calculation.

Linked transactions and connected persons

Company and partnership transactions often require a linked transaction check. Transactions may be linked where they form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series involving the same vendor and purchaser, or connected persons (FA 2003 s.108).

Connected persons can include companies under common control, shareholders and their companies, partners, relatives, spouses or civil partners, and trustees in some circumstances. Using different companies or connected persons does not necessarily prevent aggregation or anti-avoidance rules from applying.
