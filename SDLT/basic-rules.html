Basic SDLT Rules

1. What is charged?

SDLT is charged on a land transaction (FA 2003 s.43). A land transaction means the acquisition of a chargeable interest (FA 2003 s.48) in land. Therefore, the focus is on the land or property interest being acquired, not simply on where the contract is signed or where the parties live.

SDLT may still be chargeable even if there is no formal instrument, even if the instrument is executed outside the United Kingdom, and even if no party to the transaction is present or resident in the United Kingdom (FA 2003 s.42(2)). In practical terms, if the transaction involves chargeable land in England or Northern Ireland, SDLT can apply even where the buyer and seller are overseas and the documents are signed overseas.

A chargeable interest (FA 2003 s.48) is widely defined. It includes freehold interests, leasehold interests, undivided shares in land, rights over land, rentcharges, the benefit of covenants or obligations affecting land, beneficial interests such as life interests or reversionary interests, and certain powers over land, such as an executor’s or trustee’s power of appointment. Therefore, SDLT can apply not only to the purchase of a freehold property, but also to many leasehold transactions, such as the grant or assignment of a lease. However, some transactions may be exempt or relieved. For example, where land passes to a beneficiary under a will or intestacy by assent or appropriation, the transaction is generally exempt from SDLT if it is made in satisfaction of the beneficiary’s entitlement and no extra consideration is given, apart from certain secured debts or inheritance tax obligations (FA 2003 Sch 3 para 3A).

Note: SDLT is not a “home buyer tax” only. It applies to land transactions generally. Residential, non-residential and mixed-use properties have different SDLT rate structures.

2. Chargeable transaction

A land transaction is a chargeable transaction (FA 2003 s49) unless it is exempt from charge.

Some interests and transactions are outside the SDLT charge. An exempt interest (FA 2003 s.48(2)) is not a chargeable interest, so dealings in it are not chargeable to SDLT. Exempt interests include a security interest, such as a mortgage; a licence to use or occupy land; a tenancy at will; an advowson, An advowson is the right to appoint a priest or clergy member to a church benefice. For SDLT purposes, it is an exempt interest. a Crown franchise; and a manor. However, the legal substance is important: an agreement described as a licence may still be treated as a lease if, in reality, it gives exclusive occupation.

There are also exempt transactions (FA 2003 Sch 3). Common examples include transactions with no chargeable consideration (Sch 3 para 1), the grant of certain leases of dwellings by registered social landlords / relevant housing providers (Sch 3 para 2), certain transactions connected with divorce, nullity, judicial separation or civil partnership breakdown (Sch 3 para 3), land passing to a beneficiary from a deceased person's estate by assent or appropriation (Sch 3 para 3A), and certain variations of testamentary dispositions (Sch 3 para 4). These should be distinguished from SDLT reliefs: an exempt transaction is generally outside the charge, whereas a relief may need to be claimed in an SDLT return.

3. Chargeable consideration

SDLT is calculated by reference to the chargeable consideration (FA 2003 s50) . The detailed rules are in Schedule 4 (FA 2003 Sch 4) .

Chargeable consideration (FA 2003 s.50; FA 2003 Sch 4) is broadly anything given for the land transaction that is money or money’s worth. It usually means the purchase price paid in cash, but it can also include non-cash consideration, such as goods, works or services, release from a debt, or taking over an existing mortgage or other debt.

Examples of chargeable consideration include: cash paid for a freehold property; a premium paid for the grant or assignment of a lease; rent payable under a new lease; taking over all or part of an existing mortgage; paying compensation to another co-owner when jointly owned land is divided unequally; and the value of works or services provided in exchange for the land transaction. VAT, where chargeable on the transaction, is also included in the chargeable consideration.

Examples of items or situations that are not chargeable consideration include: a genuine gift of land where the recipient gives nothing in return and does not take over any mortgage; land left to a beneficiary under a will, provided no other consideration is given; and an equal physical partition of jointly owned land where each owner receives land equal to their existing share and no balancing payment is made. Moveable items that do not form part of the land, such as carpets, curtains or free-standing furniture, are also normally excluded if a fair market value is allocated to them.

4. Who pays SDLT?

The general rule is that the purchaser is liable to pay SDLT (FA 2003 s.85(1)).

However, Section 85(2) points to special rules for joint purchasers, partners and trustees. Later subsections contain further special rules. Sections 85(2A) and 85(2B) deal with cases where certain housing co-operative relief is withdrawn and a relevant successor is liable for the additional SDLT. Sections 85(3) and 85(4) deal with alternative finance arrangements Alternative finance arrangements are property finance structures, often used for Islamic finance, where a financial institution may technically buy the property and then lease or resell it to the customer. SDLT rules prevent double charges and place later withdrawal liability on the relevant person. where certain reliefs are withdrawn, the additional SDLT is payable by the relevant person rather than by applying the ordinary purchaser rule.

5. Effective date

The effective date (FA 2003 s119) is important because it fixes the timing for SDLT filing and payment.

In a normal purchase, the effective date is usually completion. However, substantial performance (FA 2003 s44) can bring the effective date forward before formal completion.

Where a land transaction is made by contract and is to be completed by a conveyance A conveyance is the legal instrument or step that completes the transfer or grant of the land interest, such as a transfer deed or lease. The contract is the agreement to transfer; the conveyance is the act or document that carries it out. (FA 2003 s.44), SDLT does not normally arise merely because the parties exchange contracts. If the contract is not substantially performed before completion, the contract and the conveyance are treated as one single land transaction, and the effective date is the date of completion (FA 2003 s.44(3)). Completion means completion of the transaction proposed, between the same parties, in substantial conformity with the contract (FA 2003 s.44(10)).

However, if the contract is substantially performed A contract is substantially performed when the purchaser takes possession of the whole or substantially the whole of the property, or when a substantial amount of the consideration is paid or provided. before formal completion, the contract itself is treated as the land transaction, and the effective date is the date of substantial performance (FA 2003 s.44(4)). Substantial performance may occur where the purchaser takes possession of the whole or substantially the whole of the property, or where a substantial amount of the consideration is paid or provided (FA 2003 s.44(5)). For example, receiving the keys and becoming entitled to occupy the property may amount to taking possession. Where consideration is not rent, paying substantially the whole of the price will usually mean paying 90% or more. Where rent is the only consideration, the first rent payment may trigger substantial performance.

6. Filing and payment deadline

For a notifiable transaction (FA 2003 s77) , the purchaser must submit a land transaction return (FA 2003 s76) within 14 days after the effective date. HMRC provides guidance on SDLT online and paper returns, and agents or conveyancers can file through HMRC Stamp Taxes Online.

A notifiable transaction (FA 2003 s.77) is a land transaction that must be reported to HMRC by submitting an SDLT return. A transaction may be notifiable even if no SDLT is actually payable. For example, an acquisition of a major interest in land, such as a freehold or leasehold interest, is generally notifiable unless it falls within a statutory exception (FA 2003 s.77A). The purchaser must deliver a land transaction return for a notifiable transaction (FA 2003 s.76).

Some transactions are not notifiable because they fall within statutory exceptions (FA 2003 s.77A). Examples include an acquisition that is exempt from SDLT under Schedule 3, a non-lease acquisition where the chargeable consideration, together with any linked transactions, is less than £40,000, the grant of a lease for 7 years or more where the premium is less than £40,000 and the relevant rent is less than £1,000, and certain low-value grants, assignments or surrenders of leases.

For a notifiable transaction, the purchaser must normally deliver the SDLT return and pay any SDLT due within 14 days after the effective date (FA 2003 s.76; FA 2003 s.86). The effective date is usually completion, but it may be earlier where the transaction is substantially performed before completion, for example where the purchaser takes possession, the first rent payment is due, or substantially the whole consideration is paid.

If the SDLT return is filed late, an automatic fixed penalty applies. The penalty is £100 if the return is filed up to 3 months after the filing date, and £200 if it is filed more than 3 months after the filing date (FA 2003 Sch 10 para 3). If the return is still not filed within 12 months after the filing date, a further tax-based penalty may apply, which can be up to the full amount of SDLT due (FA 2003 Sch 10 para 4).

The payment of SDLT (FA 2003 s86) is due no later than the filing date for the return.

If SDLT is paid late, interest is charged from the day after the payment deadline until the date of payment (FA 2003 s.87). Late payment should therefore be distinguished from late filing: late filing triggers fixed penalties, while late payment normally gives rise to interest and may also lead to penalties in cases involving underpayment, incorrect returns or failure to notify.
