Reliefs and exemptions

SDLT may be reduced or removed where a transaction is covered by an exemption or a relief.

No cash payment on a transaction does not automatically mean no SDLT. SDLT can still apply if the buyer gives something else of value, such as taking over a mortgage, releasing a debt, providing services, carrying out works, or giving other money’s worth.

1. Difference between exemption and relief

Type Meaning SDLT return?
Exemption The transaction is exempt from SDLT. In many cases, no SDLT is payable and no SDLT return is required. Often no return, but always check the notification rules.
Relief The transaction is within SDLT, but the SDLT is reduced or removed because statutory conditions are met. Usually an SDLT return is required to claim the relief, even if no tax is due.

2. Common exempt transactions

Some transactions are exempt from SDLT and normally do not require an SDLT return. The main statutory exemption provisions are in FA 2003 Sch 3.

  • No chargeable consideration: for example, a genuine gift where no money, goods, services, debt release, mortgage assumption or other consideration is given.
  • Property left by will: property inherited under a will is usually exempt.
  • Divorce or civil partnership dissolution: property transferred because of divorce, separation or dissolution of a civil partnership may be exempt.
  • Freehold purchases under £40,000: no SDLT return is normally required for a freehold purchase where the chargeable consideration is less than £40,000, unless linked transactions change the position.
  • Low-value lease transactions: some leases do not need an SDLT return because the premium and rent are below the SDLT notification limits. The rules depend on the length of the lease, the premium and the rent.
    • For a lease of 7 years or more, no SDLT return is normally required if the premium is less than £40,000 and the annual rent is less than £1,000. If either the premium reaches £40,000 or the annual rent hits £1,000, a return must be filed within 14 days of completion, even if no tax is actually owed.
    • For a lease of less than 7 years, no SDLT return is normally required if the chargeable consideration is below the relevant SDLT nil-rate threshold. This means checking both any premium and the net present value of the rent.
    • For an assignment or surrender of a lease originally granted for 7 years or more, no SDLT return is normally required if the chargeable consideration is less than £40,000. If the consideration is £40,000 or more, an SDLT return may be required, but SDLT is only payable if the consideration exceeds the relevant SDLT tax threshold.
  • Alternative property finance: certain secondary transactions under qualifying alternative finance arrangements may be exempt.

    • Example: Islamic-style home finance

    A wants to buy a home but uses an alternative finance product instead of a normal interest-bearing mortgage. The bank buys the property first and then sells or leases it to A under the finance arrangement.

    Without special SDLT rules, SDLT could arise twice: once when the bank buys the property and again when A acquires the property interest from the bank. The alternative property finance rules reduce or remove the second SDLT charge so that the result is broadly similar to a normal mortgage-funded purchase.

3. Common SDLT reliefs

SDLT reliefs reduce or remove the SDLT charge if the statutory conditions are met. Reliefs usually need to be claimed in the SDLT return.

Relief Simple meaning Study note
First-time buyer relief Relief for qualifying individuals buying their first home to live in as their only or main residence. Not available to companies or buy-to-let investors.
Group relief Relief for certain land transfers between companies in the same group. Anti-avoidance and withdrawal rules must be checked.
Charities relief Relief where a charity buys land for qualifying charitable purposes. Can be withdrawn if conditions stop being met.
Social housing relief Relief for registered providers of social housing or relevant housing providers in qualifying cases. Conditions depend on the provider and funding.
Right to Buy relief SDLT is calculated by reference to the discounted price paid. Applies to qualifying Right to Buy transactions.
Freeport / Investment Zone relief Relief for qualifying land or buildings in designated special tax sites. The land must be used in a qualifying way.
Corporate 17% rate relief Relief from the flat 17% rate for certain company purchases of high-value dwellings. This normally moves the company back to the usual company residential rates.
Multiple Dwellings Relief Historic relief for purchases of two or more dwellings. Generally abolished for transactions completing or substantially performed on or after 1 June 2024, subject to transitional rules.

4. First-time buyer relief

First-time buyer relief (FA 2003 s.57B; FA 2003 Sch 6ZA) may apply where an individual buys their first home and intends to occupy it as their only or main residence.

  • The purchaser must be an individual, not a company.
  • The purchaser must not have previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world.
  • The property must be intended to be occupied as the purchaser’s only or main residence.
  • The relief is not available if the purchase price is over the maximum qualifying limit.

Joint purchasers / co-owners

Where two or more people buy a property together, all purchasers must satisfy the first-time buyer conditions. If one purchaser has previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world, first-time buyer relief is not available for the whole transaction.

  • Each purchaser must be an individual, not a company.
  • Each purchaser must be a first-time buyer.
  • Each purchaser must intend to occupy the property as their only or main residence.
  • If one joint purchaser fails the conditions, the relief is lost for the whole purchase.

Example: A and B buy a home together. A has never owned a dwelling before, but B previously owned a flat overseas. First-time buyer relief is not available, because not all purchasers are first-time buyers.

Cross-reference: First-time buyer relief should also be covered on the residential rates page.

5. Group relief

Group relief (FA 2003 Sch 7) may apply where land is transferred between companies in the same group.

  • The vendor and purchaser must both be companies.
  • They must be in the same qualifying group For SDLT group relief, companies are normally in the same group if one is a 75% subsidiary of the other, or both are 75% subsidiaries of a third company. Broadly, this looks at ownership of ordinary share capital and group control. at the effective date.
  • The relief must be claimed in the SDLT return.
  • The relief may be denied or withdrawn if anti-avoidance rules apply.

Note: Group relief is not automatic. Always check the group relationship, commercial purpose, anti-avoidance rules and possible withdrawal.

6. Charities relief

Charities relief (FA 2003 s.68; FA 2003 Sch 8) may apply where a charity buys land for qualifying charitable purposes.

  • The purchaser must be a charity or qualifying charitable trust.
  • The land must be held for qualifying charitable purposes.
  • The transaction must not be entered into for SDLT avoidance.
  • The relief may be withdrawn if the charity stops meeting the conditions within the relevant period.

Note: If a charity buys jointly with a non-charity, the charity may only claim relief on its own share, if the conditions are met.

7. Relief from the flat 17% company rate

Certain companies and other non-natural persons may be charged a flat 17% rate on the whole purchase price when buying a residential dwelling for more than £500,000. Relief from that 17% rate may be available for qualifying business or commercial uses.

  • property rental business;
  • property development business;
  • property trading business;
  • trade involving making the property available to the public;
  • financial institution lending arrangements;
  • occupation by qualifying employees;
  • qualifying farmhouses; and
  • qualifying housing co-operatives.

Memory point: Relief from the flat 17% rate does not normally mean no SDLT. It usually means the 17% flat rate is switched off and SDLT is calculated using the normal company residential rates.

8. Multiple Dwellings Relief

Multiple Dwellings Relief was a relief for purchases of two or more dwellings in a single transaction or linked transactions. It has now generally been abolished for transactions that complete, or are substantially performed, on or after 1 June 2024.

MDR may still be relevant for historic transactions or transitional cases, for example where contracts were exchanged before the relevant abolition date and the statutory transitional rules apply.

Note: Do not include MDR as a current normal planning relief. Treat it as a historic or transitional relief only.

9. Relief withdrawal and clawback

Some SDLT reliefs can be withdrawn if the conditions stop being met after completion. Where relief is withdrawn, additional SDLT may become payable and a further return may be required.

  • Charities relief can be affected if the property stops being used for qualifying charitable purposes.
  • Group relief can be affected if the companies leave the group within the relevant period.
  • Some corporate 17% rate reliefs can be affected if the property is no longer used for the qualifying business purpose.
  • Some historic reliefs had later adjustment rules, such as changes in the number of dwellings for MDR.

Memory point: Claiming relief is not the end of the matter. Always check whether there is a later withdrawal period or continuing condition.

10. Practical checklist

Before treating a transaction as exempt or claiming relief, check:

  • is the transaction actually within SDLT?
  • is it exempt, or is it a relief that must be claimed?
  • does an SDLT return still need to be filed?
  • what relief code is needed on the SDLT return?
  • are all statutory conditions met at the effective date?
  • are there linked transactions?
  • are there connected persons?
  • is there any market value rule?
  • could the relief be withdrawn later?
  • what evidence should be kept in case HMRC asks questions?

11. Memory point

Exemption = usually no SDLT charge and often no return. Relief = SDLT applies, but a statutory claim reduces or removes the tax. Always check the return requirement, relief code, conditions and possible clawback.
