SDLT Filing and Payment Compliance

Scope: Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) applies to land transactions in England and Northern Ireland. This page reflects the law and HMRC guidance checked on 16 July 2026.

This educational summary covers notification, payment, amendments, repayments, records and common penalty rules. The purchaser remains responsible for an accurate return even where a solicitor or tax adviser submits it.

1. Initial Filing and Payment Compliance

A purchaser must deliver a return for a notifiable land transaction and pay any SDLT due within 14 days after the effective date. The 14-day period begins on the day after the effective date.

  • Legislation: Section 76 (Duty to deliver return) & Section 86 (Payment of tax) of the Finance Act (FA) 2003.
  • Statutory window: 14 days beginning with the day after the effective date.
  • Prescribed Form: Form SDLT1 (Land Transaction Return), primarily delivered via HMRC's online portal or third-party web services.
  • Current guidance: Check if you need to send an SDLT return.

The "effective date" typically corresponds to the date of contractual completion. However, practitioners must note exceptions where a contract is "substantially performed" prior to formal completion under s.44 FA 2003 (e.g., if the purchaser takes possession or pays a substantial amount of consideration).

Notifiable is not the same as taxable: a return may be required even where no SDLT is payable, including where a relief must be claimed. Some transactions do not require a return, including certain transfers with no chargeable consideration, freehold acquisitions below £40,000 and specified lease transactions. Check the statutory notification rules for the particular transaction.

2. Amendments and Overpayment Relief

A purchaser may amend a return within the statutory amendment period. Separate repayment or claim routes may apply where later events change the liability.

Standard Amendments

  • Legislation: FA2003, Sch.10, Para 6.
  • Time limit: 12 months from the filing date. The statutory filing date is normally 14 days after the effective date, even if the return was submitted earlier or later.
  • Method: HMRC may correct specified minor, non-refund errors by telephone. Small or substantial amendments must be made in writing with the information required by HMRC. SDLT2 is a supplementary return page, not an amendment form.
  • Current guidance: Tell HMRC about changes to your SDLT return.

Overpayment relief and special repayment routes

After the amendment period, a purchaser may be able to claim overpayment relief under Schedule 11A to FA 2003, normally within four years after the effective date. This is not a general extension of the amendment period. The claim must meet formal requirements and is subject to exclusions, including restrictions involving missed claims or elections, prevailing practice and unjust enrichment.

For the higher rates on additional dwellings, the supplement has generally been 5 percentage points since 31 October 2024, subject to transitional rules. An individual who paid the higher rates because they retained a previous main residence may qualify for a repayment if they dispose of that residence within the applicable replacement period. The normal period is three years, but statutory extensions can apply in limited circumstances.

For a previous main residence disposed of on or after 29 October 2018, the repayment claim must normally be made within 12 months after whichever is later: the disposal date or the filing date for the new-residence return. Earlier disposals had a different three-month limb. Use the current higher-rates guidance and repayment service.

3. Penalties, Interest, and Enforcement

Late filing, late payment and inaccurate returns have separate consequences. A penalty may be appealable where the statutory conditions are met, including where there is a reasonable excuse for late filing.

Late Filing Penalties

A late return can attract fixed penalties under Schedule 14 to FA 2003:

  • Up to 3 months late: £100.
  • More than 3 months late: £200.
  • More than 12 months late: the fixed penalty plus a tax-based penalty of up to the full amount of tax due on the return.

See current HMRC penalty and interest guidance.

Late Payment Interest

Interest on unpaid SDLT runs automatically under Section 87 FA 2003 from the day after payment was due until HMRC receives payment. The current statutory formula is:

Late-payment interest rate = Bank Rate + 4 percentage points

Late-payment interest is not a penalty and cannot be appealed as a penalty. A purchaser can nevertheless ask HMRC to review the calculation or whether interest is due. The live rate should always be checked because Bank Rate changes.

Inaccuracy Penalties

If a return contains structural errors (such as misrepresenting the consideration or invalidly claiming First-Time Buyers' Relief), penalties are assessed under Schedule 24 to the Finance Act 2007. A penalty is calculated by reference to potential lost revenue (PLR), the behaviour that caused the inaccuracy and the nature and quality of any disclosure:

Type of behaviour Unprompted disclosure Prompted disclosure Maximum
Careless Inaccuracy 0% to 30% 15% to 30% 30% of PLR
Deliberate but Not Concealed 20% to 70% 35% to 70% 70% of PLR
Deliberate and Concealed 30% to 100% 50% to 100% 100% of PLR

Reductions within these ranges depend on whether disclosure is prompted and on its quality: telling HMRC, helping HMRC quantify the inaccuracy and giving access to records. A careless inaccuracy carries no penalty where reasonable care was taken.

Records and HMRC assessments

Keep the records needed to support each return for at least six years from the effective date, including contracts, conveyances, plans, payment evidence and financing documents. Inadequate records can attract a penalty.

HMRC’s normal time limit for an SDLT assessment is four years after the effective date. It is extended to six years where tax was lost through careless behaviour and 20 years where the loss was brought about deliberately. These tax-assessment limits are distinct from the rules for assessing Schedule 24 penalties.
