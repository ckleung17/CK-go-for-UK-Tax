Replacement of main residence

The replacement of main residence (FA 2003 Sch 4ZA para 3) is an important exception to the higher rates for additional dwellings. If an individual buys a new dwelling to replace his only or main residence, the higher rates may not apply, even if the individual owns or has owned another dwelling.

The rule is mainly relevant where the purchaser owns more than one dwelling at the end of the effective date of the new purchase. If the new dwelling is a replacement of the purchaser’s only or main residence, the purchase is not treated as a higher rates transaction merely because the purchaser owns another dwelling.

Old main residence sold before or on the same day

If the old main residence is sold before or on the same day as the new main residence is bought, the higher rates should not apply if the replacement conditions are met. Broadly, the purchaser must intend to live in the new property as their only or main residence; the purchaser, or their spouse or civil partner, must have disposed of a major interest in the old dwelling within the previous three years; neither of them must retain a major interest in the old dwelling after the disposal; the purchaser must have lived in the old dwelling as their only or main residence at some point in the three years before buying the new property; and the purchaser must not have acquired another main residence in between.

Old main residence sold after the new purchase

If the purchaser buys the new main residence before selling the old main residence, the higher rates usually have to be paid first. However, the purchaser may claim a refund if the old main residence is sold within three years of buying the new property, provided the replacement conditions are met.

The refund rule applies where the purchaser intended to live in the new property as their only or main residence at the time of purchase, later disposes of a major interest in the old dwelling within three years, does not retain a major interest in the old dwelling after the disposal, and had lived in the old dwelling as their only or main residence at some point in the three years before buying the new property.

A refund claim must normally be made within the statutory time limit. For disposals of the previous main home on or after 29 October 2018, HMRC must receive the refund request within 12 months of the sale of the previous main home, or within 12 months of the SDLT return filing date for the new main home, whichever is later.

Meaning of only or main residence

Whether a property is a person’s only or main residence is based on the facts. A person cannot simply nominate a property as their main residence for SDLT purposes. Relevant factors may include:

  • where the family lives;
  • where children go to school;
  • where the purchaser works;
  • which address is used for correspondence;
  • where the purchaser is registered with a doctor or dentist; and
  • where the purchaser is registered to vote.

Note: The replacement rule is about replacing an owned main residence. Moving out of rented accommodation, employer-provided accommodation, or a parental home does not normally count as disposing of a previous main residence for this rule.

Examples

Example 1: A sells their old home and buys a new home on the same day. A intends to live in the new home as their only or main residence. The higher rates should not apply, assuming the other replacement conditions are met.

Example 2: B buys a new main residence before selling the old main residence. On the effective date of the new purchase, which is usually the completion date, B still owns both homes. Therefore, B must file the SDLT return and pay SDLT at the higher rates, normally within 14 days of the effective date.

If B later sells the old main residence within 3 years of buying the new main residence, and the replacement conditions are met, B can claim a refund of the higher-rates element. For a sale of the old main residence on or after 29 October 2018, HMRC must receive the refund claim within 12 months of whichever is later: the sale date of the old main residence, or the filing date of the SDLT return for the new main residence.

Simple timeline example

1 June 2026: B completes the purchase of the new main residence. This is usually the effective date for SDLT purposes. At this date, B still owns the old main residence, so the higher rates for additional dwellings apply.

15 June 2026: B must normally file the SDLT return and pay the SDLT within 14 days of the effective date. In this example, the deadline is 15 June 2026.

By 1 June 2029: B must normally sell the old main residence within 3 years of buying the new main residence in order to qualify for a refund of the higher-rates element.

Example sale on 1 March 2028: If B sells the old main residence on 1 March 2028, B may claim a refund of the higher-rates element, provided the replacement conditions are met.

Refund claim deadline: HMRC must normally receive the refund claim within 12 months of whichever is later:

  • the sale date of the old main residence; or
  • the SDLT return filing date for the new main residence.

Example 3: C owns a buy-to-let flat and also owns an old main residence. C sells the old main residence before buying a new main residence. Although C still owns the buy-to-let flat at the end of the purchase day, the higher rates may not apply because C has disposed of an owned previous main residence and is acquiring the new dwelling as C’s new only or main residence, provided all the replacement conditions are met.

Note: Owning a buy-to-let property does not automatically prevent the replacement of main residence exception. The key point is that the purchaser must have disposed of a previous main residence that they owned and occupied as their main residence.

Example 4: D owns a buy-to-let flat but has been living in rented accommodation. D then buys a new house to live in as D’s main residence. The replacement of main residence exception does not apply because D has not disposed of an owned previous main residence. Moving out of rented accommodation does not count as disposing of a previous main residence. Therefore, because D will own both the buy-to-let flat and the new home at the end of the purchase day, the higher rates for additional dwellings may apply.

Note: First-time buyer relief is also not available to D because D has already acquired a major interest in a dwelling, namely the buy-to-let flat. First-time buyer relief is only available where the purchaser has not previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling anywhere in the world and intends to occupy the purchased property as their only or main residence.
