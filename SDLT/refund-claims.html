SDLT refund claims

An SDLT refund claim may arise where SDLT has been paid but the purchaser later becomes entitled to recover some or all of it. Common examples include a refund of the higher rates for additional dwellings after the sale of a previous main residence, repayment of the non-resident surcharge after meeting the UK residence test, or correction of an overpaid SDLT return.

One common SDLT refund situation is where the purchaser paid the higher rates for additional dwellings because they bought a new home before selling their old home. If the old home was the purchaser’s previous only or main residence and it is sold within the required time limit, the purchaser may be able to claim a refund of the higher-rates element. This is explained in more detail in the separate page on replacement of main residence.

Note: A refund is not the same as an exemption or relief claimed at the time of filing. A refund usually means SDLT was paid first and is later reclaimed because the conditions for repayment are satisfied.

Common types of SDLT refund claims

  • refund of the higher rates for additional dwellings;
  • replacement of main residence refund;
  • repayment of the 2% non-resident surcharge;
  • amendment of an SDLT return within the amendment period;
  • overpayment relief claim where the normal amendment period has passed; and
  • correction of errors, such as incorrect property type, consideration, or relief code.

Higher rates refund: replacement of main residence

A purchaser may claim a refund of the higher rates for additional dwellings where they bought a new main residence before selling their previous main residence, paid the higher rates at completion, and later sold the previous main residence within the required time limit (FA 2003 Sch 4ZA).

The previous main residence must normally be sold within 3 years of buying the new main residence. If the previous main residence is sold on or after 29 October 2018, HMRC must normally receive the refund request within 12 months of whichever is later:

  • the sale date of the previous main residence; or
  • the filing date of the SDLT return for the new main residence.

Note: Liability is tested at the end of the effective date. If the old main residence is sold after that date, even before the SDLT return filing deadline, the higher rates must normally be reported and paid first. A qualifying later disposal is dealt with through a repayment claim.

Note: For a new home bought on or after 1 January 2017, HMRC may allow a disposal outside the normal three-year period where exceptional circumstances prevented the sale and the old home is sold without further delay after those circumstances end. Ordinary sale difficulties, such as failure to find a buyer at the desired price, delays agreeing terms or a broken transaction chain, do not normally qualify.

Example 1: higher rates refund timeline

1 June 2026: B completes the purchase of a new main residence. B still owns the old main residence, so B pays SDLT at the higher rates for additional dwellings.

15 June 2026: B must normally file the SDLT return and pay the SDLT within 14 days of the effective date.

By 1 June 2029: B must normally sell the old main residence within 3 years of buying the new main residence, unless qualifying exceptional circumstances extend the period.

1 March 2028: B sells the old main residence. B may now claim a refund of the higher-rates element, provided the replacement conditions are met.

Refund deadline: HMRC must normally receive the refund claim within 12 months of whichever is later: the sale date of the old main residence, or the SDLT return filing date for the new main residence.

Non-resident surcharge repayment

A purchaser may be able to claim a repayment of the 2% non-resident surcharge if all the purchasers are individuals and they later satisfy the SDLT residence test (FA 2003 Sch 9A).

If there are joint purchasers and any one purchaser is non-UK resident, the whole transaction is treated as a non-resident transaction, so the 2% surcharge applies to the whole purchase price, not just that person’s share. HMRC says the surcharge applies where one or more buyers is non-UK resident.

An exception may apply where spouses or civil partners buy jointly. If they are not separated, neither is acting as trustee of a settlement, and one is UK resident for SDLT purposes, both are treated as UK resident for the non-resident surcharge.

If a non-UK resident spouse or civil partner buys alone, the joint-purchase deeming rule cannot apply. The purchaser may nevertheless qualify for repayment by personally reaching 183 UK days within a qualifying continuous 365-day period. Each day counts only if the purchaser is in the UK at the end of that day.

Broadly, the individual must spend at least 183 days in the UK during any continuous 365-day period which starts no more than 364 days before the effective date and ends no more than 365 days after the effective date. The repayment claim must normally be made within 2 years of the effective date.

Family ties do not normally make a purchaser UK resident. The individual purchaser must satisfy the SDLT 183-day UK presence test. The Statutory Residence Test used for income tax and capital gains tax does not apply. A limited spouse or civil partner rule may apply where spouses or civil partners buy jointly, are living together, and one of them is UK resident for SDLT purposes.

Example 2: non-resident surcharge refund

1 August 2026: E completes the purchase of a residential property in England. At the filing date, E has not yet spent 183 days in the UK within the relevant period, so the 2% non-resident surcharge is paid.

Later: E moves to the UK and reaches 183 UK days within a qualifying continuous 365-day period.

Refund claim: E may claim repayment of the 2% surcharge, provided the claim is made within the required 2-year time limit from the effective date.

Overpayment and amended return claims

A refund may also arise because the SDLT return was filed incorrectly. For example, the purchaser may have used the wrong rates, overstated the chargeable consideration, failed to claim an available relief, or treated a mixed-use transaction as fully residential.

If the purchaser is within the normal amendment period, the return may usually be amended within one year of the filing date. After that period, an overpayment relief claim may sometimes be made within 4 years of the effective date, but this is a separate statutory remedy with important exclusions.

Note: Overpayment relief does not generally revive an expired claim or election, including a relief that the purchaser failed to claim in time. It may also be excluded where another formal correction route was available but was not used within its time limit. A claim must explain the mistake, identify the incorrect return entries, provide revised figures and include supporting evidence.

Information usually needed for a refund claim

  • Unique Transaction Reference Number, or UTRN;
  • full names of all purchasers;
  • property address;
  • effective date of the transaction;
  • amount of SDLT originally paid;
  • amount of refund claimed;
  • reason for the refund claim;
  • supporting evidence, such as completion statements, sale contract, TR1, lease, or SDLT calculation; and
  • bank account details for repayment.

Note: If there is more than one purchaser, all purchasers should be included in the claim. If an agent or solicitor claims on behalf of the purchaser, HMRC may require signed authority from the purchasers.

HMRC may check the claim later

HMRC may make a repayment before checking the full technical position. This does not necessarily mean HMRC has agreed that the refund is correct. If the claim is later found to be wrong, the purchaser may have to repay the refund, together with interest and possible penalties.

Memory point: Refund received does not always mean claim accepted. The purchaser remains responsible for making sure the refund claim is correct.
