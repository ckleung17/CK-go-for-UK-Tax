SDLT Residential Property Rates

Note: SDLT now mainly applies to land in England and Northern Ireland; Scotland has LBTT, and Wales has LTT.

1. Standard residential rates

SDLT on residential property is charged on increasing portions of the chargeable consideration. HMRC’s current residential rates page is here: HMRC SDLT residential property rates .

The statutory charging provision is FA 2003 s.55 .

Standard residential rates apply to the purchase of a residential property, such as a house or flat, where no special surcharge or relief changes the calculation. SDLT is charged on increasing portions of the purchase price or lease premium.

Higher rates for additional dwellings apply where the purchaser buys an additional residential property, such as a second home or buy-to-let property, and the replacement of main residence exception does not apply. The higher rate is added on top of the ordinary residential rates.

First-time buyer relief applies only where the purchaser, or every purchaser if there is more than one, is a first-time buyer and intends to occupy the dwelling as their only or main residence. It is therefore not available for a buy-to-let or investment purchase. A person is not a first-time buyer if they have previously acquired a major interest in a dwelling, or an equivalent interest in land, anywhere in the world. Selling the previous dwelling before the new purchase does not make the person a first-time buyer again. Therefore, a buyer who owns or has previously owned a flat overseas will usually not qualify for first-time buyer relief.

Note: First-time buyer relief was introduced from 22 November 2017. During the COVID temporary residential rates period from 8 July 2020 to 30 June 2021, the ordinary residential nil-rate band was temporarily increased to £500K, so separate first-time buyer relief was effectively replaced by the temporary residential rates during that period.

Maximum qualifying property value means the highest purchase price for which first-time buyer relief can apply. From 1 April 2025, the maximum qualifying property value is £500,000. If the purchase price is more than £500,000, first-time buyer relief is not available and the standard residential rates apply instead.

Non-resident surcharge applies to certain purchases of residential property in England or Northern Ireland by non-UK resident purchasers. It adds 2 percentage points to the SDLT residential rates that would otherwise apply (FA 2003 s.55C; FA 2003 Sch 9A).

For an individual purchaser, the residence test is a special SDLT test. The purchaser is treated as UK resident for this surcharge if they are present in the UK on at least 183 days during any continuous 365-day period falling within the relevant period. The relevant period begins 364 days before the effective date of the transaction and ends 365 days after the effective date (FA 2003 Sch 9A para 4). If the purchaser does not meet this test when the SDLT return is filed, the transaction may need to be reported on the basis that the purchaser is non-UK resident.

Because the 183-day test can look at time spent in the UK after the purchase, a purchaser who pays the 2% surcharge may later be able to claim a refund if they satisfy the 183-day requirement within the relevant post-completion period. The refund is claimed by amending the SDLT return, normally within two years beginning with the day after the effective date of the transaction.

The surcharge is cumulative. For example, if a non-UK resident purchaser buys a buy-to-let property, the SDLT calculation may include the standard residential rates, the higher rates for additional dwellings, and the additional 2% non-resident surcharge.

Note: The SDLT non-resident test is not the same as the general UK tax residence test for Income Tax or Capital Gains Tax. For this surcharge, days spent anywhere in the UK count, not only days spent in England or Northern Ireland.

Temporary residential rates refers to the temporary SDLT residential thresholds that applied from 23 September 2022 to 31 March 2025. During this period, the ordinary residential nil-rate threshold was increased from £125,000 to £250,000. The same temporary rules also increased the first-time buyer relief nil-rate threshold from £300,000 to £425,000, and increased the maximum qualifying property value for first-time buyer relief from £500,000 to £625,000.

Note: These temporary residential rates ended on 31 March 2025. From 1 April 2025, the residential nil-rate threshold returned to £125,000, and the maximum qualifying property value for first-time buyer relief returned to £500,000.
