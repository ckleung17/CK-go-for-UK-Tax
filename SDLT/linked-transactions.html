Linked transactions

Linked transactions (FA 2003 s.108) are transactions that form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser, or persons connected with them.

The rule prevents parties from artificially splitting one commercial deal into separate smaller transactions in order to obtain lower SDLT bands or avoid notification thresholds. If transactions are linked, the SDLT calculation may be affected because the consideration for the linked transactions is aggregated.

Note: Transactions are not linked merely because the same buyer and seller are involved. The question is whether the transactions form part of the same scheme, arrangement, series or bargain.

Connected persons

Linked transactions can involve the same vendor and purchaser, or persons connected with them. The connected persons test refers to CTA 2010 s.1122. Connected persons may include spouses or civil partners, relatives, business partners, companies controlled by the same person, and certain trustees or settlements.

Note: Using a spouse, relative, company or connected person to split the transaction will not necessarily prevent linkage if the transactions are part of the same overall deal.

Why linked transactions matter

Where transactions are linked, the total chargeable consideration is taken into account when working out the SDLT rate. This can increase the SDLT payable because SDLT rates are progressive. A transaction that would fall within a lower band on its own may be taxed at a higher rate when aggregated with linked transactions.

Linked transaction rules can also affect whether a transaction is notifiable. For example, a small freehold purchase below £40,000 may not need an SDLT return if considered alone, but the position may change if it is linked with other transactions.

How the calculation works

The general approach is:

  1. identify all transactions forming part of the same scheme, arrangement or series;
  2. aggregate the chargeable consideration for those linked transactions;
  3. work out the SDLT rate by reference to the total linked consideration; and
  4. apportion the resulting SDLT to each transaction as required.

Note: Rent is treated separately for lease transactions. Do not simply add rent to the purchase price or lease premium without applying the SDLT lease rules.

Example 1: house and garden bought separately

A buys a house from B for £350,000. At the same time, A also buys a separate piece of garden land from B for £50,000. The two transfers are part of one bargain.

The transactions are likely to be linked because they form part of the same arrangement between the same vendor and purchaser. The total consideration is £400,000, so SDLT should be calculated by reference to the linked total, not by treating the £50,000 garden purchase as an isolated low-value transaction.

Can the garden be bought later to avoid linkage?

Buying the garden land after completion of the house does not automatically prevent the linked transaction rules from applying. The question is whether the house purchase and the later garden purchase form part of a single scheme, arrangement or series of transactions between the same vendor and purchaser, or connected persons (FA 2003 s.108).

If the later garden purchase is genuinely separate, with no earlier agreement, option, side letter, price adjustment or understanding that the garden would also be bought, the transactions may not be linked. However, if the garden purchase was already part of the overall bargain when the house was bought, delaying completion of the garden transfer will not by itself prevent the transactions from being linked.

Note: A later purchase can still be linked with an earlier purchase. Timing is relevant evidence, but it is not decisive. The key question is whether there was one overall deal, arrangement or series.

Example 2: husband and wife split the purchase

C buys a house from D for £300,000. C’s wife buys adjoining land from D for £80,000 under a connected arrangement. The parties intend the house and land to be used together.

The use of a spouse does not automatically prevent linkage. Because spouses are connected persons and the transactions form part of the same arrangement, the linked transaction rules may apply. The combined consideration is £380,000.

Example 3: genuinely separate transactions

E buys a flat from F in January. Six months later, E separately buys an unrelated garage from F after a new negotiation. There was no earlier agreement, option, side letter or understanding that the garage would also be purchased.

The transactions are not automatically linked just because the same parties are involved. If the second purchase is genuinely separate and not part of the same scheme, arrangement or series, the linked transaction rules may not apply.

Example 4: staged purchase of plots

G agrees to buy several plots from H in stages. Each plot is transferred separately, but the staged transfers are all part of the same overall development agreement.

The transactions are likely to be linked because they form part of one scheme or series. The SDLT position should be considered by reference to the total consideration for the linked purchases.

Practical indicators of linked transactions

Factors suggesting linkage may include:

  • one commercial bargain or package deal;
  • the same or connected buyers and sellers;
  • contracts exchanged at the same time or close together;
  • one transaction being conditional on another;
  • side letters, options, pre-agreed future transfers or staged completions;
  • common financing or a single valuation covering all parts;
  • the properties being intended to be used together; and
  • splitting the price or title producing an SDLT saving.

Practical indicators against linkage

Factors suggesting the transactions may be separate include:

  • separate negotiations at different times;
  • no obligation or understanding that the later transaction would happen;
  • different commercial purposes;
  • different and unconnected parties;
  • no conditionality between the transactions;
  • separate funding, valuation and documentation; and
  • a genuine gap in time with no pre-existing arrangement.

Later linked transactions

A later transaction can still be linked with an earlier one if they form part of the same scheme, arrangement or series. If a later linked transaction occurs after the original SDLT return has been filed, the purchaser may need to review whether the earlier SDLT calculation should be amended or whether a further return is required.

Note: Always keep evidence showing why transactions were or were not linked. HMRC may ask for contracts, completion statements, correspondence, side agreements, plans, valuations, financing documents and evidence of negotiations.

Memory point

Linked transactions = same or connected parties + same scheme, arrangement or series. The key question is commercial reality, not just how many contracts or titles are used.
