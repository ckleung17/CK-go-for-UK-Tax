Mixed property

Mixed property means a land transaction involving both residential property and non-residential property. For example, a transaction may be mixed-use where a flat is bought together with a shop, office, doctor’s surgery, commercial unit, or other land that is not residential.

The distinction matters because mixed property is charged using the non-residential or mixed-use SDLT rates, not the ordinary residential rates. This can produce a lower SDLT liability than treating the whole transaction as residential, especially where the price is high or where the higher rates for additional dwellings would otherwise apply.

Note: A property is not mixed-use merely because it has a large garden, garage, driveway, outbuilding, paddock, field, or extra land. The key question is whether the non-residential element falls outside the dwelling and its garden or grounds after considering all the facts. Physical separation, fencing and independent access may be relevant, but none is decisive on its own.

Residential property

Residential property (FA 2003 s.116) includes a building that is used or suitable for use as a dwelling, or is being constructed or adapted for use as a dwelling. It also includes land that is, or forms part of, the garden or grounds of that dwelling.

Therefore, where a house is sold with its normal garden, driveway, garage, domestic outbuildings, and land forming part of its grounds, the transaction will usually be residential rather than mixed-use.

Non-residential property

Non-residential property is property that is not residential property. It may include commercial buildings, shops, offices, warehouses, agricultural land, woodland, bare land, or other land that does not form part of the dwelling or its garden or grounds.

A transaction is mixed-use where the subject matter includes both residential and non-residential property. The nature of the land is assessed at the time of the land transaction: normally the moment of completion, or the moment of substantial performance if that occurs earlier. A later change, even later on the same effective date, is ignored. The buyer’s future intention is not normally decisive.

Examples of mixed-use property

  • a shop with a flat above it;
  • a public house with residential accommodation upstairs;
  • a doctor’s surgery with a residential flat attached;
  • a farm purchase including a farmhouse and working farmland;
  • a house sold with land that is subject to a genuine commercial lease or business use; and
  • a property including a dwelling and a separate commercial unit.

Examples that may not be mixed-use

  • a house with a large garden;
  • a house with a garage, shed, stable or domestic outbuilding used with the home;
  • a house with a paddock used for the owner’s private horses;
  • a house with land that forms part of the enjoyment, privacy, amenity or setting of the dwelling; and
  • a property where the alleged non-residential use is artificial or not supported by evidence.

Note: The boundary between residential grounds and genuine non-residential land is fact-sensitive. Evidence is important. HMRC may look at legal title, physical layout, fencing, access, use at completion, leases or licences, business records, planning use, and whether the land was enjoyed as part of the home.

Why mixed-use treatment matters

If a transaction is correctly classified as mixed-use, the whole transaction is normally charged using the non-residential or mixed-use rates. This means the residential higher rates for additional dwellings and first-time buyer relief rules are not applied in the usual way, because the transaction is not treated as wholly residential.

However, mixed-use treatment should not be claimed simply because it gives a lower SDLT result. The classification must be supported by the facts at the time of the land transaction.

Non-residential and mixed-use SDLT rates

For freehold or lease premium transactions in England and Northern Ireland, the following non-residential and mixed-use SDLT rates have applied since 17 March 2016: HMRC non-residential and mixed-property rates .

Chargeable consideration SDLT rate
Up to £150,000 0%
Over £150,000 and up to £250,000 2%
Over £250,000 5%

Note: Leasehold transactions may involve separate calculations for any lease premium and the net present value of rent. Leasehold SDLT will be covered separately under the non-residential property topic.

Practical checklist

When considering whether a property is mixed-use, check:

  • what exactly is being acquired under the contract and transfer;
  • whether the residential element is a dwelling or suitable for use as a dwelling;
  • whether the extra land forms part of the garden or grounds of the dwelling;
  • whether any part is genuinely used for commercial, agricultural or non-residential purposes;
  • whether there are leases, licences, grazing agreements, business rates, planning records or accounts;
  • whether the alleged non-residential part has separate access or physical separation;
  • whether the non-residential use existed at the time of the land transaction; and
  • whether the SDLT position can be supported if HMRC opens an enquiry.

Memory point: Mixed-use property means genuinely residential plus genuinely non-residential. Large grounds alone do not automatically make a property mixed-use.

Subsequent change of use

A later change of use does not normally require a new SDLT return merely because the property changes from residential to non-residential use, or vice versa. Classification is tested at the time of the land transaction: normally completion, or substantial performance if earlier (FA 2003 s.44; FA 2003 s.55; FA 2003 s.116).

For example, if a house is residential at completion but is later converted into offices, the later conversion does not by itself change the SDLT treatment of the original purchase. Similarly, if a shop is non-residential at completion but is later converted into a flat, the later conversion does not by itself create an SDLT adjustment for the original purchase.

However, a further SDLT return or amendment may be required if the later event affects the original SDLT calculation or creates a new land transaction. Examples include contingent or uncertain consideration becoming known, a later linked transaction, withdrawal of a relief, or a new transaction such as a transfer, grant, assignment or surrender of a lease.

Linked transactions

Where mixed property is acquired through a new or replacement lease, also apply the leasehold transaction rules.

Where linked transactions collectively include residential and non-residential land, the non-residential rates generally apply to each linked transaction. The consideration is not apportioned between the residential and non-residential rate tables. See the next topic on linked transactions.

Note: Future intention is not enough. If the property is residential at the time of the land transaction, an intention to convert it to non-residential use later will not normally make the purchase mixed-use or non-residential for SDLT. The classification must be supported by the facts at the time of the land transaction.
